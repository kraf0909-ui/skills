# Patterns & Frameworks — Antifragile

## 1. The Antifragile Triad

```
When analyzing any system, ask:
- Is it Fragile? (harmed by stress)
- Is it Robust? (resists stress)
- Is it Antifragile? (benefits from stress)

Apply to: organizations, technologies, careers, diets, political systems
```

## 2. The Barbell Strategy

**When to use**: When you need both safety and growth; when the middle is dangerous.

**How**:
- Place 80-90% in ultra-safe, conservative positions
- Place 10-20% in high-risk, high-reward asymmetric bets
- Avoid the mediocre middle (moderate risk with moderate reward)

**Examples**:
- Seneca: most wealth in land, small portion in speculative trade
- Career: secure day job + entrepreneurial side project
- Investment: Treasury bills + venture capital
- Education: rigid technical skills + broad liberal arts

**Trade-offs**: Requires discipline to maintain the extremes; social pressure pushes toward the middle.

## 3. Optionality as Strategy

**When to use**: When uncertainty is high and prediction is impossible.

**How**:
- Seek situations with limited downside, unlimited upside
- Keep more options than obligations
- Prefer "long gamma" positions that benefit from volatility
- Let randomness work for you, not against you

**Key insight**: "Life is long gamma" — position yourself to benefit from disorder.

## 4. Via Negativa (Subtractive Knowledge)

**When to use**: When complexity overwhelms; when adding solutions creates new problems.

**How**:
- Remove fragility rather than add robustness
- Stop harmful interventions before starting helpful ones
- Eliminate the bad rather than seek the good
- Use "not to do" lists alongside "to do" lists

**Examples**:
- Medicine: stop harmful treatments before adding new ones
- Diet: remove processed foods before adding supplements
- Organizations: fire toxic people before hiring stars
- Decision-making: avoid stupidity rather than pursue brilliance

## 5. Skin in the Game Filter

**When to use**: Evaluating advice, predictions, or policies.

**How**:
- Ask: Does the person recommending this bear the cost if wrong?
- Favor artisans over bureaucrats
- Distrust "experts" who don't participate in the outcomes
- Look for alignment between advice-giver and advice-taker

**Red flags (no skin in the game)**:
- Bankers who keep bonuses when bets fail
- Pundits who never retract wrong predictions
- Politicians with golden parachutes
- Academics who don't trade on their theories

## 6. The Turkey Test

**When to use**: Evaluating the stability and predictability of any system.

**How**:
- Has this system experienced genuine stress recently?
- Is the apparent stability backed by fundamentals or just lack of shocks?
- What would happen if a Black Swan arrived?
- Is confidence increasing precisely because risk is accumulating?

**Warning signs**:
- Long period of stability
- Increasing confidence in forecasts
- Dismissal of tail risks
- "This time is different" thinking

## 7. Touristification Detector

**When to use**: Designing systems, education, or lifestyles.

**How**:
- Has randomness been systematically removed?
- Are all surprises planned and controlled?
- Is there any genuine exposure to variability?
- Would a small shock cause disproportionate damage?

**Antidotes**:
- Introduce controlled randomness
- Allow small failures
- Avoid over-optimization
- Preserve optionality and slack

## 8. Convexity Scanner

**When to use**: Evaluating exposures, investments, or policies.

**How**:
- Map the payoff function: is it convex (smiles) or concave (frowns)?
- Convex = benefits from volatility; concave = harmed by volatility
- Prefer convex exposures; avoid or hedge concave ones
- Use Jensen's Inequality: average of function > function of average for convex

**Applications**:
- Investment portfolios: seek convexity
- Medical interventions: watch for concave iatrogenics
- Political systems: decentralization creates convexity
- Personal health: hormesis is convexity in biology

## 9. The Lindy Filter

**When to use**: Choosing technologies, ideas, books, or practices.

**How**:
- Favor things that have survived a long time
- Be skeptical of the new (neomania)
- Old > New for non-perishable things
- The longer something lasts, the longer it's likely to last

**Exceptions**: When genuine technological disruption occurs (but most "disruption" is hype).

## 10. Sucker vs. Nonsucker Detection

**When to use**: Making decisions under uncertainty.

**How**:
- Suckers try to predict; nonsuckers position for multiple outcomes
- Suckers focus on probability; nonsuckers focus on payoff asymmetry
- Suckers are fooled by randomness; nonsuckers respect it
- Suckers seek complexity; nonsuckers seek optionality

**Fat Tony's rule**: "It's not about knowing what will happen; it's about being positioned for whatever happens."

## 11. Naive Intervention Check

**When to use**: Before taking action in complex systems.

**How**:
- First, do no harm (Primum non nocere)
- Consider iatrogenics: will intervention cause more harm than good?
- Is the problem self-correcting if left alone?
- Are you confusing catalyst with cause?
- Would procrastination (Fabian style) be better?

**Intervention hierarchy**:
1. Do nothing (often best)
2. Remove harmful intervention
3. Small, reversible intervention
4. Large, irreversible intervention (avoid)

## 12. Asymmetry Harvesting

**When to use**: Building systems, making bets, designing careers.

**How**:
- Seek positive asymmetry: small cost, large potential benefit
- Avoid negative asymmetry: large cost, small potential benefit
- Thales' option: pay little for the right to benefit greatly
- Structure life and work to capture upside while limiting downside

**Examples**:
- Writing a book: fixed cost, potentially unlimited upside
- Venture capital: many small losses, few huge wins
- Reputation: takes years to build, moments to destroy (negative asymmetry—protect it)
