# Chapter 7: Naive Intervention

## Core Idea

Intervention in complex systems often causes more harm than good—a phenomenon Taleb calls **Iatrogenics** (borrowed from medicine, where it means harm caused by the healer). The chapter argues that modernity is characterized by a compulsion to intervene, fix, optimize, and control, even when the evidence shows that doing nothing would be superior. Naive interventionists fail to understand that complex organic systems have their own healing and adaptive mechanisms, and that suppressing these mechanisms through ill-conceived intervention produces fragility, not resilience.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Iatrogenics**: Harm caused by the intervention itself. The term comes from medicine but applies broadly to any domain where attempted solutions worsen the problem.
- **First, Do No Harm (Primum Non Nocere)**: The classical medical principle that is routinely violated by modern interventionism. Taleb elevates it to a general epistemic principle.
- **The Opposite of Iatrogenics**: Systems that are allowed to heal themselves, where non-intervention produces better outcomes than intervention.
- **Non-Naive Interventionism**: Intervention that is aware of its own limits, that waits for evidence of benefit, and that respects the system's own adaptive capacity.
- **In Praise of Procrastination (The Fabian Kind)**: Strategic delay as a form of wisdom. Waiting allows the system to resolve problems on its own and reveals whether intervention is truly necessary.

## Key Concepts

- **Intervention and Iatrogenics**: Every intervention carries the risk of iatrogenic harm. The more complex the system, the greater this risk.
- **First, Do No Harm**: The default position should be non-intervention unless there is strong, clear evidence that intervention will help and not harm.
- **Iatrogenics in High Places**: Iatrogenics is not limited to medicine. It operates in economics (bailouts create moral hazard), politics (foreign intervention destabilizes), education (over-teaching kills curiosity), and parenting (overprotection produces fragility).
- **Can a Whale Fly Like an Eagle?**: A critique of the assumption that expertise in one domain transfers to another. Interveners often lack the specific knowledge needed for the systems they try to control.
- **Not Doing Nothing**: The distinction between passive non-intervention and active observation. "Not doing nothing" means monitoring and preparing without premature action.
- **Neuroticism in Industrial Proportions**: Modern institutions (media, state, corporations) generate and feed on neurotic overreaction to small problems, producing a culture of constant, harmful intervention.
- **A Legal Way to Kill People**: The observation that iatrogenic harm is often legally and socially protected, while natural harm is stigmatized.
- **Media-Driven Neuroticism**: The media amplifies rare risks and suppresses common ones, driving public demand for unnecessary and harmful interventions.
- **The State Can Help—When Incompetent**: A paradoxical observation: states that are too competent at intervention cause more iatrogenic harm than states that are too incompetent to intervene effectively.
- **France Is Messier than You Think**: A counterexample to the myth of efficient top-down state intervention. Even France, the model of centralized governance, is messier and more bottom-up than it appears.
- **Sweden and the Large State**: The Swedish model works not because of large-state competence but because of cultural factors that limit the harm of intervention.
- **Catalyst-as-Cause Confusion**: Mistaking the trigger of an event for its cause. Interventions often address catalysts while ignoring underlying causes, producing only temporary and misleading relief.

## Mental Models

- **Iatrogenics Checklist**: Before intervening, ask: (1) What is the evidence that this will help? (2) What harm could this cause? (3) Can the system heal itself if left alone? (4) Am I confusing a catalyst with a cause?
- **The Procrastination Principle**: When in doubt, wait. Time reveals whether a problem is self-resolving, and delay prevents premature, harmful action.
- **Intervention Asymmetry**: The harm from unnecessary intervention is often greater than the harm from necessary non-intervention. The downside of acting wrongly exceeds the downside of waiting.

## Anti-patterns

- **Naive Intervention**: Acting on the assumption that doing something is always better than doing nothing. This is the defining error of modernity.
- **Intervention Without Skin in the Game**: Interveners who do not bear the costs of their own mistakes produce iatrogenic harm with impunity.
- **Catalyst-as-Cause Confusion**: Treating symptoms or triggers as root causes, producing interventions that address surface phenomena while worsening underlying conditions.
- **Media-Driven Panic**: Allowing media amplification of rare risks to drive policy and personal decisions toward harmful overreaction.
- **The Soviet-Harvard Delusion**: The belief that complex systems can be understood, predicted, and controlled from the top down.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Iatrogenics**—harm caused by intervention—is pervasive and underestimated. Every proposed solution should be evaluated for its potential to worsen the problem.
2. The default stance toward complex systems should be **non-intervention** unless there is strong evidence that intervention will produce net benefit.
3. **Procrastination is a virtue**. Strategic delay allows systems to self-correct and reveals whether intervention is truly needed.
4. Modern institutions generate **neuroticism in industrial proportions**, driving a culture of harmful overreaction and unnecessary intervention.
5. The media systematically distorts risk perception, creating demand for iatrogenic interventions.
6. Interveners should have **skin in the game**. Those who do not bear the costs of their interventions will over-intervene.
7. Do not confuse catalysts with causes. Addressing the trigger of a crisis while ignoring its root cause produces only temporary relief and long-term fragility.
