# Chapter 6: Tell Them I Love (Some) Randomness

## Core Idea

Not all randomness is harmful; **some randomness is essential for systemic health**. The chapter argues that small, frequent shocks—what Taleb calls **political annealing**—can prevent large, catastrophic ones. Just as metallurgical annealing uses controlled heating and cooling to strengthen metal, political and social systems need controlled volatility to remain adaptive. The chapter controversially explores whether small wars save lives by preventing large ones, and critiques modernity's pursuit of total stability as a time bomb of accumulated fragility.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Political Annealing**: The principle that small, manageable shocks strengthen political and social systems, just as thermal cycles strengthen metal. This is a direct application of antifragility to governance.
- **The Time Bomb Called Stability**: The counterintuitive idea that long periods of stability are not necessarily good—they can indicate that volatility is being suppressed and accumulating for a catastrophic release.
- **The Hungry Donkeys Model**: A metaphor for systems that need randomness to resolve conflicts or make decisions. Two equally hungry donkeys equidistant from food need a random nudge to break the symmetry.

## Key Concepts

- **Political Annealing**: Small political shocks (elections, protests, policy debates, even minor conflicts) serve as stressors that keep the system adaptive. Their absence leads to brittleness.
- **The Time Bomb Called Stability**: Prolonged stability can be a warning sign. When a system has not experienced stress for a long time, it may have lost its capacity to respond to stress, or stress may be building in hidden form.
- **Do (Small) Wars Save Lives?**: A provocative exploration of whether the absence of small wars leads to large, catastrophic wars. The argument is not pro-war but pro-volatility: managed, small-scale conflict may prevent total war.
- **What to Tell Foreign Policy Makers**: Policy should not aim to eliminate all conflict but to ensure that conflicts are small, frequent, and manageable—allowing the system to learn rather than accumulate hidden grievances.
- **What Do We Call Here Modernity?**: Modernity is characterized by the belief that all randomness can and should be eliminated. This belief is itself a source of fragility.
- **Hungry Donkeys**: Randomness can be a constructive force that breaks deadlocks, resolves indeterminacies, and prevents stagnation.

## Mental Models

- **Annealing Lens**: When evaluating a system's health, ask: "When was the last time this system was stress-tested?" Long periods without stress are not necessarily good news.
- **Volatility as Safety**: Reframe small shocks as investments in systemic resilience. The cost of a small shock today may be the avoidance of a catastrophic shock tomorrow.
- **Stability Suspicion**: Treat prolonged stability as a diagnostic signal requiring investigation, not as proof of health.

## Anti-patterns

- **Total Stability Pursuit**: Attempting to eliminate all volatility, conflict, and randomness from a system. This creates the conditions for catastrophic failure.
- **Confusing Peace with Suppression**: A society without visible conflict may be one where conflict is suppressed rather than resolved. Suppressed conflict accumulates.
- **Eliminating Small Wars**: If small wars serve an annealing function, their elimination may increase the probability of total war.
- **Modernity's Anti-Randomness Bias**: The assumption that all randomness is bad and all order is good. This bias blinds us to the constructive role of volatility.

## Key Takeaways

1. Some randomness is beneficial. Small, frequent shocks keep systems adaptive and prevent catastrophic accumulation of hidden risk.
2. **Political Annealing**: Political systems, like metals, need thermal cycles—periodic stress—to maintain strength and flexibility.
3. Long periods of stability can be dangerous. They may indicate suppressed volatility that will eventually be released catastrophically.
4. The question is not whether conflict or randomness will occur, but at what scale. Small, manageable shocks are preferable to large, unmanageable ones.
5. Modernity's attempt to eliminate all randomness is itself a source of fragility. Randomness is not always noise; sometimes it is signal.
6. Foreign policy should aim for managed volatility, not total peace. A world with no small wars may be a world preparing for a large one.
