# Chapter 15: History Written by the Losers

## Core Idea

Contrary to the saying that history is written by the winners, Taleb argues that in the domain of technology, medicine, and innovation, history is written by the **losers** — the tinkerers, the trial-and-errorers, the people who failed repeatedly until something worked. The Industrial Revolution was not planned by brilliant inventors with clear visions; it emerged from relentless, nonteleological tinkering. The chapter is a full-throated defense of **tinkering** over **planning**, of **stochastic search** over **directed research**.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Nonteleological Tinkering**: Progress through purposeless, undirected trial and error rather than goal-directed research and planning.
- **Matt Ridley's Anti-Teleological Argument**: Evolution and innovation do not have goals. They proceed through random variation and selection, not through foresight.
- **The Inverse Turkey Problem**: Just as the turkey mistakes the farmer's feeding for benevolence until Thanksgiving, planners mistake the appearance of control for actual control — until the plan collapses.
- **To Fail Seven Times, Plus or Minus Two**: Innovation requires a tolerance for repeated failure. The exact number is less important than the stochastic process of trying many things.

## Key Concepts

- **The Evidence Staring at Us**: The evidence of history overwhelmingly supports tinkering over planning, yet we continue to believe in the myth of directed innovation.
- **Is It Like Cooking?**: Cooking is tinkering — you try, taste, adjust. It is not chemistry. Most practical knowledge is cooking-like, not formula-like.
- **The Industrial Revolution**: Not the product of great inventors with grand visions, but of thousands of tinkerers making small, incremental, often accidental improvements.
- **Governments Should Spend on Nonteleological Tinkering, Not Research**: Government funding should support broad, undirected experimentation rather than targeted research with predefined goals.
- **The Case in Medicine**: Most major medical breakthroughs (penicillin, X-rays, pacemakers) were accidents or side effects of tinkering, not the results of directed research programs.
- **Corporate Teleology**: Corporations that plan and set strategic goals often fail because they eliminate the optionality that comes from undirected experimentation.
- **The Charlatan, the Academic, and the Showman**: Three archetypes who claim credit for innovation they did not create — all benefit from the myth that progress comes from planning and genius.

## Mental Models

- **Tinkering as Stochastic Search**: Innovation is a search process over a vast space of possibilities. The most efficient search is random, local, and failure-tolerant.
- **The Cooking Model of Knowledge**: Practical knowledge is acquired like cooking — through iteration, feedback, and adjustment — not like chemistry, through formulas and first principles.
- **Teleology Detection**: When someone claims that X happened because of a plan or vision, be deeply skeptical. Ask: what is the evidence that tinkering and accident were not the real causes?

## Anti-patterns

- **Teleological Fallacy**: Believing that because something has a purpose or function, it was created with that purpose in mind. This confuses selection with design.
- **Directed Research Worship**: The belief that targeted research with clear goals outperforms broad, undirected experimentation.
- **The Charlatan's Narrative**: Allowing those who claim to have planned success to write history, while the actual tinkerers are forgotten.
- **Corporate Strategic Planning**: The illusion that setting five-year goals and strategic visions leads to innovation, when it typically eliminates the stochastic search that produces it.

## Key Takeaways

1. History is written by the losers — the tinkerers who failed repeatedly — not by the planners who claim to have directed progress.
2. The Industrial Revolution, modern medicine, and most technology emerged from nonteleological tinkering, not from directed research.
3. Government and corporate funding should support broad, undirected experimentation rather than targeted research with predefined outcomes.
4. The cooking model of knowledge — iterative, feedback-driven, failure-tolerant — is more accurate than the chemistry model for most practical domains.
5. The Inverse Turkey Problem warns us: the appearance of control and planning is often the most dangerous illusion, because it masks underlying fragility.
