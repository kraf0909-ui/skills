# Chapter 20: Time and Fragility

## Core Idea

Time is the ultimate stressor, and the Lindy Effect is its signature. This chapter argues that the only reliable judge of robustness and antifragility is time itself. The Lindy Effect states that for non-perishable things (ideas, technologies, books, religions), the longer they have survived, the longer they are expected to survive. Taleb contrasts this with "neomania"—the modern obsession with the new—and exposes the "treadmill effect" by which technological novelty creates the illusion of progress while often increasing fragility. The chapter is a defense of the old against the new, not out of nostalgia, but out of epistemic humility: time has filtered out what does not work.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Lindy Effect**: Named after a New York deli where actors gathered, the effect states that the future life expectancy of a non-perishable thing is proportional to its current age. A book that has been in print for 40 years will likely be in print for another 40; a new book has no such expectation.
- **Neomania**: The naive enthusiasm for the new simply because it is new. A form of presentism and chronological snobbery.
- **Treadmill Effect**: The phenomenon by which technological improvements are quickly absorbed as new baselines, creating no lasting gain in satisfaction or welfare.
- **Metrification**: The dangerous modern tendency to measure everything, which often destroys the very thing being measured (Goodhart's Law) and creates fragility through target-driven behavior.

## Key Concepts

- **From Simonides to Jensen**: The ancient poet Simonides' dictum that "the word is the image of the thing" contrasts with modern overreliance on metrics and representations.
- **Learning to Subtract**: Time teaches us what to remove, not just what to add. The oldest surviving ideas are those that have shed their inessential elements.
- **Technology at Its Best**: Technology that removes rather than adds—like the bicycle, which subtracts distance as a constraint—is antifragile. Technology that adds complexity often is not.
- **To Age in Reverse: The Lindy Effect**: The counterintuitive property that some things get *stronger* with age. Every year a technology survives without obsolescence is evidence that it will survive longer still.
- **Architecture and the Irreversible Neomania**: Modern architecture's obsession with novelty has produced buildings that are ugly, expensive to maintain, and hostile to human flourishing.
- **Wall to Wall Windows**: An example of neomanic architecture—glass boxes that are thermally inefficient, structurally fragile, and aesthetically impoverished.
- **Turning Science into Journalism**: The pressure to produce novel results turns scientific research into a news cycle, favoring flashy but fragile findings over robust, time-tested knowledge.
- **What Should Break**: Taleb argues that we should design systems so that the *right* things break—small, replaceable components rather than the whole system.
- **Prophets and the Present**: True prophets are not those who predict the future, but those who understand what is timeless.
- **Empedocles' Dog**: A reference to the ancient philosopher and the idea that some knowledge is buried and must be excavated from the past.

## Mental Models

- **Time as Filter**: Time is the only judge that does not lie. It eliminates the fragile and preserves the robust. Trust things that have survived.
- **The Treadmill**: New technology raises expectations as much as it raises capabilities, leaving net welfare unchanged.
- **Metrification as Fragility**: What gets measured gets gamed. Metrics create concave exposures to manipulation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Neomania**: The automatic assumption that newer is better. This is a form of chronological bias that ignores the Lindy filter.
- **Irreversible Neomania**: Building systems, architectures, and institutions that cannot be undone when they fail.
- **Turning Science into Journalism**: The institutional pressure for novelty in research destroys the cumulative, time-tested nature of true science.
- **The Tyranny of Metrics**: Measuring the wrong things, or measuring too much, creates fragility through goal displacement.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Time is the only reliable judge of robustness.** The Lindy Effect is the empirical signature of antifragility in non-perishable things.
2. **Neomania is a cognitive bias.** The new is not necessarily better; it is merely less tested.
3. **The treadmill effect means technology often creates no net gain.** What matters is not the absolute level of technology but the robustness of the systems it supports.
4. **Metrification creates fragility.** Be suspicious of any system that optimizes for measurable targets.
5. **Design for graceful failure.** The right parts of a system should break under stress, not the whole.
