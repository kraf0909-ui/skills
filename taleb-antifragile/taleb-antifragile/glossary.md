# Glossary — Antifragile

## A

**Antifragility** — The property of systems that gain from disorder, stress, volatility, and shocks. Beyond resilience or robustness; the antifragile gets better when exposed to randomness. (Ch 1)

**Antifragile Triad** — The three categories: Fragile (harmed by stress), Robust (resists stress), Antifragile (benefits from stress). (Ch 1)

## B

**Barbell Strategy** — A bifurcated approach combining extreme safety with extreme risk-taking, avoiding the mediocre middle. Named after Seneca's financial strategy. (Ch 10, 11)

**Black Swan** — A highly improbable, high-impact event that is retrospectively predictable but not prospectively so. From Taleb's earlier work. (Ch 8)

**Bottom-up** — Emergent order arising from decentralized trial and error, as opposed to top-down design. Taleb favors bottom-up systems for their antifragility. (Ch 5)

## C

**Convexity (Jensen's Inequality)** — The mathematical property where the average of a function exceeds the function of the average. Convex exposure benefits from volatility; concave exposure is harmed. (Ch 18, 19, 21)

## D

**Domain Independence** — The failure to transfer knowledge across domains. People understand antifragility in one area but not another. (Ch 1)

## E

**Epiphenomena** — Apparent causal relationships that are actually spurious correlations. The illusion that scholarship causes practice when practice often comes first. (Ch 13)

**Extremistan** — Environments where a single observation can disproportionately impact the total (e.g., wealth, book sales). Contrasts with Mediocristan. (Ch 5)

## F

**Fat Tony** — Taleb's fictional Brooklyn trader persona representing practical, street-smart wisdom over theoretical knowledge. Debates Socrates and Fragilistas. (Ch 9, 17)

**Fragilista** — Someone who causes fragility through naive intervention, prediction, or denial of randomness—typically an academic, bureaucrat, or policy maker without skin in the game. (Ch 9)

## G

**Great Turkey Problem** — The turkey fed for 1,000 days has increasing confidence in the farmer's kindness, right before Thanksgiving. A metaphor for false confidence from stable history. (Ch 5)

**Green Lumber Fallacy** — Mistaking the label/category of something for its essence. Named after a trader who profited from "green lumber" without knowing it was freshly cut (not painted green). (Ch 14)

## H

**Hormesis** — The biological phenomenon where low doses of stressors or toxins strengthen an organism. A mechanism of antifragility. (Ch 2)

**Hydra** — The mythological creature that grows two heads when one is cut off. Taleb's symbol of antifragility—gains from attack. (Ch 1)

## I

**Iatrogenics** — Harm caused by the healer/intervener. In medicine, unnecessary treatment that makes patients worse. Extended to any domain where intervention causes more harm than good. (Ch 7, 21)

**Inverse Turkey Problem** — The opposite of the Turkey Problem: things that look bad in the short run but are good in the long run. (Ch 15)

## L

**Lindy Effect** — The idea that the future life expectancy of non-perishable things (technologies, ideas, books) increases with every day they survive. Old things are likely to outlast new things. (Ch 20)

**Long Gamma** — Options trading terminology for benefiting from volatility. Taleb extends it as a life philosophy—position yourself to gain from disorder. (Ch 12)

## M

**Mediocristan** — Environments where no single observation can drastically impact the total (e.g., height, weight). Contrasts with Extremistan. (Ch 5)

## N

**Naive Intervention** — Action taken without understanding second-order effects, often causing iatrogenic harm. The illusion that doing something is always better than doing nothing. (Ch 7)

**Neomania** — The love of the new for its own sake. Taleb argues that older technologies and ideas are often more robust due to the Lindy Effect. (Ch 20)

**Nonpredictive** — Operating without relying on predictions. Taleb advocates building systems and making decisions that don't require accurate forecasting. (Ch 9)

## O

**Optionality** — The property of having more options than obligations. Positive asymmetry where upside is unlimited and downside is bounded. A key source of antifragility. (Ch 12)

**Overcompensation** — The response to stress that exceeds what's needed to recover, leaving the system stronger than before. A mechanism of antifragility. (Ch 2)

## P

**Philosopher's Stone (Inverse)** — The alchemical dream of turning lead into gold. Taleb's "inverse" version: how to turn gold into mud through concave exposure and intervention. (Ch 19)

**Procrastination (Fabian)** — Strategic delay as a form of optionality. Waiting for more information before acting, named after Fabius Maximus who delayed fighting Hannibal. (Ch 7)

## R

**Robust** — The middle state of the Triad: resists shocks but does not improve from them. Less desirable than antifragile. (Ch 1)

## S

**Seneca's Barbell** — The Stoic philosopher's financial strategy of keeping most wealth in safe assets while risking a small portion. The origin of the Barbell Strategy. (Ch 10, 11)

**Skin in the Game** — Having a personal stake in the outcome of one's actions. Essential for ethical behavior and antifragile systems. Absence creates fragility. (Ch 23)

**Stiglitz Syndrome** — Criticizing a system while benefiting from it, or making predictions without bearing the cost of being wrong. Named after economist Joseph Stiglitz. (Ch 23)

**Subtractive Knowledge** — Knowledge acquired by removing what doesn't work (via negativa) rather than adding what might. Taleb favors subtraction over addition. (Ch 19, 20)

**Sucker-Nonsucker Distinction** — Fat Tony's criterion: a sucker is someone who can be fooled by randomness, who doesn't understand asymmetry and optionality. (Ch 9, 17)

## T

**Teleological Fallacy** — The belief that everything has a purpose or design. Taleb argues that much of human progress comes from tinkering, not top-down planning. (Ch 11, 15)

**The Triad** — Fragile / Robust / Antifragile. Taleb's central classification system for how things respond to volatility and stress. (Ch 1)

**Touristification** — The systematic removal of randomness and stress from life or systems, making them more fragile. Like a tourist on a packaged tour vs. a traveler. (Ch 3, 16)

**Trial and Error** — The engine of antifragility. Random exploration with small failures leading to large successes. Contrasts with rational planning. (Ch 4, 15)

**Turkey Problem** — See Great Turkey Problem.

## V

**Via Negativa** — The way of subtraction. Improving by removing rather than adding. Taleb's preferred approach: remove fragility rather than try to add robustness. (Ch 19, 20)

**Volatility** — Variability and randomness in a system. Feared by the fragile, endured by the robust, loved by the antifragile. (Throughout)

## Y

**Yevos** — Taleb's term for evolutionary/organic systems that improve through stress and selection. (Ch 4)
