# Chapter 4: 我的试炼之路 (1983-1994)

## Core Idea
Dalio rebuilt Bridgewater by systematizing decision-making through algorithms and computer models, pioneering the separation of alpha and beta, and expanding globally — particularly in China. This period transformed Bridgewater from a small advisory into a major institutional investor.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Systematic Decision-Making**: Converting principles into algorithms
  - When to use: Repeated decisions with clear criteria
  - How: Write down decision criteria, backtest on historical data, implement in code
- **Alpha Overlay (阿尔法叠加)**: Separating passive market exposure from active bets
  - When to use: Portfolio construction and risk management
  - How: Identify beta (market return) separately from alpha (skill-based return)
- **Data-Driven Culture**: Using real-time data and algorithms for decisions
  - When to use: Investment management, business operations
  - How: Build systems that process data and suggest actions, human verifies

## Key Concepts
- **Daily Observations**: Dalio's daily market commentary, started in the late 1970s
- **McDonald's / McNugget story**: Using commodity futures to lock in chicken costs
- **Rate and currency systems**: Early computer models for macro trading
- **Bridgewater China Partners**: Early private equity effort in China (1994)
- **Hilda Ochoa / World Bank**: First institutional client, $5M bond mandate

## Mental Models
- Think of your brain and computer as partners — each does what it does best
- Use algorithms for processing speed and emotional neutrality; use humans for imagination and judgment
- View markets as systems that can be modeled, not as random walks

## Anti-patterns
- **Relying solely on intuition**: Even great intuition should be tested systematically
- **Ignoring timing**: Good fundamental analysis can still fail with bad timing
- **Over-diversification**: Spreading too thin reduces edge; concentrate on high-conviction ideas

## Key Takeaways
1. Computers can make better decisions than humans for rule-based, data-heavy problems
2. Separate beta (market) from alpha (skill) to evaluate true performance
3. Write down your decision criteria and backtest them
4. Build systems that learn and improve over time
5. Global perspective reveals opportunities others miss
