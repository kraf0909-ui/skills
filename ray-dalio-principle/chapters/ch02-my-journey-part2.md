# Chapter 2: 跨越门槛 (1967-1979)

## Core Idea
The 1967-1979 period taught Dalio that historical patterns repeat ("history rhymes"), that government promises about currency stability cannot be trusted, and that understanding macroeconomic cause-effect relationships is essential for survival in markets.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **History Rhymes**: Major economic events have happened before and will happen again
  - When to use: When encountering unprecedented-seeming situations
  - How: Study historical analogs, understand the causal mechanisms
- **Don't Trust Government Promises on Currency**: When officials promise no devaluation, the opposite is more likely
  - When to use: During currency crises or when evaluating sovereign risk
  - How: Watch actions, not words; study historical precedents
- **Cause-Effect Analysis**: Markets move based on logical cause-effect relationships, not magic
  - When to use: All investment and business decisions
  - How: Trace the chain from policy action → economic effect → market reaction

## Key Concepts
- **Nixon Shock (1971)**: Dollar-gold peg broken, dollar devalued
- **Oil Crisis (1973)**: First oil shock, stagflation begins
- **Interest rate futures / commodity futures**: Early derivatives markets Dalio traded
- **Yield curve inversion**: Short rates above long rates as recession signal
- **Beautiful 50 stocks**: Nifty Fifty bubble and burst

## Mental Models
- Think of markets as machines with discoverable cause-effect relationships
- Use "history rhymes" when someone says "this time is different"
- Consider second-order and third-order effects, not just immediate reactions

## Anti-patterns
- **Believing "this time is different"**: The most expensive words in investing
- **Trusting official denials**: Especially about currency stability
- **Confusing nominal and real returns**: Inflation can erode apparent gains
- **Overconfidence in recent trends**: The 1960s bull market bred complacency

## Key Takeaways
1. Study history — major events have happened before and follow patterns
2. Government officials often deny imminent currency problems right before they happen
3. Cause-effect relationships in markets are logical and discoverable
4. Inflation and deflation are both threats — position for both possibilities
5. Commodity markets follow supply-demand logic more directly than stocks
