# Patterns

## Decision-Making Patterns

### Expected Value Decision
**When to use**: Any decision under uncertainty with multiple possible outcomes.
**How**:
1. List all possible outcomes for each option
2. Estimate probability of each outcome
3. Estimate value (positive or negative) of each outcome
4. Calculate: Probability × Value for each outcome
5. Sum to get expected value for each option
6. Choose the option with highest expected value
**Trade-offs**: Requires accurate probability and value estimates; people are systematically biased in both.

### Two-Step Decision Process
**When to use**: All significant decisions, especially where emotions run high.
**How**:
1. Learning phase: Gather all relevant information, seek contrary views, understand causal mechanisms
2. Decision phase: Only after thorough learning, form conclusion and act
**Trade-offs**: Slower than intuitive decisions; prevents premature conclusions.

### Credibility-Weighted Input
**When to use**: Group decisions where expertise varies significantly.
**How**:
1. Identify who has relevant track record (3+ successful experiences)
2. Assess quality of their reasoning (logical, evidence-based, causal)
3. Weight their input accordingly
4. If you disagree with highly credible people, investigate why before acting
**Trade-offs**: Requires accurate credibility assessment; can create hierarchy concerns if not implemented transparently.

## Problem-Solving Patterns

### Five-Step Process
**When to use**: Any personal or organizational goal achievement.
**How**:
1. Have clear goals (prioritized, realistic, aligned with values)
2. Identify problems and don't tolerate them (be precise about what the problem is)
3. Diagnose to root causes (distinguish symptoms from causes)
4. Design a plan (like a movie script: who does what, when)
5. Push through to completion (establish metrics, habits, accountability)
**Trade-offs**: Requires discipline to not skip steps; most people fail at steps 2 or 5.

### Pain + Reflection = Progress
**When to use**: After any failure, setback, or painful experience.
**How**:
1. Experience the pain (don't suppress it)
2. Record what happened while fresh
3. Reflect when calm (not while emotional)
4. Extract the principle: what would you do differently?
5. Apply the principle to future similar situations
**Trade-offs**: Requires emotional maturity; many people avoid pain rather than learn from it.

### Root Cause Analysis
**When to use**: When problems recur or when significant failures occur.
**How**:
1. Define the problem precisely (not "we failed" but "X happened instead of Y")
2. Ask "why" repeatedly (typically 5 times) until you reach fundamental cause
3. Distinguish proximate causes from root causes
4. Identify which principle was violated
5. Design fix that addresses root cause, not just symptom
**Trade-offs**: Takes more time than symptomatic fixes; prevents recurrence.

## Organizational Patterns

### Idea Meritocracy Implementation
**When to use**: Building or transforming organizational decision-making.
**How**:
1. Establish radical truth as cultural norm (honest feedback, no shooting messengers)
2. Implement radical transparency (information broadly accessible)
3. Build credibility tracking (baseball cards, track records)
4. Create credibility-weighted decision mechanisms
5. Train people in thoughtful disagreement
**Trade-offs**: Uncomfortable for most people initially; requires ~18 months for cultural adaptation; some people will leave.

### Organization as Machine
**When to use**: Designing, managing, or transforming organizations.
**How**:
1. Define clear goals and outcomes
2. Design processes (the "machine") to produce those outcomes
3. Put the right people in the right roles
4. Measure outcomes vs. goals
5. Diagnose: design problem or people problem?
6. Redesign accordingly
**Trade-offs**: Can feel impersonal if not balanced with meaningful relationships; requires significant management discipline.

### Error-Driven Learning System
**When to use**: Building a learning organization.
**How**:
1. Create psychological safety for admitting errors
2. Log errors systematically (what, when, who, impact)
3. Analyze errors without blame (root cause analysis)
4. Extract principles for prevention
5. Implement changes
6. Track whether same errors recur
**Trade-offs**: Requires tolerance for visible failure; can be uncomfortable in cultures that demand perfection.

## People Management Patterns

### Values-Ability-Skills Hiring
**When to use**: All hiring decisions.
**How**:
1. Assess values first (truth-seeking, openness, commitment to mission)
2. Assess ability second (capacity to learn, adapt, think)
3. Assess skills last (specific technical capabilities — most teachable)
4. Reject candidates who lack values fit, even if strong on skills
**Trade-offs**: Reduces candidate pool; may slow hiring; prevents costly bad hires.

### Baseball Card Profiling
**When to use**: Team building, role assignment, development planning.
**How**:
1. Assess individuals across multiple dimensions (personality, skills, track record)
2. Create visible profiles showing strengths and weaknesses
3. Use for role matching (put people where they excel)
4. Share profiles transparently within team
5. Update based on ongoing performance data
**Trade-offs**: Requires sophisticated assessment; can feel invasive without proper cultural foundation.

### Tough Love Feedback
**When to use**: Performance management, coaching, development.
**How**:
1. Show genuine care for the person's success
2. Give specific, accurate feedback on performance gaps
3. Focus on behaviors and outcomes, not personality
4. Provide support and resources for improvement
5. Set clear expectations and timelines
6. Follow through: recognize improvement or take further action
**Trade-offs**: Can feel harsh initially; requires trust to be received well; essential for high performance.

## Communication Patterns

### Thoughtful Disagreement
**When to use**: When you disagree with someone on an important topic.
**How**:
1. Assume the other person might see something you don't
2. Seek to understand their reasoning before defending your view
3. Ask questions to explore their perspective
4. Find areas of agreement first
5. Focus on finding the best answer, not winning the argument
**Trade-offs**: Takes more time than quick decisions; requires emotional maturity.

### Two-Minute Rule in Discussion
**When to use**: Meetings, debates, any group discussion.
**How**:
1. Give each person up to two minutes to express their view without interruption
2. Use a timer to enforce
3. After two minutes, others can ask clarifying questions
4. Only then should counter-arguments be offered
**Trade-offs**: Slightly slows discussion; ensures all voices are heard.
