# Patterns & Frameworks — The Evolution of Central Banks

## 1. The Natural Development Framework

```
Central Bank Evolution:

Government Imposition Theory (Wrong):
Government decides → Creates central bank → Imposes on banking system

Natural Development Theory (Goodhart):
Banking system develops → Market failures emerge →
  - Need for liquidity concentration
  - Need for reserve management
  - Need for supervisory discipline
  → Central bank evolves naturally
  → Macroeconomic functions added later
```

**Application**: When analyzing any central bank's origins, look for microeconomic structural needs first, not macroeconomic policy mandates.

## 2. The Twin Functions Framework

```
Banks Provide Two Functions:
1. Payment Services (transactions, clearing)
2. Portfolio Management (maturity transformation)

The Combination Creates Fragility:
- Deposits are fixed nominal value claims
- Assets are non-marketable, uncertain value
- Mismatch creates run risk

Goodhart's Proposal:
Separate the functions → Mutual fund banks for payments
→ Eliminates bank runs while preserving credit intermediation
```

**Application**: When designing financial reform, consider whether separating payment and portfolio functions would improve stability.

## 3. Market Failure Hierarchy in Banking

```
Level 1: Information Asymmetry
  - Depositors cannot assess bank risk
  - Banks cannot fully monitor each other
  → Clubs emerge

Level 2: Club Limitations
  - Conflicts of interest among members
  - No enforcement power
  - Cannot handle systemic crises
  → Need for independent arbiter

Level 3: Central Bank Emergence
  - Independent from commercial interests
  - Has enforcement power
  - Can provide liquidity in crises
  - Can supervise the system
```

**Application**: When evaluating banking regulation, assess which level of market failure is being addressed.

## 4. The Free Banking Critique Pattern

```
Free Banking Argument:
1. Competition disciplines banks
2. Clearinghouses prevent overissue
3. Market forces ensure stability

Goodhart's Counterarguments:
1. Competition creates procyclicality (race to the bottom in booms)
2. Clearinghouses fail during systemic crises
3. Historical "successes" (Scotland, Canada) relied on external liquidity support
4. Information asymmetries persist

Conclusion: Free banking theory underestimates structural market failures
```

**Application**: When encountering arguments for deregulation or free banking, test them against these structural critiques.

## 5. The Procyclicality Pattern

```
Banking System Dynamics:

Boom Phase:
- Competition drives credit expansion
- Risk assessment weakens
- Reserves decline relative to deposits
- Asset prices rise, collateral values increase
- Feedback loop amplifies expansion

Crisis Phase:
- Shock triggers doubt
- Depositors withdraw
- Banks simultaneously contract
- Asset prices fall
- Feedback loop amplifies contraction

Central Bank's Role:
- Countercyclical liquidity provision
- Supervisory restraint during booms
- Lender of last resort during crises
```

**Application**: When analyzing banking crises, map the boom-bust cycle and identify where central bank intervention could have helped.

## 6. The Insurance Impossibility Pattern

```
Private Deposit Insurance Fails Because:
1. Risk Assessment Problem
   - Bank assets are opaque
   - True risk cannot be objectively measured
   - Adverse selection: risky banks most eager to buy

2. Moral Hazard
   - Insured banks take more risk
   - Depositors stop monitoring
   - Risk premium disappears

3. Resource Inadequacy
   - Private funds insufficient for systemic crisis
   - Cannot credibly promise to pay

4. Enforcement Gap
   - No authority to close failing banks
   - No power to enforce discipline

Result: Any viable insurance scheme becomes functionally equivalent to a central bank
```

**Application**: When evaluating deposit insurance schemes, assess whether they address these fundamental problems.

## 7. The Clearinghouse Limitations Pattern

```
What Clearinghouses Can Do:
- Settle interbank claims
- Monitor note issuance (to some extent)
- Provide limited liquidity support

What Clearinghouses Cannot Do:
- Prevent simultaneous expansion of all banks
- Prevent simultaneous contraction during crises
- Overcome conflicts of interest among members
- Provide credible enforcement
- Handle systemic liquidity needs

Historical Evidence:
- Suffolk Bank: Failed to prevent crises
- New York Clearinghouse: Required government support in 1907
- Private clearinghouses: Always limited by member interests
```

**Application**: When evaluating private alternatives to central banking, test whether they can handle systemic crises.

## 8. The Non-Marketable Assets Theory

```
Why Banks Are Fragile:

Standard View:
- Banks borrow short and lend long (maturity mismatch)
- This creates run risk

Goodhart's Deeper View:
- Bank assets are largely non-marketable (loans)
- True value is uncertain and costly to determine
- Depositors prefer fixed nominal value claims
- This combination makes banks inherently fragile

Implication:
- Fragility is structural, not just cyclical
- Cannot be eliminated by better regulation alone
- Requires institutional support (central bank)
```

**Application**: When analyzing bank fragility, look beyond maturity mismatch to the non-marketable nature of bank assets.

## 9. The Historical Comparative Method

```
Goodhart's Approach:
1. Examine historical cases of free banking
   - Scotland (before 1845)
   - Canada (before 1935)
   - United States (before 1913)

2. Test whether they were truly "free"
   - Did they have access to external liquidity?
   - Was there implicit government support?
   - How frequent were banking crises?

3. Draw conclusions about natural evolution
   - What functions emerged?
   - What market failures persisted?
   - How did central banks develop?

Key Finding: "Free" banking systems either had external support or experienced frequent crises
```

**Application**: When using historical examples in policy debates, examine whether they truly support the claimed conclusions.

## 10. The Policy Implications Framework

```
From Goodhart's Analysis:

What Central Banks Should Do:
1. Provide liquidity in crises (lender of last resort)
2. Supervise banks to limit excessive risk-taking
3. Maintain payment system stability
4. Countercyclical policy to offset banking procyclicality

What Central Banks Cannot Do:
1. Eliminate all banking crises
2. Replace market discipline entirely
3. Prevent all moral hazard
4. Fine-tune the economy perfectly

The Fundamental Trade-off:
More support → More stability but more moral hazard
Less support → Less moral hazard but more crises
```

**Application**: When designing central bank policy, acknowledge the fundamental trade-offs rather than seeking optimal solutions.
