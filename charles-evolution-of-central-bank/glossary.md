# Glossary — The Evolution of Central Banks

## A

**Asymmetric Information** — The imbalance of knowledge between banks and depositors, or among banks themselves. A fundamental market failure that makes private monitoring and discipline inadequate. (Ch 5)

## B

**Bagehot, Walter** — 19th-century British journalist and economist. Advocated for a "natural system" of banking where note issuance would be concentrated in a single bank through market forces rather than government design. (Ch 2)

**Bagehot's Principle** — The doctrine that in a crisis, the central bank should lend freely at a penalty rate to solvent but illiquid banks. (Ch 2)

**Bank Run** — A sudden withdrawal of deposits by many customers simultaneously, often triggered by fear of insolvency. A key problem that central banks help mitigate. (Ch 4, 5, 7)

**Brand Name Capital** — The reputational investment banks make to signal safety to depositors. Fragile because it can be destroyed quickly by rumors. (Ch 5)

## C

**Central Bank** — An institution that evolved naturally from the banking system's need for liquidity concentration, reserve management, and supervisory discipline. Not originally designed for macroeconomic management. (Throughout)

**Clearinghouse** — A mechanism for settling interbank claims. While it can provide some disciplinary function, it cannot prevent simultaneous expansion or contraction of the banking system. (Ch 3)

**Club (Banking Club)** — An arrangement where banks cooperate to share information and monitor each other. Emerges naturally due to information inadequacy but requires an independent arbiter (central bank) to be effective. (Ch 5)

**Contagion Risk** — The danger that failure of one bank will spread to others due to depositor panic and information asymmetry. A key justification for central bank intervention. (Ch 5)

## D

**Deposit Insurance** — Government-backed guarantee of deposits. Private alternatives fail due to moral hazard, inability to assess risk, and insufficient resources in systemic crises. (Ch 6)

**Discount Window** — The facility through which central banks lend to commercial banks, typically against eligible collateral. (Ch 7)

## F

**Free Banking** — The theory that banking should be left to competitive market forces without a central bank. Goodhart argues this fails due to inherent market imperfections. (Ch 2, 3, 4)

## H

**Hayek, Friedrich** — Austrian economist who advocated for the denationalization of money and free banking. Goodhart critiques his view as overlooking key market failures. (Ch 2)

**Henry Thornton** — Early 19th-century British economist who argued for a centralized banking system with a single bank holding the reserves of all others. (Ch 2)

## I

**Information Inadequacy** — The core market failure in banking: depositors cannot adequately assess bank risk, and banks cannot fully monitor each other. Leads to the natural emergence of clubs and central banks. (Ch 5)

**Irrational Exuberance** — The tendency for banks to expand credit excessively during booms, driven by competition and optimism. (Ch 4)

## L

**Lender of Last Resort** — The central bank's function of providing liquidity to solvent but illiquid banks during crises. A key role that evolved naturally from the banking system's needs. (Ch 2, 7)

**Liquidity** — The ability to meet short-term obligations. Central banks concentrate liquidity and provide it to the system when needed. (Ch 3, 7)

## M

**Macroeconomic Management** — The use of monetary policy to manage aggregate demand, inflation, and employment. Goodhart argues this was a later addition to the central bank's original microeconomic functions. (Ch 1, 8)

**Market Failure** — Situations where free markets fail to produce efficient outcomes. In banking, information asymmetries and externalities create persistent market failures that justify central banking. (Throughout)

**Maturity Transformation** — The practice of borrowing short (deposits) and lending long (loans). Creates inherent fragility in the banking system. (Ch 7)

**Monetary Policy** — The management of money supply and interest rates. Originally a byproduct of central banking, not its primary purpose. (Ch 1, 8)

**Moral Hazard** — The tendency for insurance or guarantees to encourage riskier behavior. A major problem in banking that makes private insurance difficult. (Ch 6)

**Mutual Fund Bank** — Goodhart's proposed alternative where payment services are separated from portfolio management. Deposits would be claims on a diversified portfolio, eliminating bank runs. (Ch 7)

## N

**Natural Development** — Goodhart's thesis that central banks evolved naturally from the banking system's structural needs, rather than being imposed by government. (Ch 1, 8)

**Non-Marketable Assets** — Loans and other bank assets whose true value is difficult to determine because they are not traded in liquid markets. A key source of banking fragility. (Ch 7)

## P

**Portfolio Management** — One of the twin functions of banks (along with payments). Involves transforming short-term deposits into long-term loans. Creates fragility due to non-marketable assets. (Ch 7)

**Payment System** — The infrastructure for transferring money between parties. One of the twin functions of banks. Requires stability and confidence. (Ch 7)

**Private Insurance** — Theoretical alternative to central bank support. Goodhart argues it fails due to information problems, moral hazard, and inability to handle systemic crises. (Ch 6)

**Procyclicality** — The tendency of the banking system to amplify economic cycles by expanding credit in booms and contracting in busts. (Ch 4)

## R

**Reserve Management** — The concentration and management of bank reserves. A natural function that leads to the emergence of a central bank. (Ch 3, 7)

**Reserves** — Funds held by banks to meet withdrawals and interbank obligations. Centralization of reserves is a key step in central bank evolution. (Ch 3)

## S

**Scottish Free Banking** — Often cited as a successful example of free banking. Goodhart argues it succeeded because it had access to liquidity support from London. (Ch 4)

**Self-Regulation** — The idea that banks can monitor and discipline each other. Limited by conflicts of interest and information asymmetries. (Ch 5)

**Suffolk Bank** — A historical private clearinghouse in the United States that attempted to regulate note issuance. Ultimately failed to prevent banking crises. (Ch 3)

**Supervisory Discipline** — The oversight of banks to ensure safety and soundness. A natural function that evolves into central bank supervision. (Ch 5, 7)

**Systemic Risk** — The danger that failure of one institution will cause cascading failures throughout the financial system. Justifies central bank intervention. (Ch 5, 6)

## T

**Thornton, Henry** — See Henry Thornton.

**Too Big to Fail** — The doctrine that certain institutions cannot be allowed to fail due to systemic consequences. Creates moral hazard but is unavoidable. (Ch 6, 7)

**Twin Functions** — Goodhart's framework: banks provide both payment services and portfolio management. The combination creates fragility. (Ch 7)

## V

**Velocity of Circulation** — The rate at which money changes hands. Affected by banking system stability and confidence. (Ch 4)

## W

**White, Lawrence** — Modern economist who has argued for free banking. Goodhart critiques his historical interpretation. (Ch 2, 4)
