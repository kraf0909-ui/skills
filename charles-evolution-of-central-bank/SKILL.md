## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### Natural Development Thesis
Central banks did not emerge because governments decided to create them for macroeconomic management. They evolved naturally from the banking system's own structural needs:
- **Liquidity concentration**: Banks need a place to hold reserves and access emergency liquidity
- **Reserve management**: A centralized mechanism for managing interbank obligations
- **Supervisory discipline**: An independent arbiter to monitor and enforce prudential standards

**Key insight**: Macroeconomic functions (monetary policy, inflation targeting) were grafted onto central banks later. The microeconomic functions came first and remain more fundamental.

### The Twin Functions Framework
Banks provide two distinct services that create structural fragility when combined:
1. **Payment services**: Transaction processing, clearing, settlement
2. **Portfolio management**: Maturity transformation, credit intermediation

The combination is fragile because:
- Deposits are fixed nominal value claims (depositors expect to withdraw at par)
- Bank assets are largely non-marketable loans (true value is uncertain)
- This mismatch creates inherent run risk

**Goodhart's proposal**: Separate the functions. Use mutual fund banks for payment services (where deposits are claims on a diversified portfolio, eliminating run risk) while preserving credit intermediation through other vehicles.

### Market Failure Hierarchy
Banking suffers from persistent market failures that justify central banking:
1. **Information asymmetry**: Depositors cannot assess bank risk; banks cannot fully monitor each other
2. **Externalities**: One bank's failure can cascade through the system (contagion)
3. **Collective action problems**: Private clubs and clearinghouses are limited by conflicts of interest and lack enforcement power

**Result**: Private solutions (clubs, clearinghouses, private insurance) inevitably prove inadequate. A public institution with independence from commercial interests and enforcement authority is needed.

### The Free Banking Critique
Goodhart systematically dismantles the case for free banking:
- **Competition does not ensure stability**: It creates procyclicality — banks race to expand in booms and simultaneously contract in crises
- **Clearinghouses are inadequate**: They cannot prevent system-wide expansion/contraction or handle systemic crises
- **Historical "successes" are misleading**: Scottish and Canadian free banking succeeded because they had access to external liquidity (London) and/or implicit government support
- **Information problems persist**: No amount of competition solves the fundamental information asymmetry between banks and depositors

### The Non-Marketable Assets Theory
Goodhart's most original contribution: bank fragility is not just about maturity mismatch. It is rooted in the fact that bank assets (loans) are non-marketable:
- True value is costly to determine
- Cannot be quickly liquidated at fair prices
- This makes banks inherently fragile regardless of regulation

**Implication**: Banking crises cannot be eliminated by better regulation alone. Institutional support (central banks as lenders of last resort) is structurally necessary.
