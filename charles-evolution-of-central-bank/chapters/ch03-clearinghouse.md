# Chapter 3: The Key Role of the Clearinghouse

## Core Idea
The nineteenth-century free banking debate centered on whether the clearinghouse mechanism could provide sufficient automatic discipline to prevent excessive bank expansion. Goodhart argues that while clearinghouses do create a powerful centripetal force toward reserve centralization and can check individual bank overissue, they suffer from two critical flaws: (1) they synchronize all banks to expand or contract together without determining whether the average rate of growth is stable, and (2) they can be circumvented by aggressive banks offering above-market interest rates to retain deposits. More importantly, the historical record shows that clearinghouses and dominant correspondent banks naturally begin to assume quasi-central banking functions—but these private arrangements are fundamentally limited by conflicts of interest. True central banking requires an institution that is noncompetitive and non-profit-maximizing, a metamorphosis that private commercial competitors cannot achieve.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Clearinghouse Discipline**: The mechanism whereby a bank expanding faster than the average loses reserves through the clearing process, theoretically restraining overissue.
- **Natural Evolution of Central Banking**: The historical observation that even without legislation, private banking systems tend to develop centralizing institutions (clearinghouses, correspondent systems, dominant banks) that perform quasi-central banking functions.
- **Conflict of Interest Framework**: The systematic analysis of why a competitive, profit-maximizing institution cannot adequately perform regulatory, supervisory, or lender-of-last-resort functions for its rivals.
- **Information Asymmetry Between Banks and Depositors**: Banks possess superior information about each other's standing and riskiness compared to nonbank individuals, leading to a natural concentration of interbank deposits among the strongest institutions.
- **The Metamorphosis to Noncompetitive Status**: The argument that true central banking only emerges when an institution transcends its commercial, profit-maximizing role.

## Key Concepts
- **Adverse Clearing Balances**: The automatic loss of reserves suffered by a relatively fast-growing bank through the clearinghouse, which was seen as the primary check on overissue in free banking theory.
- **Suffolk Bank System**: A voluntary system in New England (started 1819) where the Suffolk Bank acted as a clearing agent, receiving deposits from country banks and redeeming their notes—an early example of private "free enterprise" central banking.
- **Correspondent System**: The network of interbank deposit relationships, particularly in unit banking systems (e.g., the U.S.), which naturally leads to the pyramiding and centralization of reserves in major city banks.
- **Optional Clause**: A historical Scottish banking practice (prohibited 1765) where bank notes gave the issuer the option of deferred payment with interest, used to prevent note redemption runs.
- **Post Notes**: Interest-bearing notes payable at a fixed future date, issued by Boston banks (1825-1828) and others, representing an attempt to circumvent clearinghouse discipline.
- **Lender of Last Resort**: The function of providing emergency liquidity during crises. Goodhart argues this cannot be adequately performed by private competitors due to conflicts of interest.
- **Moral Hazard in Central Banking**: The risk that explicit or implicit central bank support will weaken the self-reliance of commercial banks, a concern famously raised by Hankey in his debate with Bagehot.

## Mental Models
- **The Centripetal Force of Banking**: Natural market forces (clearing needs, correspondent services, information advantages) constantly push banking systems toward centralization, even without government intervention.
- **Trade Association Limitations**: Even clearinghouse associations, as trade associations of commercial competitors, retain considerable scope for conflicts of interest. Support operations tend to be limited to the "lowest common denominator" of what competing banks will agree to.
- **The Impossibility of Self-Regulation by Competitors**: A bank that is also a competitor cannot impartially regulate, supervise, or decide whether to support its rivals. The range of possible conflicts is considerable—from aggressive note redemption to refusing crisis assistance.
- **Leadership vs. Financial Resources**: In most serious banking crises, the central bank does not have sufficient resources to resolve the crisis alone. Its crucial role is providing leadership and a "seal of approval" that encourages collusive action among other leading banks (e.g., the Baring crisis of 1890, the 1973-74 "lifeboat").

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming Clearinghouses Are Sufficient**: The free banking assumption that clearinghouse discipline alone can maintain monetary stability ignores that (a) it synchronizes rather than stabilizes expansion, and (b) it can be circumvented by interest-rate competition for deposits.
- **Believing Private Institutions Can Replace Central Banks**: Timberlake and others suggest clearinghouses could take over micro-structural central banking functions. Goodhart argues this is historically inaccurate and logically flawed due to conflicts of interest.
- **Ignoring the Dark Side of Correspondent Relationships**: The natural centralization of reserves with dominant banks creates not only efficiency but also vulnerability—competitors are unwilling to fully rely on a rival, and the dominant bank may use its position anti-competitively.
- **The 1844 Bank of England Act as Faulty Concept**: Codifying the central bank as "just one competitive bank among many" was an incorrect concept that impeded the development of true central banking in England.

## Key Takeaways
1. The clearinghouse mechanism tends to make all banks expand or contract at a similar rate, but does not determine whether that average rate is stable or subject to sharp fluctuations.
2. Aggressive banks can circumvent clearinghouse discipline by offering above-market interest rates on deposits, creating an informational problem for depositors who cannot distinguish efficiency from riskiness.
3. Historical examples (Suffolk Bank, U.S. clearinghouses, New York correspondent banks) demonstrate that private banking systems naturally evolve quasi-central banking functions—but these are inherently limited.
4. Conflicts of interest are pervasive and severe: dominant commercial banks have repeatedly used their central position anti-competitively (e.g., Bank of England vs. joint-stock banks in the 1830s, Banque de France vs. Credit Mobilier, National Bank of Commerce vs. Knickerbocker Trust in 1907).
5. True central banking requires an institution to be "above the competitive battle"—noncompetitive and non-profit-maximizing. This metamorphosis was difficult, slow, and often resisted (e.g., by Bank of England shareholders and directors like Hankey).
6. The moral hazard created by central bank support is real, but the solution is not to abolish the central bank; it is to develop appropriate supervision and regulation.
