# Chapter 2: The Case for Free Banking

## Core Idea
The intellectual and historical case for free banking rests on two main pillars: (1) a general free-trade analogy, arguing that competition and laissez-faire should apply to banking just as to other industries, and (2) a deep distrust of government and monopolistic management of currency, which is seen as inherently prone to inflationary abuse. Goodhart systematically presents these arguments—drawing on Bagehot, Henry Thornton, Hayek, and modern free-banking economists—while also exposing their internal contradictions and practical limitations. The chapter establishes that while the free banking case is theoretically coherent, it fails to account for the natural centralizing tendencies and systemic instability that arise in real-world banking systems.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Free Trade Analogy in Banking**: The argument that if free competition is beneficial in other trades, the onus is on regulators to prove why banking should be an exception.
- **Natural System of Banking**: Bagehot's hypothetical ideal of many independent banks of roughly equal size, each maintaining its own reserve, with no single bank predominating.
- **One-Reserve System vs. Many-Reserve System**: The contrast between a centralized system (where one bank holds the ultimate reserve) and a decentralized system (where many banks hold their own reserves).
- **Inherent Inflationary Tendencies of Central Banks**: The argument that central banks, due to their monopoly power and relationship with government, have a structural bias toward inflationary overissue.
- **Market Discipline via Reputation**: Klein's and Hayek's argument that under competitive conditions, banks will maintain the value of their liabilities to preserve reputation and attract depositors.

## Key Concepts
- **Bagehot's "Natural System"**: A hypothetical banking system of many independent banks, each keeping its own reserve, which Bagehot preferred in theory but considered politically impractical to implement.
- **Henry Thornton's Centralized System**: Thornton's opposing view that a single principal bank is best equipped to bear the responsibility for systemic stability, and that dividing this responsibility would lead to unsafe competition.
- **Legal Tender and Inconvertibility**: The dangers of designating bank notes as legal tender while suspending convertibility, which historically provided governments with an inflationary tax revenue mechanism.
- **Seignorage**: The economic profit derived from issuing money (the margin between the zero interest on notes and the interest on assets held against them), which Fama argues is a logical target for government confiscation via central bank monopoly.
- **Gresham's Law in Free Banking**: The possibility that less trustworthy (inflationary) bank monies might drive out more conservative ones, unless information is perfect and costless.
- **Optional Clause**: A historical practice (e.g., in Scotland until 1765) where bank notes gave the issuer the option of paying with interest after a fixed date rather than on demand, used to prevent runs.
- **Denationalisation of Money**: Hayek's radical proposal to allow private issuers to compete not just in note issue but in the standard of value (e.g., commodity baskets), going beyond the classical Gold Standard free banking model.

## Mental Models
- **The Political Impossibility of Revolution**: Bagehot's pragmatic acceptance that despite the theoretical superiority of free banking, the abolition of the Bank of England was "childish" and "not practical politics"—credit and loyalty, once established, cannot be easily unwound.
- **Reputation as Constraint**: In a competitive banking system without a central bank, a bank's most valuable asset is its reputation for maintaining the value of its liabilities. Market forces will punish inflationary or risky behavior by driving depositors to more conservative competitors.
- **The Inflation Tax Mechanism**: Governments have historically used their exclusive power to issue money to "defraud and plunder the people" (Hayek). Central banks, as government creatures, are structurally vulnerable to this abuse.
- **Information Asymmetry in Currency Competition**: The assumption that depositors can perfectly monitor and compare the riskiness of different bank monies is unrealistic. In practice, information is costly, and externalities exist.

## Anti-patterns
- **Naive Free Banking Idealism**: Bagehot's "engagingly naive" enthusiasm for the natural system ignores that banks themselves have incentives to shift the burden of holding reserves onto others, leading naturally to centralization.
- **Ignoring the Procyclical Instinct of Banks**: The assumption that independent banks would lend freely during crises is contradicted by historical evidence. In panics, all bankers become anxious, augment their reserves, and restrict lending—exacerbating the crisis.
- **Conflating Free Banking with Expansionism**: Many historical advocates of free banking were actually "expansionists" who wanted to hold down interest rates, not maintain monetary stability. This confusion weakens the intellectual coherence of the free banking movement.
- **Assuming Perfect Information**: Modern free banking models (Klein, Hayek) rely heavily on the assumption of perfect or near-perfect information. In reality, ordinary depositors cannot easily discern whether a bank's higher deposit rates reflect greater efficiency or greater risk.

## Key Takeaways
1. Bagehot, despite being the seminal writer on central banking, theoretically preferred a "natural system" of many independent banks. However, he considered the abolition of the Bank of England politically impossible.
2. Henry Thornton argued the opposite: a single principal bank is necessary to prevent unsafe competition and to ensure that responsibility for systemic stability is not divided.
3. A major strand of the anti-central bank argument is distrust of government management of paper currency, rooted in historical episodes of inflationary abuse (e.g., John Law, the Assignats, the Bank of England's 1797-1819 suspension).
4. Hayek's evolution from advocating competition between national currencies to full "denationalisation of money" (private note issue competing on standards of value) represents the most radical form of the free banking argument.
5. Even within the free banking framework, market discipline relies on costly information and reputation mechanisms that may be insufficient to prevent systemic instability or inflationary overissue.
