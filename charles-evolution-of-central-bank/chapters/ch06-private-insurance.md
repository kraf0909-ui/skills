# Chapter 6: Private Insurance?

## Core Idea

If Central Banks are to be abolished or severely restricted, an alternative mechanism is needed to provide the support and insurance functions that contain contagious banking crises. The proposal that private deposit insurance could substitute for Central Bank support is examined and found wanting. Private insurance schemes face insurmountable problems: there is no objective means of assessing risk for premium-setting; moral hazard cannot be adequately controlled without supervision and regulation equivalent to that already exercised by Central Banks; private insurance funds are vulnerable to insolvency when systemic crises occur; and the enforcement authority of private bodies over their member banks is weak without public-sector backing. The chapter concludes that private deposit insurance is impracticable as a general solution and that any viable insurance arrangement would evolve into an institution functionally equivalent to a Central Bank or public regulatory body.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Private Insurance Critique**: A systematic examination of why private deposit insurance schemes fail to replicate the functions of Central Bank support, covering risk assessment, moral hazard, enforcement, and systemic resilience.
- **Moral Hazard in Deposit Insurance**: The framework that insurance protection, whether public or private, creates incentives for insured institutions to take on greater risk than they would otherwise, necessitating accompanying regulation and supervision.
- **Public vs. Private Insurance Comparison**: A comparative analysis demonstrating that government-sponsored insurance has natural advantages over private alternatives due to powers of taxation, money creation, and enforcement authority.

## Key Concepts

- **Private Deposit Insurance**: The proposal that banks could combine to set up private insurance of deposits, argued to be in the interests of the banks themselves to prevent contagious crises.
- **Risk-Based Premiums**: The theoretical ideal that insurance premiums should vary with the riskiness of the insured institution's assets. In practice, there is no objective and widely agreed means of assessing such risk, and premiums unrelated to risk create incentives for excessive risk-taking (as with FDIC and FSLIC in the 1984 CEA report).
- **Moral Hazard**: The tendency of insured institutions to take on more risk than they would otherwise, since they obtain the benefits of higher yields while the insurance fund bears the costs of greater risk. Variable premiums may not remove this problem, especially for insolvent firms under insurer control (Guttentag and Herring, 1982a).
- **Capture of Regulatory Agencies**: The danger that a private insurance agency may be "captured" by its major clients, potentially more so than in the case of a public agency.
- **Systemic Insolvency of Insurance Funds**: The risk that a private insurance fund itself becomes insolvent when facing widespread bank failures. Examples include the New York Safety Fund (1845) and the private insurance agencies for S&Ls in Ohio and Maryland, where the failure of just one large institution was sufficient to exhaust reserves.
- **Public Backing as Necessity**: The argument that some public institution must stand behind any private insurance system, ready to inject additional money if necessary—at which point the authorities would wish to oversee the system's strategic deliberations.
- **Limited Private Insurance**: The more viable case for privately organized insurance when the objective is limited to protecting a proportion of small depositors' holdings in failing banks, rather than deterring runs on banks generally (as in the UK bank and building society deposit insurance schemes).

## Mental Models

- **The Insurance-Regulation Duality**: Deposit insurance and prudential regulation are inseparable twins. You cannot have effective insurance without regulation, and regulation without some form of insurance is incomplete. Any institution providing one will inevitably be drawn into providing the other.
- **The Systemic Risk Trap**: Private insurance works well for actuarially calculable, diversifiable risks (like personal accidents). Banking risks are fundamentally different—they are subject to macro-political-economy developments (wars, changes in government, major monetary policy shifts) that are neither predictable nor influenceable by insurers and that affect the whole system simultaneously.
- **The Transition to Public Authority**: Even if private insurance is established, the logic of systemic risk pushes inexorably toward public backing, public oversight, and ultimately a public institution functionally indistinguishable from a Central Bank.

## Anti-patterns

- **Assuming Private Insurance Can Replace Central Banking**: The belief that private contractual arrangements can replicate the systemic support functions of a Central Bank without equivalent regulatory authority and public backing.
- **Flat-Rate Premiums**: Charging insurance premiums proportional to deposit volume without regard to asset riskiness, which systematically subsidizes risky institutions and penalizes conservative ones.
- **Ignoring the Enforcement Problem**: Expecting private insurance bodies to enforce portfolio restrictions and supervisory standards on their member banks without legal authority comparable to that of public regulators.
- **Overlooking Systemic Correlation**: Treating banking risks as independently distributed actuarial events when they are in fact highly correlated through macroeconomic and political shocks.
- **Relying on 100% Private Coverage**: Believing that complete private insurance coverage would solve the problem, when in fact it would create even greater moral hazard and require even more extensive regulation.

## Key Takeaways

1. Private deposit insurance schemes have historically been rare and largely unsuccessful when not imposed or strongly encouraged by public authorities.
2. There is no objective, widely agreed means of assessing bank risk for insurance premium-setting purposes.
3. If insurance premiums are unrelated to risk, insured institutions have incentives to take on excessive risk—necessitating accompanying regulation and supervision equivalent to Central Bank functions.
4. Private insurance funds are vulnerable to insolvency in systemic crises and lack the powers of taxation and money creation that give government-sponsored insurance its credibility.
5. A private insurance body would struggle to enforce its authority over member banks without official and legal support; the danger of regulatory capture by major clients may be greater than with public agencies.
6. The end result of attempting to provide general deposit insurance privately would likely be an institution functionally equivalent to existing Central Bank supervisory departments or separate public regulatory bodies.
7. The case for limited private insurance is stronger when the objective is restricted to protecting small depositors in failing banks rather than preventing systemic runs.
8. The main risks to deposit insurers arise not from actuarially calculable events but from macro-political-economy developments that private insurers can neither predict nor influence.
