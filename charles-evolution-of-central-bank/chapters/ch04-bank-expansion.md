# Chapter 4: Bank Expansion and Fluctuation

## Core Idea
Free banking systems, even when constrained by the Gold Standard and clearinghouse discipline, are inherently prone to procyclical expansion and contraction. Competitive pressures drive banks to expand lending during prosperous periods to maintain market share, while the same pressures cause them to contract sharply during crises. The historical evidence—from England's 1833-1842 cycle to the 1970s LDC lending boom—demonstrates that banking systems without a noncompetitive central authority tend toward boom-bust patterns. The advocates of free banking cite Scotland and Canada as counterexamples, but Goodhart shows these systems succeeded precisely because they were not truly independent: they relied on the Bank of England and New York/London money markets respectively as their ultimate sources of liquidity. The crucial insight is that the most difficult central banking function is not crisis lending (which even competitive banks can sometimes manage), but the willingness to restrict credit during quiet, prosperous periods—a discipline that conflicts directly with profit maximization.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Procyclical Banking Hypothesis**: The argument that competitive banking systems naturally amplify business cycles through credit expansion in booms and contraction in busts.
- **Real Bills Doctrine (Bankmassige Cover)**: The Banking School's claim that bank expansion is naturally limited if banks only lend against "real bills" (short-term, self-liquidating commercial paper).
- **The Conflict Between Profit and Responsibility**: The fundamental tension for a central commercial bank between maximizing earnings by lending during booms and holding reserves to meet potential crises.
- **Moral Hazard from Crisis Support**: The argument that successful crisis intervention encourages banks to hold smaller reserves in normal times, potentially increasing systemic fragility.
- **Branch Banking vs. Unit Banking**: The comparative analysis of how banking structure (oligopolistic branch systems like Scotland/Canada vs. fragmented unit banking like the U.S.) affects stability.

## Key Concepts
- **Banking School vs. Currency School**: The historical divide between those who believed bank note issue was naturally constrained by the real bills doctrine (Banking School) and those who believed only strict metallic reserve requirements could prevent overissue (Currency School).
- **Mountifort Longfield's Quantified Argument**: A key nineteenth-century critique of free banking showing that conservative banks would lose market share during booms and might not recover it during panics—a point that Vera Smith considered "by far the most important controversial point in the theory of free banking."
- **Financial "Mania"**: The phenomenon, observed in 1836 England and repeatedly throughout history, where deregulation or innovation creates surplus profit opportunities that lead to exaggerated financial expansions.
- **The Palmer Rule**: The Bank of England's early nineteenth-century practice of maintaining a fixed ratio between its bullion reserve and its note issue—a precursor to more systematic monetary management.
- **Lender of Last Resort (Bagehot's Principle)**: The doctrine that the central bank should lend freely during crises on good collateral at high rates. Goodhart notes this is only half the challenge.
- **Countercyclical Policy**: The need for a central bank to "lean into the wind"—restricting credit during booms and expanding it during busts—a function impossible for profit-maximizing competitors.

## Mental Models
- **The Boom-Bust Credit Cycle**: Competitive pressures create a ratchet effect. During booms, banks that refuse to expand lose market share to more aggressive competitors. During busts, all banks contract simultaneously, exacerbating the downturn. Only a noncompetitive institution can break this cycle.
- **The Illusion of Scottish/Canadian Independence**: These systems appeared to demonstrate free banking stability, but in reality they were satellites of London and New York. Their ultimate liquidity backstop was the Bank of England. When truly independent systems (like Switzerland before 1905) lost access to external support, they experienced crises that led directly to central bank creation.
- **The Quiet Period Problem**: The most difficult central banking challenge is not crisis lending but restraint during prosperity. A profit-maximizing bank cannot afford to hold idle reserves while competitors are earning high returns on all good paper. This is the core of Henry Thornton's analysis in Chapter VI of his *Enquiry*.
- **Commercial Banking Instincts vs. Central Banking Necessities**: Commercial bankers are trained to minimize default risk and maximize earning assets. Central bankers must do the opposite: lend on questionable paper during crises and refuse good paper during booms. These instincts are fundamentally incompatible.

## Anti-patterns
- **Citing Scotland and Canada as Free Banking Success Stories**: These cases are often used to prove free banking works, but they actually demonstrate the opposite—the systems relied on external central banking support. Their success was contingent, not independent.
- **Assuming the Real Bills Doctrine Provides Adequate Constraint**: The belief that lending only against short-term commercial paper naturally limits expansion ignores that the volume of "real bills" expands procyclically with trade and speculation.
- **Focusing Only on Crisis Management**: Many discussions of central banking focus on the lender-of-last-resort function during panics. This misses the more important and more difficult function of preventive restraint during normal times.
- **Blaming Legal Restrictions for All Instability**: Free banking advocates often attribute historical banking instability to legal restrictions (e.g., limitations on joint-stock banking). While these played a role, Goodhart argues competitive pressures themselves generate instability regardless of legal structure.

## Key Takeaways
1. Competitive banking systems tend to be procyclical at best, and directly responsible for severe fluctuations at worst. The 1833-1842 British experience, as analyzed by Matthews, shows banks expanding freely in booms and contracting sharply in busts.
2. The real bills doctrine does not provide a reliable constraint on expansion because the volume of eligible paper itself expands procyclically.
3. Scotland and Canada are poor evidence for free banking because their banking systems were ultimately dependent on London and New York liquidity. Swiss experience (1870, 1886 crises) shows what happens when such external support is withdrawn.
4. The most difficult central banking function is countercyclical restraint during prosperous periods, not crisis lending. A profit-maximizing competitive bank cannot perform this function because it must maximize earning assets while competitors are expanding.
5. The metamorphosis to a noncompetitive, non-profit-maximizing role is essential not just for crisis management but for the day-to-day discipline of the banking system. Without it, even centralized reserve systems will fail to prevent cycles.
6. Successful crisis intervention (e.g., clearinghouse emergency currency) creates moral hazard by encouraging smaller reserve holdings. Effective central banking requires combining crisis support with ongoing supervision and restrictive measures during expansions.
