# Chapter 7: Why Banks Need a Central Bank

## Core Idea

The conventional rationale for special Central Bank treatment of banks emphasizes their twin functions in providing both payments and portfolio management services. Goodhart challenges this view, arguing that payment services could be provided more safely by mutual collective investment intermediaries ("mutual fund banking") with market-value-varying liabilities. The true source of banking vulnerability—and hence the real reason banks need a Central Bank—lies not in their payment function but in the nature of their asset portfolios. Banks specialize in holding nonmarketable loans of uncertain true value, extended on fixed nominal value terms to borrowers whose project outcomes are costly to observe. This characteristic induces both banks and depositors to prefer fixed nominal value deposits with convertibility guarantees, makes the "true" market value of bank assets uncertain and not strictly calculable, and thereby raises the risk of runs while making bank supervision and Central Bank support both more necessary and more difficult.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Twin Functions Critique**: A critical examination of the conventional view that banks' joint provision of payment services and portfolio management explains their special fragility and need for Central Bank support.
- **Mutual Fund Banking Proposal**: The alternative vision of payment services provided by collective investment intermediaries whose liabilities fluctuate with underlying asset values, eliminating the mismatch between fixed nominal liabilities and uncertain asset values.
- **The Nonmarketable Asset Theory of Banking**: The argument that the defining characteristic of banking is the holding of nonmarketable (or nonmarketed) assets, which creates the fundamental conditions for runs, contagion, and the need for external support.
- **Fixed Nominal Value Contracting**: The framework explaining why both banks and depositors prefer fixed nominal value contracts—loans to borrowers and deposits from savers—due to information and monitoring costs.

## Key Concepts

- **Banks' Twin Functions**: Fama's description of banks as having two functions—providing transaction/accounting services and portfolio management. Goodhart argues that the first function is not unique to banks and does not, by itself, create special vulnerability.
- **Payment Services Without Banks**: The argument that transaction services could be provided by mutual collective investment intermediaries (unit trusts, open-end investment trusts) where liabilities represent proportional claims on marketable assets. Examples include Merrill Lynch cash management services and money market mutual funds.
- **100% Reserve-Backed Funds**: The Tobin/Friedman proposal that banks be required to hold segregated safe assets (e.g., Treasury securities) against checkable deposits. Goodhart argues this would largely remove the profitability of banking and would be subject to evasion unless the institution were already non-profit-maximizing.
- **Mark-to-Market Banking**: The Kaufman proposal that banks revalue assets daily at market prices and be forcibly closed when net capital value falls close to zero, enabling 100% deposit insurance. Goodhart suggests this would drive a shift toward mutual fund banking.
- **Nonmarketable Assets**: Loans to persons and small businesses where set-up costs for proper markets are formidable, public information is limited and expensive, and banks specialize in choosing borrowers and monitoring their behavior.
- **Fixed Nominal Value Loans**: Banks extend loans on fixed nominal value terms (irrespective of contingent outcome) supported by collateral, shorter duration than project life, and bankruptcy costs for defaulting borrowers—to reduce their own information costs and increase borrower incentives.
- **Information Asymmetry Between Bank and Borrower**: Even with their advantages over ordinary savers, banks remain at a comparative disadvantage compared with borrowers in assessing the latter's conditions, intentions, and prospects.
- **Hidden Reserves**: The practice of maintaining unreported reserves, justified by practical bankers as reducing the likelihood of runs and maintaining confidence—illustrating how better public information can sometimes be destabilizing when asset values are uncertain.
- **The True Market Value Problem**: Since no one actually knows the "true" value of nonmarketable loans, the impairment of a subset of such loans at one bank throws doubt on the position of other banks believed to have made similar loans, creating contagion risk even without payment function entanglement.

## Mental Models

- **The Bank as Information Intermediary**: Rather than viewing banks primarily as payment providers, see them as specialized information processors—entities that invest in relationships with borrowers to overcome information asymmetries that prevent direct market financing. This information specificity is both their core function and their core vulnerability.
- **The Fixed Value Trap**: The preference for fixed nominal value contracts (loans and deposits) is individually rational given information costs, but collectively creates systemic fragility. Each party seeks certainty, but the resulting structure is inherently unstable because the "true" asset values remain hidden and uncertain.
- **Mutual Fund Banking as Safer Alternative**: Imagine a payment system where your checking account balance fluctuates daily with the market value of a portfolio of Treasury securities. No bank runs, no contagion, no solvency risk—because the liability value adjusts automatically with asset value. The technology for this exists; historical path dependence keeps us in the more dangerous system.
- **The Non-Separability of Macro and Micro Functions**: In modern banking systems with contagious panic potential, the Central Bank's macro function (managing overall monetary conditions) and micro function (supporting individual institutions) cannot be cleanly separated. Concern for individual bank health inevitably affects systemic management, and vice versa.

## Anti-patterns

- **Attributing Banking Fragility to Payment Services**: The common error of assuming that banks' provision of transaction services is the primary source of their special vulnerability, when in fact payment services could be provided more safely by non-bank intermediaries.
- **Assuming Asset-Liability Segregation is Sustainable**: Believing that banks can be forced to maintain strict segregation of safe assets against payment deposits without creating incentives for evasion and regulatory arbitrage—unless the institution is already non-profit-maximizing.
- **Ignoring the Information Intensity of Bank Lending**: Treating bank loans as if they were simply less liquid versions of marketable securities, rather than recognizing the deep information relationships and monitoring functions that make them nonmarketable in the first place.
- **Expecting Market Discipline Alone**: Relying on market forces to discipline banks without recognizing that the opacity of nonmarketable loan portfolios makes market assessment of bank condition inherently difficult and slow.
- **Separating Macro and Micro Central Banking**: Advocating the separation of monetary policy management from bank supervision, which may be feasible only in banking systems with low contagion risk (e.g., historical Germany with high cash usage and high capital ratios) but not in modern deposit-based systems.

## Key Takeaways

1. The conventional rationale for special Central Bank treatment—the joint provision of payment and portfolio management services—is flawed; payment services could be provided more safely by mutual collective investment intermediaries.
2. If banks abandoned payment services and restricted liabilities to noncheckable form, it would not much reduce bank riskiness; they would still require Central Bank assistance.
3. The truly important distinction between banks and other financial intermediaries resides in the characteristics of their asset portfolios: banks hold nonmarketable loans of uncertain true value.
4. Banks and depositors both prefer fixed nominal value contracts because information and monitoring costs make contingent-outcome contracts impractical and because fixed values create better incentives for performance.
5. The combination of nominal convertibility guarantees with uncertainty about the true value of nonmarketable assets leads to the possibility of runs on individual banks and systemic crises.
6. Flows of funds between banks can have severely adverse effects on the economy even without any flight into cash, because they disrupt lending relationships and force borrowers to undergo expensive search costs for replacement funds.
7. A Central Bank is needed to prevent, or if necessary recycle, such flows—acting more swiftly and decisively than competing commercial banks, which face conflicts of interest and greater risk to their own profit positions.
8. All three major proposals for eliminating systemic risk (Tobin/Friedman 100% reserves, Kaufman mark-to-market, Goodhart's mutual fund banking) share the core requirement that payment-service providers should hold only marketable assets—confirming that nonmarketable assets are the root of banking instability.
