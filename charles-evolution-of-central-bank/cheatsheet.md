# Cheatsheet — The Evolution of Central Banks

## Core Arguments at a Glance

| Question | Free Banking View | Goodhart's View |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Why do central banks exist? | Government imposition | Natural evolution from market failures |
| What causes banking instability? | Government intervention | Structural market failures (information, externalities) |
| Can clearinghouses replace central banks? | Yes | No — limited by conflicts of interest and systemic crises |
| Can private insurance replace deposit guarantees? | Yes | No — information problems and resource inadequacy |
| Is bank fragility eliminable? | Yes, through competition | No — inherent in non-marketable assets |

## Decision Rules

### When Evaluating Banking Reform Proposals
```
Does the proposal address information asymmetry?
  ├─ No → Likely inadequate
  └─ Yes → Does it handle systemic crises?
      ├─ No → Will fail in extreme scenarios
      └─ Yes → Does it have enforcement power?
          ├─ No → Cannot ensure compliance
          └─ Yes → Viable alternative
```

### When Assessing Historical Evidence
```
Claim: "X was a successful free banking system"
  └─ Check:
      1. Did it have access to external liquidity? (e.g., London)
      2. How frequent were banking crises?
      3. Was there implicit government support?
      4. Did it truly lack a central bank function?
  └─ If any answer is "yes" → Not truly free banking
```

### When Designing Financial Regulation
```
Goal: Reduce banking fragility
  ├─ Option A: Better supervision
  │   └─ Effective but cannot eliminate all risk
  ├─ Option B: Separate payment and portfolio functions
  │   └─ Goodhart's preferred structural solution
  ├─ Option C: Higher capital requirements
  │   └─ Helps but banks find ways around
  └─ Option D: Central bank support
      └─ Necessary but creates moral hazard

Best approach: Combine B + C + D with careful calibration
```

## Trade-off Matrices

### Central Bank Support vs. Moral Hazard
| Approach | Stability | Moral Hazard | Feasibility |
|----------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| No support (pure free banking) | Low | None | Low (market failures persist) |
| Limited support (Bagehot) | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| Full support (modern central bank) | High | High | High |
| Pre-commit to no support | Medium | Low | Low (time inconsistency) |

**Goodhart's recommendation**: Accept some moral hazard as the price of stability; use supervision to limit risk-taking.

### Private vs. Public Solutions to Banking Problems
| Problem | Private Solution | Limitation | Public Solution |
|---------|-----------------|------------|-----------------|
| Information asymmetry | Clubs, rating agencies | Conflicts of interest, free-riding | Central bank supervision |
| Liquidity in crises | Clearinghouses, interbank markets | Collapse in panic | Lender of last resort |
| Deposit insurance | Private funds | Resource inadequacy, adverse selection | Government-backed insurance |
| Payment system | Private networks | Fragmentation, instability | Centralized settlement |

## Key Historical Cases

| Case | Period | Claimed Success? | Goodhart's Assessment |
|------|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| Scotland | 1715-1845 | Yes (free banking) | Had London liquidity access; note-issuing was limited |
| Canada | 1867-1935 | Yes (free banking) | Had implicit government support; few but severe crises |
| United States | 1836-1913 | No (crisis-prone) | No central bank; frequent panics |
| England | 1694+ | N/A | Bank of England evolved naturally from government finance needs |

## Tells & Smells

### Fragile Banking System Indicators
- Rapid credit growth relative to GDP
- Declining reserve ratios
- Rising asset prices fueled by credit
- Weakening lending standards
- Increasing reliance on short-term funding

### Inadequate Reform Proposals
- Assume perfect information
- Ignore systemic risk
- Rely solely on market discipline
- Neglect enforcement mechanisms
- Claim historical successes without examining details

### Sound Central Bank Design
- Independent from political pressure
- Independent from commercial banking interests
- Has supervisory authority
- Can provide liquidity in crises
- Has tools to limit moral hazard

## Policy Recommendations Summary

### What Goodhart Would Recommend
1. **Maintain central bank independence** — From both politics and commercial banking
2. **Strengthen supervision** — Information asymmetry requires active oversight
3. **Consider structural separation** — Mutual fund banks for payment services
4. **Accept lender of last resort** — Necessary given structural fragility
5. **Be realistic about fine-tuning** — Monetary policy has limits

### What Goodhart Would Oppose
1. **Abolishing central banks** — Market failures are real and persistent
2. **Relying solely on market discipline** — Information problems prevent this
3. **Assuming perfect regulation can prevent crises** — Fragility is structural
4. **Ignoring moral hazard** — But don't let it prevent necessary support

## Fundamental Equations

```
Banking Fragility = Non-marketable Assets + Fixed Nominal Deposits + Maturity Mismatch

Central Bank Necessity = Information Asymmetry + Systemic Risk + Externalities

Policy Trade-off = Stability ↑ ↔ Moral Hazard ↑
```
