# Chapter 13: Revival of the Over-the-Counter Market

## Core Idea
The secondary market for Treasury securities evolved from a bank-dominated, illiquid environment to an active over-the-counter market with dealer networks, price quotations, and institutional participation. This revival was driven by the scale of wartime issuance, the broadened investor base, and the natural trading activity generated by a larger stock of outstanding securities.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Liquidity as Self-Reinforcing**: More trading attracts more participants; more participants deepen liquidity. The wartime expansion created the critical mass for a liquid secondary market.
- **Dealer Networks as Market Infrastructure**: Brokers and dealers emerged as intermediaries, providing continuous price quotations and facilitating trades between dispersed investors.
- **The OTC vs. Exchange Distinction**: Treasury securities traded over-the-counter (dealer-to-dealer, dealer-to-customer) rather than on organised exchanges. This suited the heterogeneous, institutional nature of the market.

## Key Concepts
- **Over-the-counter (OTC) market**: Decentralised trading through dealers rather than on a central exchange
- **Dealer**: A market maker who buys and sells from inventory, providing liquidity
- **Broker**: An intermediary who matches buyers and sellers without taking inventory risk
- **Bid-ask spread**: The difference between dealer buying and selling prices; a measure of liquidity
- **Price quotations**: Published dealer quotes that provide transparency

## Mental Models
- Think of the OTC market as a network: its value increases with the number of connected participants (network effects)
- Use liquidity metrics (bid-ask spread, trading volume, quote frequency) to assess market health

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming exchanges are always superior**: OTC markets suit heterogeneous, institutional products; exchanges suit standardised, retail products
- **Ignoring the role of dealers**: They provide liquidity by bearing inventory risk, which requires capital and expertise

## Key Takeaways
1. The secondary market was moribund before the war; by the late 1920s it was active and liquid
2. Dealer networks emerged as the core market infrastructure
3. The broadened investor base created natural two-way flow
4. Price quotations became regular and reliable, reducing information asymmetry
5. The OTC structure suited the institutional, heterogeneous nature of Treasury securities
