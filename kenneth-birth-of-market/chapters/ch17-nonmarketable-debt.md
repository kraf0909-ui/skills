# Chapter 17: Nonmarketable Treasury Debt

## Core Idea
The Depression saw the creation of nonmarketable Treasury securities — primarily savings bonds — designed for retail investors who could not or would not participate in the marketable securities market. This was a new form of government borrowing that separated the investor base into wholesale (marketable) and retail (nonmarketable) segments.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Retail-Wholesale Separation**: Nonmarketable securities allowed the Treasury to tap small savers without exposing them to market risk. This broadened the investor base while protecting inexperienced investors from interest rate risk.
- **The Captive Retail Market**: Nonmarketable bonds could not be traded, so investors were "captive" until maturity or redemption. This reduced the Treasury's rollover risk but limited investor flexibility.
- **Savings Bonds as Fiscal Tool**: Series A through E savings bonds were marketed as patriotic, safe investments for ordinary Americans. They became a major source of wartime and post-war finance.

## Key Concepts
- **Savings bonds**: Nonmarketable, registered securities sold to individuals in small denominations
- **Series A (1935)**: The first savings bond; $25 denomination, 2.9% interest, 1-year maturity
- **Series E (1941)**: The iconic "war bond"; sold in denominations from $25 to $10,000
- **Accrual securities**: Interest accrues and is paid at maturity rather than as periodic coupons
- **Redemption**: Investors could redeem before maturity, typically with an interest penalty

## Mental Models
- Use nonmarketable securities when the goal is to reach small investors who would not buy marketable bonds
- Think of the retail-wholesale split as a segmentation strategy: different products for different customers

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming nonmarketable debt is costless**: It often carries higher administrative costs and may require higher effective yields to attract investors
- **Ignoring the liquidity trade-off**: Investors sacrifice liquidity for safety and convenience

## Key Takeaways
1. Nonmarketable Treasury securities were created during the Depression to reach small investors
2. Series A (1935) was the first savings bond; Series E (1941) became the iconic war bond
3. Savings bonds broadened the investor base but created a captive, non-tradable segment
4. The retail-wholesale separation became a permanent feature of Treasury debt management
5. Nonmarketable debt would become a major financing tool during WWII and beyond
