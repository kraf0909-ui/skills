# Chapter 22: The Brief Revival and Subsequent Extinction of National Bank Notes

## Core Idea
National bank notes — currency backed by Treasury bonds — experienced a brief revival during the Depression before being eliminated in 1935. This episode illustrates the path dependence of financial institutions: the note-bond link created in the 1860s persisted for 70 years despite changing economic conditions.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Path Dependence in Financial Institutions**: Once created, institutional structures persist because of vested interests, sunk costs, and complementary arrangements. National bank notes survived long after their original rationale faded.
- **The Note-Bond Collateral Link**: Banks had to hold Treasury bonds to issue notes. This created artificial demand for government securities but also tied currency to debt management.
- **Reform as Political Economy**: Eliminating national bank notes required overcoming the interests of banks that benefited from the note-issuing privilege.

## Key Concepts
- **National bank notes**: Currency issued by nationally chartered banks, backed by Treasury bonds deposited with the Comptroller of the Currency
- **Note collateral**: Treasury bonds that banks deposited to secure their note issuance
- **Banking Act of 1935**: Eliminated national bank notes and transferred note issuance authority to the Federal Reserve
- **Federal Reserve notes**: The replacement currency, backed by Federal Reserve assets rather than Treasury bonds

## Mental Models
- Think of institutional reform as requiring both economic rationale and political opportunity: the Depression provided the latter
- Use path dependence to explain why seemingly anachronistic institutions persist

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming inefficient institutions are quickly reformed**: They often persist because of vested interests
- **Ignoring the collateral demand effect**: National bank notes created artificial demand for Treasury bonds that disappeared after reform

## Key Takeaways
1. National bank notes were backed by Treasury bonds, creating a link between currency and debt
2. The note-bond collateral requirement created artificial demand for government securities
3. The Banking Act of 1935 eliminated national bank notes, transferring currency issuance to the Federal Reserve
4. The reform removed a distortion in the government securities market
5. The episode illustrates how crisis creates opportunities for institutional reform
