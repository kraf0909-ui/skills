# Chapter 3: Treasury Debt Management before World War I

## Core Idea
Before World War I, Treasury debt management was highly constrained. Congress specified the terms of each issue (maturity, coupon, price), leaving little discretion to the Secretary. The market was small, illiquid, and dominated by national banks holding bonds as collateral for note issuance.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Congressional Control of Debt Terms**: Unlike modern practice where Treasury has broad discretion, pre-war Congress micromanaged each issue's characteristics. This reflected a constitutional suspicion of executive financial power.
- **The Collateral-Driven Demand**: National banks had to hold Treasury bonds to back their note issuance. This created a captive, price-insensitive buyer — helpful for placement but damaging for price discovery and liquidity.
- **Bearer vs. Registered Bonds**: Bearer bonds were transferable by delivery (convenient but risky); registered bonds required transfer on the Treasury's books (secure but cumbersome).

## Key Concepts
- **War Revenue Act of 1898**: Authorised Spanish-American War bonds with Congress-specified terms: 20-year maturity, 3% coupon, par price
- **Bearer bonds**: Engraved certificates with detachable coupons; ownership transferred by physical delivery
- **Registered bonds**: Ownership recorded by the Treasury; transfers required official registration
- **Panama Canal bonds (1911)**: The last pre-war offering; six years passed before the next wartime sale

## Mental Models
- Think of pre-war debt management as "retail" not "wholesale": each issue was bespoke, negotiated with Congress, placed with a narrow clientele
- Use the collateral-demand model to understand why the market was illiquid: banks bought bonds to issue notes, not to trade

## Anti-patterns
- **Projecting modern Treasury discretion onto the past**: Pre-war Secretaries had far less flexibility than their modern successors
- **Assuming illiquidity was a failure**: It was a natural consequence of small scale, narrow ownership, and bespoke issuance

## Key Takeaways
1. In mid-1914, only six Treasury issues were outstanding, totaling $963 million
2. Congress specified maturity, coupon, and price for each issue; Treasury had minimal discretion
3. National banks held ~80% of the debt as collateral for note issuance
4. Bearer bonds dominated but registered bonds existed for investors wanting security
5. New offerings were rare; the market was illiquid and narrowly held
