# Chapter 9: Federal Reserve Support of the Treasury Market during World War I

## Core Idea
The Federal Reserve supported the government securities market during World War I by discounting bank loans collateralised by Treasury securities at preferential rates. This effectively pegged short-term rates and supported bond prices, subordinating monetary policy to fiscal needs — a pattern that would recur throughout the century.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Preferential Discount Rate**: The Fed charged lower rates on loans collateralised by government securities than on other loans. This created an incentive for banks to hold Treasuries and made government financing cheaper.
- **Rate Pegging as Fiscal Support**: By standing ready to discount government-secured paper at a fixed rate, the Fed effectively guaranteed a floor under bond prices and a ceiling on yields.
- **Monetary Policy Subordination**: The Fed's wartime role was explicitly to support government finance, not to pursue independent monetary objectives. This established a tension that would persist.

## Key Concepts
- **Preferential discount rate**: Lower Fed lending rate for loans secured by government securities
- **Section 13(8) of the Federal Reserve Act**: Authorised the Fed to discount government-secured paper
- **War Finance Corporation**: Government agency that lent to war industries, sometimes with Treasury support
- **Rate pegging**: Maintaining interest rates at fixed levels through central bank intervention

## Mental Models
- Think of the preferential rate as a subsidy to government borrowing: it reduced the Treasury's cost at the expense of monetary flexibility
- Use the subordination framework to understand the political economy of central banking in wartime

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming the Fed was independent during the war**: It was explicitly subordinated to Treasury needs
- **Ignoring the inflationary consequences**: Rate pegging and money creation contributed to post-war inflation

## Key Takeaways
1. The Fed discounted government-secured loans at preferential rates, typically 0.5–1% below standard rates
2. This created a strong incentive for banks to hold Treasury securities
3. The policy effectively pegged short-term rates and supported government bond prices
4. Monetary policy was explicitly subordinated to fiscal needs during the war
5. The precedent for Fed support of the government securities market was established
