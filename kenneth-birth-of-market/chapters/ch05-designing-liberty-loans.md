# Chapter 5: Designing the Liberty Loans

## Core Idea
The Liberty Loans were the largest subscription offerings in history. Their design — fixed-price, fixed-coupon, callable long-term bonds — reflected the Treasury's need to raise massive sums quickly while appealing to a broad investor base. The design choices (coupon, maturity, call provisions, tax status) had lasting consequences for the market.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Subscription Offering vs. Auction**: The Treasury chose fixed-price subscription sales over competitive auctions. This guaranteed the amount raised but sacrificed price discovery and potentially left money on the table.
- **The Call Provision as Risk Transfer**: Callable bonds gave the Treasury the right to redeem early if rates fell. This transferred refinancing risk to investors, who demanded higher coupons as compensation.
- **Tax Exemption as Marketing Tool**: Early Liberty Loans were exempt from federal income tax, making them attractive to wealthy investors. This was later modified as tax rates rose.

## Key Concepts
- **First Liberty Loan (May 1917)**: $2 billion at 3.5%, 25-year maturity, callable after 15 years, tax-exempt
- **Second Liberty Loan (Oct 1917)**: $3.8 billion at 4%, 25-year, callable after 10 years, partially tax-exempt
- **Third Liberty Loan (Apr 1918)**: $3 billion at 4.5%, 10-year, not callable, partially tax-exempt
- **Fourth Liberty Loan (Oct 1918)**: $6 billion at 4.25%, 20-year, callable after 15 years
- **Victory Liberty Loan (Apr 1919)**: $4.5 billion at 4.75%, 20-year, callable after 4 years

## Mental Models
- Use subscription offerings when certainty of proceeds matters more than price optimisation
- Think of call provisions as options: valuable to the issuer, costly to the investor, priced in the coupon

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming subscription sales were a mistake**: They were appropriate for the goal (maximum participation, guaranteed proceeds) even if auctions might have priced more efficiently
- **Ignoring the tax dimension**: Tax exemption was a critical design feature that shaped the investor base

## Key Takeaways
1. Five Liberty Loans raised roughly $17 billion in total (plus $8 billion in short-term certificates)
2. Coupons rose from 3.5% to 4.75% as market conditions and competition for funds intensified
3. Call provisions became standard, giving Treasury refinancing flexibility
4. Tax exemption attracted wealthy investors but reduced federal revenue
5. The fixed-price subscription model prioritized broad participation over price discovery
