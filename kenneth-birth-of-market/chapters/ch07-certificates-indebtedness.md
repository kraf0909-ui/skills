# Chapter 7: Treasury Cash Management: Certificates of Indebtedness

## Core Idea
Certificates of Indebtedness were short-term coupon-bearing securities (maturing in one year or less) that bridged the timing mismatch between government expenditures and tax receipts. They were a critical cash management tool during the war and established the precedent for modern Treasury bill issuance.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Cash Management as Distinct from Debt Management**: Short-term instruments serve liquidity needs, not long-term financing. This distinction is crucial for understanding why the Treasury issues bills even when it has no long-term borrowing need.
- **The Anticipatory Security**: Certificates were often issued "in anticipation of" bond sales or tax receipts. They allowed the Treasury to spend before revenues arrived.
- **The Yield Curve as Policy Space**: By issuing at multiple maturities, the Treasury could shape the yield curve and manage rollover risk.

## Key Concepts
- **Certificates of Indebtedness**: Short-term coupon securities, typically 3–12 months, issued at par
- **Tax anticipation certificates**: Issued before tax receipts arrived, redeemed when taxes were collected
- **Bond anticipation certificates**: Issued before long-term bond sales, redeemed with proceeds
- **Rollover risk**: The risk that short-term debt cannot be refinanced at acceptable rates

## Mental Models
- Think of certificates as the Treasury's "credit card": they smooth cash flow timing mismatches
- Use the maturity structure to assess risk: heavy reliance on short-term debt creates rollover vulnerability

## Anti-patterns
- **Conflating cash management with deficit finance**: Certificates served liquidity needs even when the budget was balanced
- **Ignoring rollover risk**: Short-term debt is cheaper but requires continuous market access

## Key Takeaways
1. Certificates of Indebtedness were the primary short-term instrument before Treasury bills
2. They were typically issued in anticipation of tax receipts or bond sales
3. About $8 billion in certificates were outstanding at the war's peak
4. They established the precedent for regular short-term issuance
5. The transition from certificates to bills (1929) was an important innovation in market structure
