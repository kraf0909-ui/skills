# Chapter 14: Evolution of the Primary Market and the Introduction of Treasury Bills

## Core Idea
The primary market evolved from bespoke, congressional-controlled issuance to regular, predictable auctions. The introduction of Treasury bills in 1929 was a landmark innovation: the first regular auction of short-term government securities, establishing the auction as the standard primary market mechanism and creating the modern bill market.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Regular and Predictable Issuance**: The Treasury began announcing issuance calendars in advance, allowing market participants to plan. This reduced uncertainty, broadened participation, and lowered borrowing costs.
- **The Auction as Price Discovery**: Competitive bidding replaced fixed-price subscription for short-term securities. Auctions reveal market demand rather than imposing a price, improving efficiency.
- **The Bill Market as Benchmark**: Treasury bills became the risk-free short-term benchmark, pricing reference for money markets, and foundation for yield curve construction.

## Key Concepts
- **Treasury bills (1929)**: Short-term discount securities (originally 3-month, later 6-month and 1-year) sold by competitive auction
- **Competitive auction**: Bidders submit yield/price bids; securities allocated to highest bids until the offering is exhausted
- **Noncompetitive bidding**: Small bidders can submit quantity-only bids, receiving the weighted average yield
- **Regular issuance**: Weekly 3-month bills, later supplemented by longer maturities
- **Discount basis**: Bills are issued at a discount to face value and redeemed at par; the difference is the interest

## Mental Models
- Use auctions when price discovery matters and the market is deep enough to produce competitive bidding
- Think of regular issuance as a commitment device: it builds credibility and reduces uncertainty premiums

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming auctions are always superior to subscriptions**: Subscriptions may be better when broad participation or guaranteed proceeds are the priority
- **Ignoring the transition costs**: Moving from subscription to auction required market education and infrastructure development

## Key Takeaways
1. Treasury bills were introduced in 1929 as the first regular auction instrument
2. The auction replaced fixed-price subscription for short-term securities
3. Regular weekly issuance became a foundational principle
4. Bills became the risk-free short-term benchmark for the money market
5. The success of bill auctions established the template for later auction offerings
