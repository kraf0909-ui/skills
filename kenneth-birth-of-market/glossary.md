# Glossary

**Auction** — A competitive bidding process for selling securities; the Treasury introduced competitive auctions for bills in 1929. (Ch 14, Ch 20)

**Bearer bond** — A bond transferable by physical delivery of the certificate; convenient but risky. (Ch 3)

**Bid-ask spread** — The difference between a dealer's buying and selling prices; a measure of market liquidity. (Ch 13)

**Book-entry settlement** — Electronic transfer of securities ownership without physical certificates; introduced 1968. (Ch 1, Ch 24)

**Broker** — An intermediary who matches buyers and sellers without taking inventory risk. (Ch 13)

**Call provision** — The issuer's right to redeem a bond before maturity; standard on Liberty Loans. (Ch 5)

**Certificate of Indebtedness** — Short-term coupon-bearing security (≤1 year) used for cash management before Treasury bills. (Ch 7)

**Clearinghouse** — A local association of banks that nets interbank payments. (Ch 2)

**Competitive auction** — Bidders submit yield/price bids; securities allocated to highest bids. (Ch 14)

**Consol** — A perpetual bond with no maturity date; not used by the US Treasury but relevant for comparison. (Ch 3)

**Correspondent banking** — Banks holding deposits at other banks (usually in money centers) to facilitate distant payments. (Ch 2)

**Dealer** — A market maker who buys and sells from inventory, providing liquidity. (Ch 13)

**Debt ceiling** — Statutory limit on total federal debt; created by the Second Liberty Bond Act (1917). (Ch 21)

**Deflation** — A decline in the general price level; increased the real burden of debt during the Depression. (Ch 18)

**Discount basis** — Securities issued at a discount to face value and redeemed at par; the difference is the interest. (Ch 14)

**Federal Reserve Act (1913)** — Created the Federal Reserve System to provide an elastic currency and improve payments. (Ch 2)

**Gold standard** — Monetary system linking currency to gold at a fixed price; abandoned by the US in 1933. (Ch 2, Ch 18)

**Liberty Loan** — One of five major bond issues (1917–1919) that financed US participation in WWI. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Liquidity** — The ease with which an asset can be bought or sold without affecting its price. (Ch 13)

**Marketable security** — A security that can be freely traded in secondary markets. (Ch 1, Ch 17)

**Maturity extension** — Issuing longer-term debt to reduce rollover frequency and lock in rates. (Ch 19)

**National bank note** — Currency issued by nationally chartered banks, backed by Treasury bonds; eliminated 1935. (Ch 2, Ch 22)

**Noncompetitive bid** — A quantity-only bid in a Treasury auction, receiving the weighted average yield. (Ch 14)

**Nonmarketable security** — A security that cannot be traded in secondary markets (e.g., savings bonds). (Ch 17)

**Open market repurchase** — The Treasury buying back its own bonds in the secondary market. (Ch 12)

**OTC (over-the-counter) market** — Decentralised trading through dealers rather than on a central exchange. (Ch 13)

**Preferential discount rate** — Lower Fed lending rate for loans secured by government securities. (Ch 9)

**Primary market** — The market for new securities issuance. (Ch 1, Ch 14, Ch 20)

**Primary dealer** — A designated dealer obligated to bid at Treasury auctions and make markets. (Ch 20)

**Rate pegging** — Maintaining interest rates at fixed levels through central bank intervention. (Ch 9, Ch 19)

**Refunding** — Issuing new bonds to repay maturing or callable bonds. (Ch 12, Ch 19)

**Registered bond** — A bond whose ownership is recorded by the issuer; transfers require official registration. (Ch 3)

**Regular and predictable issuance** — Announcing issuance calendars in advance to reduce uncertainty. (Ch 14)

**Repurchase operation** — Treasury purchases of outstanding bonds in the open market. (Ch 12)

**Rollover risk** — The risk that short-term debt cannot be refinanced at acceptable rates. (Ch 7, Ch 18)

**Savings bond** — Nonmarketable, registered security sold to individuals in small denominations. (Ch 17)

**Secondary market** — The market for resale of previously issued securities. (Ch 1, Ch 13)

**Second Liberty Bond Act (1917)** — Authorised aggregate debt subject to a ceiling; delegated discretion to Treasury. (Ch 21)

**Sinking fund** — Statutory requirement to retire specified amounts of debt annually. (Ch 12)

**Subscription offering** — Securities sold at a fixed price; if oversubscribed, allocation is prorated. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Syndicate underwriting** — A group of banks jointly purchases an issue and resells to investors. (Ch 20)

**Tax anticipation certificate** — Short-term security issued before tax receipts arrive. (Ch 7)

**Treasury bill** — Short-term discount security (≤1 year) sold by competitive auction; introduced 1929. (Ch 14)

**Treasury Tax and Loan (TT&L)** — System for placing Treasury funds at commercial banks; evolved from War Loan Deposit Accounts. (Ch 8)

**Victory Liberty Loan** — The final post-war bond issue (1919); $4.5 billion at 4.75%. (Ch 5)

**War Loan Deposit Account** — Interest-bearing Treasury deposit at commercial banks; precursor to TT&L. (Ch 8)

**War Revenue Act** — Sharp tax increases during WWI to finance part of war costs. (Ch 4)
