# Cheatsheet

## Decision Rules

### When to use subscription offerings vs. auctions?
- **Subscription**: Broad participation is the priority, guaranteed proceeds are essential, or market conditions are too uncertain for competitive bidding
- **Auction**: Price discovery matters, the market is deep enough for competitive bidding, and proceeds uncertainty is acceptable
- **Hybrid**: Use noncompetitive bidding within auctions to accommodate small investors

### When to issue short-term vs. long-term debt?
- **Short-term (bills, certificates)**: Cash management, liquidity provision, yield curve construction, or when long-term rates are unusually high
- **Long-term (bonds, notes)**: Lock in rates, reduce rollover risk, match asset duration, or when the yield curve is flat/inverted
- **Barbell strategy**: Issue both short and long, minimising intermediate maturities

### When to refinance existing debt?
- **Refinance when**: Market rates are 1.5%+ below existing coupons, the issue is callable or maturing, and market conditions are stable
- **Do not refinance when**: Rates are near or above existing coupons, the issue has non-callable protection, or market volatility is high

### When to extend maturities?
- **Extend when**: Rollover risk is elevated, the yield curve is flat, or policy goals favour rate stability over cost minimisation
- **Shorten when**: The yield curve is steeply upward-sloping and rollover risk is low

## Decision Tree: Choosing a Primary Market Mechanism

```
Is broad retail participation the primary goal?
  YES → Subscription offering or savings bonds
  NO → Is price discovery the priority?
          YES → Competitive auction
          NO → Is certainty of proceeds essential?
                    YES → Syndicate underwriting
                    NO → Negotiated sale or private placement
```

## Trade-off Matrix: Debt Management Strategies

| Strategy | Cost | Risk | Flexibility | Best For |
|----------|------|------|-------------|----------|
| Short-term bills | Lower (usually) | High (rollover) | High | Cash management, liquidity |
| Long-term bonds | Higher (usually) | Low (rate locked) | Low | Rate certainty, liability matching |
| Callable bonds | Medium | Medium (to issuer) | High | Expecting rate declines |
| Index-linked | Variable | Inflation transfer | Medium | Inflation hedging |
| Nonmarketable | Higher admin | Low (captive) | Low | Retail access, stability |

## Thresholds & Defaults

- **Pre-war debt (1914)**: ~$1 billion across 6 issues
- **WWI peak debt (1919)**: ~$25 billion
- **1920s low (1930)**: ~$16 billion
- **Depression peak (1939)**: ~$40 billion
- **Liberty Loan coupons**: 3.5% → 4.75% across five issues
- **Bill introduction**: 1929, 3-month maturity, competitive auction
- **National bank note elimination**: 1935
- **Gold standard abandonment**: 1933
- **Debt ceiling creation**: 1917 (Second Liberty Bond Act)
- **Modern market pillars**: 3 of 4 trace to 1917–1939; book-entry added 1968

## Tells & Smells

- **If debt is rising while GDP is falling**: deflation may be amplifying the real burden
- **If short-term rates are pegged below market**: the central bank is subordinating monetary policy to fiscal needs
- **If Congress specifies individual issue terms**: Treasury discretion is constrained, reducing operational flexibility
- **If banks hold most government debt**: the investor base is narrow and potentially captive
- **If new issuance is irregular and unpredictable**: market participation will be limited and borrowing costs higher
- **If nonmarketable debt is growing**: the Treasury is prioritising retail access or captive funding over market liquidity
- **If call provisions are standard**: the Treasury expects rates to fall or wants refinancing flexibility
- **If foreign holdings are rising**: the debt is becoming more vulnerable to external sentiment shifts

## Quick Reference: Treasury Market Evolution

| Period | Debt Level | Key Innovation | Market Character |
|--------|-----------|----------------|------------------|
| Pre-1914 | ~$1bn | Congressional control | Illiquid, bank-dominated |
| WWI | $1bn → $25bn | Liberty Loans, Fed support | Mass expansion, patriotic marketing |
| 1920s | $25bn → $16bn | Bills, auctions, regular issuance | Sophistication in shrinkage |
| Depression | $16bn → $40bn | Savings bonds, active refinancing | Adaptation to crisis |
| Post-1939 | Continued growth | Book-entry, primary dealers | Modern market maturity |
