## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. The Debt/GDP Ratio as the Primary Diagnostic
Use the Debt/GDP ratio, not nominal debt, to assess sustainability. A £1.7 trillion debt in 2017 was 89% of GDP — historically manageable, though the trajectory matters more than the level. Britain has twice experienced peaks above 240% (post-1815, post-1945) and grown out of both.

### 2. The Paradox of Debt: Borrowing Is Easiest When Least Necessary
Governments can borrow most cheaply and repay most easily when the economy is strong and confidence is high. Conversely, crises force borrowing when rates are high and repayment capacity is low. This asymmetry shapes the entire history of British public debt.

### 3. War as the Dominant Driver of Debt Peaks
Britain’s two great debt peaks (1815, 1945) were both war-driven. War creates an unavoidable mismatch between immediate expenditure needs and current tax capacity. The debt is the mechanism for deferring this burden across time — and, through bond markets, across social classes.

### 4. Growth, Not Repayment, Is the Normal Exit Route
Actual repayment of debt is rare and difficult. The two post-war debt reductions were achieved primarily through sustained economic growth, which shrinks the Debt/GDP ratio via the compound arithmetic of the denominator. Sinking funds and fiscal tightening have historically been less effective and more politically vulnerable.

### 5. The Intergenerational Transfer Framework
National debt is a claim by one group of citizens on another — or by current generations on future ones. Domestically held debt is a redistribution, not a net loss. Foreign-held debt (about 25% of UK gilts by 2022) creates a genuine net outflow. The key ethical question is who bears the burden: taxpayers or bondholders, present or future.

### 6. The Off-Balance-Sheet Temptation
Governments repeatedly seek to hide liabilities outside the formal National Debt — from the South Sea Company’s creative accounting to modern PFI schemes and unfunded public-sector pensions. This undermines transparency, raises costs, and damages credibility.

### 7. The Sinking Fund Illusion
Pitt’s Sinking Fund (1786) was the most celebrated repayment scheme in British history. Its fatal flaw: operating a sinking fund while still net borrowing is logically self-defeating — the government borrows at higher rates than it earns on the fund. The fund also proved politically vulnerable to diversion.

### 8. Conversion and Refinancing as Debt Management Tools
When market rates fall below a bond’s coupon, governments can redeem and reissue at lower rates. Conversion is not default if bondholders are offered cash alternatives. Britain used this repeatedly (notably 1888, 1932). Anti-conversion clauses and timing are critical to success.

### 9. The r–g Sustainability Condition
Debt is sustainable when the real interest rate (r) is below the real growth rate (g). If r > g, even a primary surplus may not prevent explosive debt dynamics. The UK post-2008 benefited from r < g for a decade; the 2020s saw this reverse as rates rose and growth stagnated.

### 10. Fiscal Rules: Credibility vs. Flexibility
From Pitt’s Sinking Fund to Gordon Brown’s Golden Rule to the Sunak/Reeves frameworks, fiscal rules attempt to constrain political temptation. All rules are vulnerable to gaming, crisis override, and redefinition. Slater’s preference: strengthened independent oversight (OBR model) over rigid numerical targets.

### 11. The PFI Trap: Privatising Finance, Not Management
Private Finance Initiative projects transferred investment off the public balance sheet at the cost of much higher financing rates. The government’s borrowing advantage (risk-spreading, lower rates) was sacrificed for accounting presentation. The true cost only emerged in Whole of Government Accounts.

### 12. Index-Linked Bonds: A Double-Edged Instrument
Index-linked gilts protect investors against inflation but expose the government to unexpected inflation spikes. The UK has an unusually high proportion of index-linked debt. When post-Covid inflation surged, the interest cost jumped disproportionately.

### 13. Quantitative Easing as Debt Duration Reduction
QE transferred long-dated gilts from commercial banks to the Bank of England, replacing them with instantly rate-sensitive central bank reserves. This halved the effective average maturity of UK debt — a vulnerability exposed when rates rose after 2022.
