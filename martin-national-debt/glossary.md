# Glossary

**American Loan** — US government lending to Britain during/after WWI and WWII; the interwar loan was defaulted in 1932. (Ch 7, Ch 8)

**Annuity** — A contract paying fixed annual income in return for capital; may be for a term, a life, or perpetual. (Ch 3, App I)

**Bank of England** — Founded 1694 to lend £1.2 million to government; became the central bank and linchpin of public credit. (Ch 3)

**Barro's Ricardian Equivalence** — The theory that government bonds are not net wealth because taxpayers anticipate future taxes to repay them. Slater is sceptical of its practical relevance. (Ch 10)

**Bearer bond** — A bond transferable by physical delivery; most modern bonds are registered, not bearer. (App I)

**Bond** — A government promise to repay borrowed money with interest; the term is now used generically for government debt securities. (App I)

**Consols (Consolidated Annuities)** — Perpetual British government bonds with no maturity date; the archetypal gilt. (Ch 3, App I)

**Conversion** — Exchanging existing bonds for new ones with different terms, usually lower coupons; a standard debt management tool. (Ch 5, Ch 8, App I)

**Coupon** — The fixed annual interest payment on a bond. (App I)

**Debt/GDP ratio** — The standard measure of debt burden: nominal debt divided by annual GDP. (Ch 1, Ch 12)

**Financial repression** — A regime of controlled interest rates, captive buyers, and inflation that transfers wealth from creditors to debtors. (Ch 10)

**Fiscal Sustainability Report (FSR)** — The OBR's 50-year projection of public finances under unchanged policy. (Ch 13, Ch 15)

**Fully-funded pension** — A pension system with accumulated assets covering liabilities. (App II)

**Gilts** — British government bonds, named for the gilt-edged paper of early certificates. (App I)

**Gold Standard** — A monetary system linking currency to gold; Britain left it in 1931 and did not return after WWII. (Ch 8)

**Index-linked bond** — A bond whose coupon and principal are adjusted for inflation. (Ch 15, App I)

**Liability-Driven Investment (LDI)** — Derivatives-based strategies used by pension funds to hedge interest rate risk; the 2022 gilt crisis exposed their dangers. (Ch 15)

**National Debt** — The total outstanding borrowing of the UK central government; officially measured as Public Sector Net Debt excluding public-sector banks. (Ch 1)

**Pay-as-you-go (PAYG)** — A pension system where current contributions pay current benefits, without accumulated assets. (App II)

**Perpetuity** — A bond with no maturity date, paying interest forever. (App I)

**PFI (Private Finance Initiative)** — Off-balance-sheet infrastructure finance using private capital at higher cost than public borrowing. (Ch 13)

**Primary balance** — Government revenue minus non-interest expenditure; the key variable in debt sustainability. (Ch 12)

**Public Sector Net Debt (PSND)** — The official UK measure of government debt. (Ch 1)

**Quantitative Easing (QE)** — Central bank purchases of government bonds to increase money supply and lower rates. (Ch 10, Ch 13, Ch 15)

**Quantitative Tightening (QT)** — The reverse of QE: central bank sales of bonds back to the market. (Ch 15)

**r–g (interest rate minus growth rate)** — The critical determinant of debt dynamics: when r > g, debt tends to explode without primary surpluses. (Ch 12, Ch 15)

**Ricardian Equivalence** — See Barro's Ricardian Equivalence. (Ch 10)

**Sinking fund** — A dedicated revenue stream for debt repayment; Pitt's (1786) was the most famous but logically flawed when net borrowing continued. (Ch 5)

**South Sea Bubble (1720)** — A debt-equity conversion scheme that collapsed, damaging public confidence in financial innovation. (Ch 4)

**Stock** — In British usage, registered government debt without physical certificates (e.g. Consols). (App I)

**Tontine** — A group annuity where payments are divided among survivors; popular in France, rarely used in Britain. (Ch 3, App I)

**Treasury Bills** — Short-term government borrowing (1–12 months), issued at discount. (App I)

**War Loan** — The massive bond issues of WWI, notably the 5% War Loan of 1917, converted to 3.5% in 1932. (Ch 7, Ch 8)

**Ways and Means Advances** — Short-term borrowing from the Bank of England to smooth government cash flows. (Ch 7)

**Whole of Government Accounts (WGA)** — Corporate-style accounts including assets and broader liabilities beyond the National Debt. (Ch 13)
