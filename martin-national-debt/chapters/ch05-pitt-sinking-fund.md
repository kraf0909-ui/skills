# Chapter 5: Pitt's Sinking Fund and the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars

## Core Idea
The French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars (1793–1815) drove British debt to its highest-ever level relative to GDP (~240%). Pitt the Younger's Sinking Fund (1786) was the most celebrated attempt at systematic debt repayment, but its logical and political flaws became apparent under the pressure of massive wartime borrowing.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Sinking Fund Paradox**: A government cannot simultaneously borrow and run a sinking fund productively. If it borrows at 5% and invests the fund at 3%, it loses money on the spread. The fund only makes sense when net borrowing has ceased.
- **Conversion as Debt Management**: When market rates fall, governments can offer to exchange high-coupon bonds for new low-coupon bonds, reducing interest costs without nominal default.
- **The Debt Peak of 1815**: At ~240% of GDP, this was the highest British debt ratio ever. It was absorbed not by repayment but by a century of economic growth.

## Key Concepts
- **Pitt's Sinking Fund (1786)**: £1 million annually dedicated to debt repayment, administered by independent commissioners
- **Conversion (1888 Goschen conversion)**: The landmark exchange of 3% Consols for new lower-rate stock, though this occurred later
- **War finance**: Heavy reliance on long-term borrowing rather than taxation, reflecting political constraints on tax rises
- **The Peace of Amiens (1802)**: Brief interlude that demonstrated how quickly debt stabilisation could occur when war ended

## Mental Models
- Use "sinking fund" only when the government is running a genuine primary surplus; otherwise it is a costly illusion
- Think of conversion as refinancing: it works when markets are calm and the government has credibility

## Anti-patterns
- **Believing sinking funds guarantee repayment**: They are politically and logically vulnerable
- **Ignoring the opportunity cost**: Money in a sinking fund is money not available for other purposes

## Key Takeaways
1. Pitt's Sinking Fund was rigorous in design but flawed in conception for a government still borrowing
2. The Napoleonic Wars added roughly £600 million to the debt, taking it to about 240% of GDP
3. The debt was never repaid in nominal terms; it was "grown out of" over the nineteenth century
4. Conversion operations required careful market timing and "grooming" of bondholders
5. The post-1815 debt structure, with its mass of perpetual Consols, shaped British finance for a century
