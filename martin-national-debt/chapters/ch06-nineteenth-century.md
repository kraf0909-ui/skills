# Chapter 6: The Nineteenth Century: A Century of Peace

## Core Idea
The nineteenth century demonstrated that sustained economic growth, not fiscal austerity, is the most effective debt-reduction strategy. Britain's debt/GDP ratio fell from ~240% (1815) to ~25% (1914) primarily because GDP grew much faster than debt, despite only modest nominal repayment.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Growth as the Default Debt Strategy**: When g > r (growth exceeds real interest rate), the debt/GDP ratio falls automatically. This was the secret of Victorian debt reduction.
- **The Victorian Repayment Episode (1875–1914)**: The only sustained period of genuine nominal repayment in British history, driven by Gladstonian moralism and administrative structures. Even this would have been more modest without the Boer War interruption.
- **The Paradox of Easy Repayment**: It is easiest to repay debt when it is least necessary — when the economy is strong and the debt burden already light.

## Key Concepts
- **Gladstone and debt**: His moral aversion to debt shaped policy, though his own Egyptian debt holdings revealed complexities
- **Sinking funds of the late nineteenth century**: More successful than Pitt's because they began after net borrowing ceased
- **The Boer War (1899–1902)**: Interrupted repayment and added new debt
- **National Debt Commissioners**: Still-existing body originally set up to administer sinking funds

## Mental Models
- Use growth projections, not fiscal tightening plans, as the primary debt sustainability tool
- Think of nominal repayment as a luxury good: societies buy it only when they can easily afford it

## Anti-patterns
- **Advocating austerity as the primary debt tool**: History shows growth is more effective and less politically damaging
- **Ignoring the distributional effects**: Debt repayment through taxation has different effects than reduction through growth

## Key Takeaways
1. The debt/GDP ratio fell from ~240% to ~25% over the century, but nominal debt only fell from ~£800m to ~£600m
2. The difference was GDP growth: the denominator expanded dramatically while the numerator was nearly stable
3. The 1875–1914 period saw genuine repayment of nearly £300 million, but this was exceptional
4. Gladstone's moral commitment to debt reduction was politically powerful but not always economically optimal
5. The National Debt Office and Post Office Savings Bank channelled small savings into government bonds
