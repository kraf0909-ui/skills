# Appendix I: Bond Technicalities

## Core Idea
Government bonds have evolved from complex, bespoke instruments (annuities, tontines, lottery loans) to standardised securities, but their fundamental mechanics — pricing, yield, duration, conversion — remain essential to understanding debt management.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Bond Pricing Inverse Rule**: Bond prices move inversely with interest rates. A 3% Consol priced at £60 yields 5%; at £30 yields 10%.
- **Yield vs. Price Thinking**: Bond professionals think in prices and yields, not rates. "Bonds going down" means yields (and expected rates) going up.
- **Duration and Sensitivity**: Longer bonds are more sensitive to interest rate changes. Perpetuities are most sensitive; bonds approaching maturity converge to par regardless of rate changes.

## Key Concepts
- **Coupon**: The fixed annual interest payment on a bond
- **Par/Under par**: Issuing below face value to raise effective yield without raising nominal coupon
- **Conversion**: Exchanging old bonds for new at different rates; requires credible redemption threat
- **Index-linked bonds**: Coupon and principal indexed to inflation (RPI/CPI)
- **Gilts**: Government bonds, named for the gilt-edged certificates originally used
- **Treasury Bills**: Short-term (1–12 month) government borrowing, issued at discount
- **Consols**: Perpetual bonds with no maturity date; the archetypal British gilt

## Mental Models
- Use bond price sensitivity to assess vulnerability: a long-duration portfolio loses most when rates rise
- Think of conversion as refinancing: it saves interest but may increase nominal debt if sweeteners are offered

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing nominal and effective rates**: A 3% bond issued under par at £50 has an effective yield of 6%
- **Ignoring redemption options**: Most "perpetuals" can be redeemed at government option, making them less safe than they appear

## Key Takeaways
1. Bond prices vary inversely with market interest rates
2. Perpetuities are most rate-sensitive; short bonds least
3. Conversion is a standard debt management tool, not default
4. Index-linked bonds protect investors but expose governments to inflation risk
5. The UK's index-linked proportion is unusually high by international standards
