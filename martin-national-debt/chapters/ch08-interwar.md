# Chapter 8: The Inter-War Years

## Core Idea
The interwar period was dominated by the attempt to manage the First World War's debt legacy. The 1932 conversion operation — reducing the coupon on War Loan from 5% to 3.5% — was a landmark success, but the period also saw Britain's only modern default (on the US loan) and the intellectual ferment that would produce Keynesian economics.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Conversion Operation**: When market rates have fallen far below a bond's coupon, governments can offer to exchange old bonds for new. Success depends on credible threat of redemption, careful market timing, and offering a cash alternative.
- **The 1932 War Loan Conversion**: The largest single debt management operation in British history. £2 billion of 5% War Loan exchanged for 3.5% stock, saving massive interest costs.
- **The US Loan Default (1932)**: Britain suspended payments on its US war debt, arguing that German reparations (which funded the payments) had been suspended. The US never accepted this logic.

## Key Concepts
- **The Colwyn Committee (1927)**: Investigated National Debt and taxation; its report shaped interwar thinking
- **The Treasury View**: The orthodox position that public works could not reduce unemployment because they would crowd out private investment
- **Keynes's challenge**: The emerging critique of Treasury orthodoxy, though not yet fully formed into the General Theory
- **The Gold Standard (1925)**: Britain's return to gold at the pre-war parity, overvaluing sterling and constraining macroeconomic policy

## Mental Models
- Use conversion when r_market << r_coupon and the government has credible redemption rights
- Think of the interwar period as a laboratory: different countries tried different approaches to similar debt problems

## Anti-patterns
- **Calling the 1932 conversion a "default"**: It was a legitimate exercise of contractual redemption rights, with a cash alternative offered
- **Ignoring the macroeconomic context**: The conversion succeeded because interest rates were exceptionally low, not because of administrative brilliance alone

## Key Takeaways
1. The 1932 conversion saved roughly £30 million annually in interest — about 1% of GDP
2. The conversion was accepted by 99%+ of bondholders, demonstrating the power of credible government action
3. The US loan default was politically damaging but economically unavoidable given the broader European situation
4. The Treasury View dominated policy but was intellectually vulnerable; Keynes was developing his critique
5. The interwar experience shaped post-1945 attitudes to debt management and macroeconomic policy
