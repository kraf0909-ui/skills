# Chapter 14: Conclusions

## Core Idea
History offers five key lessons for thinking about the National Debt: (1) the current level is historically manageable but the trajectory is concerning; (2) growth, not repayment, is the normal exit from debt peaks; (3) hidden liabilities are growing faster than headline debt; (4) the debt's mechanical security depends on maintaining credibility and favourable characteristics; (5) existing debt reduces flexibility to respond to future crises.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Five Historical Lessons**:
  1. Current debt is below historical peaks but rising in peacetime
  2. Britain's historical context has been relatively favourable — this is not guaranteed
  3. Official figures exclude rapidly growing other commitments
  4. The OBR's long-term projections are very challenging
  5. Private-sector debt is comparable in magnitude and also rising
- **The "Mending the Roof" Principle**: In peacetime, governments should reduce debt to rebuild borrowing capacity for future crises.
- **The Credibility Paradox**: Debt sustainability depends on the state's credibility, which depends on its perceived integrity and control of tax revenue — both of which have become more questionable.

## Key Concepts
- **Structural deficit**: The peacetime gap between expenditure and revenue, distinct from cyclical or crisis borrowing
- **Debt as crisis insurance**: The ability to borrow in emergencies (2008, Covid) is valuable and must be preserved
- **The off-balance-sheet temptation**: Governments repeatedly hide liabilities; this undermines rational decision-making

## Mental Models
- Use history as a guide to possibilities, not predictions: past performance does not guarantee future resilience
- Think of debt as a strategic reserve: it is valuable to have capacity, costly to have used it

## Anti-patterns
- **Panic**: The debt is not an immediate existential threat
- **Complacency**: The favourable characteristics that buffered the debt can erode quickly
- **Austerity as the only tool**: Growth and efficiency improvements are less painful alternatives

## Key Takeaways
1. Actual repayment of debt is rare and difficult; growth is the historical norm for debt reduction
2. The Victorian repayment episode (1875–1914) was exceptional and required strong political commitment plus a robust economy
3. The debt's mechanical security in 2017 was good: sterling denomination, long maturity, no gold standard
4. Low interest rates would not last forever; when they rose, finances would look more vulnerable
5. Brexit represented a shock of potentially similar magnitude to 2008, with unclear implications
6. The hidden cost of debt is reduced flexibility to respond to future bad luck
