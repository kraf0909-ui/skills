# Chapter 11: National Debt and the 'United' Kingdom?

## Core Idea
The "British" National Debt has not always been uniformly British. The Acts of Union with Scotland (1707) and Ireland (1801) brought different pre-existing debts into a unified structure. More recently, devolution raises questions about how debt would be allocated in the event of Scottish independence or Irish reunification.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Territorial Debt Problem**: When political unions dissolve, how are shared debts allocated? There is no universally accepted formula.
- **The Scottish Debt Share**: In the 2014 independence referendum, estimates of Scotland's share of UK debt ranged from zero (if Scotland refused to accept any) to a per-capita share (~£120 billion) to a GDP-based share.
- **The Irish Precedent**: The 1921 Anglo-Irish Treaty left Ireland with a share of UK debt, but this was effectively written off during the 1930s.

## Key Concepts
- **Acts of Union 1707 and 1801**: Brought Scottish and Irish debts into the UK system
- **The Irish Land Acts**: UK government loans to Irish tenants to buy out landlords — a form of social debt
- **Devolution and debt**: The Barnett formula and its implications for debt attribution
- **The 2014 Scottish independence referendum**: Raised but did not resolve debt allocation questions

## Mental Models
- Think of national debt as a joint liability of a political community; its allocation on dissolution is inherently political, not technical
- Use historical precedent (Ireland 1921) cautiously: each case is unique

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming debt allocation is a technical accounting exercise**: It is fundamentally a political negotiation
- **Ignoring the credibility dimension**: How a successor state handles inherited debt signals its creditworthiness

## Key Takeaways
1. The UK National Debt incorporated Scottish and Irish elements at the Acts of Union
2. The 1921 Anglo-Irish settlement left Ireland with debt obligations that were later abandoned
3. Scottish independence would raise complex debt allocation questions with no clear precedent
4. The credibility of a successor state's debt depends partly on how it handles inherited obligations
5. The political economy of debt allocation is as important as the arithmetic
