# Chapter 10: The Post-Second World War Years

## Core Idea
The three decades after 1945 saw the most successful debt reduction in British history: from ~240% to ~50% of GDP. This was achieved not through repayment but through a combination of economic growth, inflation, and financial repression. The Keynesian consensus prioritised full employment over debt reduction, and the debt melted away as a policy concern.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Financial Repression**: A regime of controlled interest rates, captive domestic buyers, and inflation that systematically transfers wealth from creditors to debtors. This was the unacknowledged engine of post-war debt reduction.
- **The Keynesian Consensus**: Full employment and growth were the explicit policy goals; debt reduction was incidental. This reversed the Victorian priority.
- **Inflation as Fiscal Instrument**: When nominal rates are held below inflation, the real value of debt erodes automatically. This was the "hidden" mechanism of debt reduction.

## Key Concepts
- **The 1958 "Robot" plan**: A proposed but rejected scheme to float the pound and liberalise capital markets
- **The Radcliffe Report (1959)**: Investigated the monetary system; its scepticism about monetary policy reinforced the fiscal/Keynesian approach
- **Barro's Ricardian Equivalence (1974)**: The theoretical argument that government bonds are not net wealth because taxpayers anticipate future tax liabilities. Slater is sceptical of its practical relevance.
- **The 1970s inflation**: Accelerating inflation that further eroded debt but created macroeconomic chaos

## Mental Models
- Think of post-war debt reduction as a three-legged stool: growth + inflation + financial repression. Remove any leg and the process stalls.
- Use the Keynesian framework when analysing debt in a full-employment context: the debt is not the constraint, real resources are.

## Anti-patterns
- **Attributing debt reduction to fiscal virtue**: It was primarily growth and inflation, not austerity
- **Applying Ricardian Equivalence literally**: The theoretical conditions (perfect foresight, infinite horizons, no liquidity constraints) are never met in practice

## Key Takeaways
1. The debt/GDP ratio fell from ~240% (1945) to ~50% (1975) without significant nominal repayment
2. The primary mechanism was GDP growth; secondary was inflation eroding the real value
3. Financial repression (controlled rates, captive buyers) kept interest costs low
4. The Keynesian consensus explicitly deprioritised debt reduction in favour of employment
5. The 1970s inflation accelerated debt erosion but at the cost of macroeconomic instability
