# Chapter 15: Recent Developments (November 2024)

## Core Idea
Since the 2018 first edition, the National Debt has grown from £1.76 trillion to £2.77 trillion (September 2024), and the Debt/GDP ratio from 89% to 99%. Three factors drove this: an unprecedented sequence of peacetime shocks (Brexit, Covid, cost-of-living crisis, Truss government), sharpening long-term challenges (aging, climate, defence), and the rapid erosion of previously favourable debt characteristics.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Shock Sequence**: A rapid series of peacetime crises, each increasing debt and reducing future flexibility. The positive reading: the government's ability to borrow enabled decisive crisis response.
- **The r–g Reversal**: The post-2022 rise in interest rates (r) and stagnation of growth (g) reversed the favourable debt dynamics of the 2010s. The UK was doubly hit: r rose sharply and g fell.
- **The QE/QT Trap**: Quantitative Easing had halved the effective average maturity of UK debt by replacing long gilts with instantly rate-sensitive central bank reserves. When rates rose, the interest bill jumped immediately.
- **The Foreign Ownership Problem**: Foreign holdings rose from ~13% (2004) to ~25% (2022), increasing market volatility and creating a net outflow of interest payments (~£22bn annually).

## Key Concepts
- **The Truss Mini-Budget (2022)**: Unfunded tax cuts, no OBR review, sacking of the Treasury permanent secretary. Market reaction was immediate and severe: sterling fell, gilt yields spiked, and pension funds using liability-driven investments (LDIs) faced margin-call crises. The Bank of England had to intervene.
- **Index-linked bonds**: The UK's unusually high proportion of index-linked gilts meant the post-Covid inflation spike disproportionately increased interest costs.
- **Fiscal Rules evolution**: From Brown's Golden Rule (1997) to Sunak's falling-debt-ratio rule to Reeves's two-fold Stability Rule (current budget balance by year 3) and Investment Rule (PSNFL falling by year 3).
- **The House of Lords report (2024)**: Found no short-term panic needed but urged urgency on long-term trends.

## Mental Models
- Think of the 2020s as a stress test: favourable debt characteristics that took decades to build eroded in a few years
- Use the r–g framework to diagnose why UK debt sustainability deteriorated faster than peers

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming long maturity provides insulation**: QE had effectively shortened duration; the insulation was illusory
- **Treating fiscal rules as credible commitments**: The history of rules (including Pitt's) shows they are always vulnerable to override

## Key Takeaways
1. Debt rose from £1.76tn (2017) to £2.77tn (2024); Debt/GDP from 89% to 99%
2. Annual borrowing in 2023–24 was £168bn (6.5% of GDP), versus £62bn (3.2%) in 2016–17
3. The interest bill roughly doubled, from £47bn to £86bn annually
4. The OBR's long-term projection worsened: from 234% Debt/GDP by 2066–67 to 310% by the mid-2070s
5. The Truss episode demonstrated how quickly market confidence can erode and how costly restoration is
6. The UK's previously favourable debt characteristics (long maturity, domestic ownership, low r–g) have all weakened
7. Slater's preference remains for strengthened OBR-style transparency over rigid fiscal rules
