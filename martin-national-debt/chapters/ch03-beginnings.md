# Chapter 3: The Beginnings of the National Debt

## Core Idea
The National Debt was born in 1694 with the founding of the Bank of England, created explicitly to lend £1.2 million to the government for war against France. This transformed government finance from improvised royal borrowing to a permanent, marketable, parliamentary-guaranteed instrument.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Permanent Debt Innovation**: Unlike earlier loans intended for repayment, the Bank of England loan was designed as perpetual — the government need never repay principal, only interest. This was transformative.
- **The Parliamentary Guarantee**: The critical credibility mechanism. Parliament committed future tax revenues to service the debt, making it fundamentally different from royal promises.
- **The Funded vs. Unfunded Distinction**: Funded debt had dedicated tax revenues assigned to service it; unfunded debt (short-term bills) did not.

## Key Concepts
- **Bank of England (1694)**: Founded explicitly to finance government war borrowing; private shareholders with public purpose
- **Consols (Consolidated Annuities)**: The archetypal British perpetual bond, originating in 1751
- **The Bankers' Debt (1672)**: The unintended precursor — loans frozen by the Stop of the Exchequer, later regularised
- **Tontines**: Group annuities where survivors inherited the shares of the deceased; popular on the continent, rarely used in Britain
- **Lottery loans**: Bonds with lottery elements to attract risk-seeking investors

## Mental Models
- Use "perpetual debt" when you need to smooth war costs across generations without ever concentrating repayment in any single period
- Think of the Bank of England as a credibility device: its private structure and parliamentary backing made government promises believable

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing bonds, stocks, and loans**: These terms have shifted meaning over time; "stock" originally meant registered (not certificated) debt
- **Assuming early debt was like modern debt**: No secondary market initially, transfer was cumbersome, and many instruments were complex and bespoke

## Key Takeaways
1. The Bank of England was founded in 1694 specifically to lend to government — banking came later
2. The £1.2 million loan was about 1.5% of GDP at the time — equivalent to roughly £30 billion today
3. The parliamentary guarantee was the critical innovation, not the financial engineering
4. Early debt instruments were complex: annuities, tontines, lottery loans, and simple bonds coexisted
5. The South Sea Company (1711) was an early attempt to consolidate and manage the debt, with disastrous later consequences
