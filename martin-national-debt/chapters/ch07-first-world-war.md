# Chapter 7: The First World War

## Core Idea
The First World War transformed British public finance. The debt exploded from roughly 25% to over 130% of GDP in just five years. The war was financed partly by taxation, partly by domestic borrowing, and critically by foreign borrowing — especially from the United States — creating a new dimension of international indebtedness that would shape the interwar period.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The War Finance Trilemma**: Governments face three competing imperatives in war: (1) raise taxes to pay for war, (2) borrow domestically, (3) borrow internationally. Each has different distributional and sustainability implications.
- **Foreign Debt as Strategic Constraint**: Borrowing from allies (especially the US) created post-war obligations that constrained diplomatic and economic policy. The American Loan became a persistent irritant in Anglo-American relations.
- **Inflation as Hidden Taxation**: The wartime expansion of money supply and post-war inflation eroded the real value of debt, effectively transferring wealth from creditors to debtors.

## Key Concepts
- **Ways and Means Advances**: Short-term borrowing from the Bank of England to smooth cash flows
- **War Loans (1914, 1917)**: Massive domestic bond issues marketed to the public as patriotic duty
- **American Loan**: US government lending to Britain, creating dollar-denominated obligations
- **The 1917 War Loan at 5%**: The benchmark long-term rate for a generation
- **Post-war inflation**: Prices roughly doubled between 1914 and 1920, eroding the real burden

## Mental Models
- Think of war debt as a three-way transfer: from future taxpayers to current soldiers/suppliers, mediated by bondholders
- Use the foreign/domestic distinction to assess strategic vulnerability: foreign debt creates external constraints

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming war debts are simply "paid off"**: They are absorbed through growth, inflation, and restructuring over decades
- **Ignoring the monetary dimension**: The wartime money supply expansion was as significant as the fiscal borrowing

## Key Takeaways
1. The debt rose from ~25% to ~130% of GDP between 1914 and 1919
2. About one-third of war finance came from taxation, two-thirds from borrowing
3. Foreign borrowing (especially from the US) created obligations that constrained post-war policy
4. The 1917 5% War Loan became the benchmark rate that shaped interwar debt management
5. Post-war inflation significantly eroded the real value of the debt, but also created social and economic disruption
