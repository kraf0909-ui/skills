# Chapter 2: Pre-history

## Core Idea
Before the formal National Debt, English monarchs financed state activities through taxation, feudal obligations, and improvised borrowing. The critical preconditions for permanent public debt — parliamentary control over taxation, credible commitment to repayment, and a financial intermediary class — emerged gradually from medieval to Stuart times.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Parliamentary Taxation Constraint**: The need for parliamentary consent to taxation, hard-won by the seventeenth century, created both a constraint and a credibility advantage. Lenders trusted parliamentary-guaranteed debt more than royal promises.
- **Tallies as Proto-Debt**: Medieval wooden tallies were receipts for tax payments that could circulate as transferable claims — an early form of government obligation.

## Key Concepts
- **Tallies**: Notched wooden sticks recording tax payments, transferable and sometimes circulating as money
- **Feudal aids**: Occasional levies the king could demand from tenants-in-chief
- **Ship money**: A Stuart tax on ports later extended inland, provoking constitutional resistance
- **The Stop of the Exchequer (1672)**: Charles II’s default on banker loans, demonstrating the credibility problem of royal borrowing

## Mental Models
- Think of pre-modern public finance as a credibility-building exercise: every default made future borrowing harder and more expensive
- View the Glorious Revolution (1688) not just as political but as financial: it imported Dutch methods of public credit

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming modern financial institutions existed in the past**: The Bank of England (1694) was revolutionary precisely because nothing like it had existed before
- **Ignoring the role of political structure**: The form of government (absolute vs. parliamentary) profoundly shaped borrowing capacity

## Key Takeaways
1. Medieval English kings were chronically short of money relative to their ambitions
2. The need for parliamentary consent to taxation was a double-edged sword: constraining but credibility-enhancing
3. The Stop of the Exchequer (1672) demonstrated why royal borrowing lacked credibility
4. By 1688, England had the political preconditions but not yet the institutional mechanisms for permanent public debt
5. The Dutch model of funded debt, brought by William III, provided the template
