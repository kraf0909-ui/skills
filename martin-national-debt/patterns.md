# Patterns

## Pattern: The War-Debt Cycle
**When to use**: Analyzing any major conflict's fiscal impact
**How**: Expect debt to spike during war, stabilise at a higher plateau after peace, and decline only slowly (if at all) through growth rather than repayment
**Trade-offs**: War borrowing preserves domestic living standards but creates intergenerational transfer; excessive reliance on foreign borrowing adds strategic constraints
**Examples**: Napoleonic Wars (peak ~240% GDP), WWI (~130%), WWII (~240%)

## Pattern: The Sinking Fund Trap
**When to use**: Evaluating any dedicated repayment scheme
**How**: Check whether the government is simultaneously borrowing; if so, the fund loses money on the interest spread and is politically vulnerable to diversion
**Trade-offs**: Sinking funds signal commitment but are economically costly and logically self-defeating during net borrowing
**Examples**: Pitt's Fund (1786) — rigorous in design but flawed in conception; later nineteenth-century funds worked better because net borrowing had ceased

## Pattern: Conversion Operations
**When to use**: When market rates have fallen significantly below existing bond coupons
**How**: Offer to exchange old high-coupon bonds for new low-coupon bonds; success requires (1) legal right to redeem, (2) cash alternative offered, (3) careful market timing and "grooming"
**Trade-offs**: Saves interest but may increase nominal debt if sweeteners offered; can damage credibility if mishandled
**Examples**: 1888 Goschen conversion; 1932 War Loan conversion (5% to 3.5%)

## Pattern: Financial Repression
**When to use**: When governments need to fund large deficits without market-rate interest costs
**How**: Combine controlled interest rates, captive domestic buyers (banks, pension funds, insurance companies), and inflation above nominal rates
**Trade-offs**: Effectively taxes bondholders and savers; damages capital market development; unsustainable in open economies
**Examples**: UK 1945–1970s; most advanced economies post-2008 to some degree

## Pattern: The Off-Balance-Sheet Temptation
**When to use**: When governments seek to invest or commit without increasing headline debt
**How**: Create special vehicles, PFI schemes, or unfunded promises that generate real liabilities without formal debt recognition
**Trade-offs**: Avoids short-term political costs but raises long-term costs (higher interest, reduced transparency, damaged credibility)
**Examples**: South Sea Company (1711); PFI (1990s–2010s); unfunded public-sector pensions

## Pattern: Growth-Driven Debt Reduction
**When to use**: As the default strategy for managing high but stable debt
**How**: Maintain primary balance near zero; rely on GDP growth in the denominator to reduce Debt/GDP ratio compoundingly over time
**Trade-offs**: Politically easiest but requires sustained growth; does not reduce nominal debt; vulnerable to growth shocks
**Examples**: Post-1815 (century-long reduction from ~240% to ~25%); post-1945 (reduction to ~50% by 1975)

## Pattern: The r–g Diagnostic
**When to use**: First-pass assessment of debt sustainability in any country
**How**: Compare real interest rate (r) to real growth rate (g). If r < g, debt can be stable with modest deficits. If r > g, primary surpluses are required.
**Trade-offs**: Simple and powerful but ignores distribution, risk premia, and the possibility that r responds to debt levels
**Examples**: UK 2010s (r < g, favourable); UK 2020s (r > g, unfavourable)

## Pattern: Fiscal Rules and Their Erosion
**When to use**: Designing or evaluating institutional constraints on government borrowing
**How**: Establish a numerical target or procedural rule; expect gradual redefinition, creative accounting, and eventual override in crisis
**Trade-offs**: Rules provide discipline and signalling but reduce flexibility; independent oversight (OBR model) may be more robust than hard targets
**Examples**: Pitt's Sinking Fund; Maastricht 60%/3%; Brown's Golden Rule; Sunak's falling-debt rule; Reeves's Stability/Investment Rules

## Pattern: The QE/QT Duration Trap
**When to use**: When central banks have engaged in large-scale asset purchases
**How**: QE replaces long-dated bonds with instantly rate-sensitive central bank reserves; when rates rise, the interest bill jumps immediately, regardless of apparent bond maturity
**Trade-offs**: QE provides stimulus and liquidity but erodes the government's natural insulation from rate changes
**Examples**: UK post-2022: apparent average maturity 14–15 years, effective maturity halved by QE

## Pattern: Foreign Ownership Risk
**When to use**: Assessing vulnerability to market flight and external drain
**How**: Track foreign holdings as a share of total debt; foreign investors are more flighty and create net outflows (interest leaves the country)
**Trade-offs**: Foreign buyers expand the market and may lower average rates, but increase volatility and external obligation
**Examples**: UK foreign holdings rose from ~13% (2004) to ~25% (2022); contributed to Truss episode severity
