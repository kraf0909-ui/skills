# Cheatsheet

## Decision Rules

### When is debt sustainable?
- If r < g: debt/GDP can fall even with modest primary deficits
- If r > g: primary surplus is required to stabilise debt/GDP
- If r – g > 3% persistently: urgent fiscal adjustment needed

### When to repay debt vs. let it grow?
- **Repay when**: economy strong, debt/GDP low, no foreseeable crises, political consensus exists
- **Let it stabilise when**: r < g, debt/GDP moderate, growth is the engine
- **Worry when**: debt rising in peacetime, r > g, hidden liabilities growing, external shocks likely

### When can conversion operations succeed?
- Market rate is well below existing coupon (at least 1–1.5% gap)
- Government has legal redemption right
- Cash alternative is credibly offered
- Market has been "groomed" with advance signals
- Macroeconomic conditions are stable

### When to use fiscal rules?
- Rules work best as signalling devices and constraints on normal politics
- Rules will always be overridden in genuine crises (and should be)
- Independent oversight (OBR) is more robust than numerical targets
- Any rule can be gamed; transparency is the ultimate safeguard

## Decision Tree: How to Respond to Rising Debt/GDP

```
Is the rise due to a temporary crisis (war, pandemic, financial crash)?
  YES → Borrow to respond; plan stabilisation as crisis ends
  NO → Is the rise due to structural deficit (peacetime spending > revenue)?
          YES → Is r < g?
                    YES → Prioritise growth-enhancing policies; modest consolidation
                    NO → Fiscal adjustment needed: some mix of tax rises and spending restraint
          NO → Is the rise due to hidden liabilities (pensions, PFI, etc.)?
                    YES → Bring onto balance sheet; restructure if possible
                    NO → Investigate measurement/definition issues
```

## Trade-off Matrix: Debt Management Strategies

| Strategy | Speed | Political Cost | Economic Cost | Risk | Best For |
|----------|-------|---------------|---------------|------|----------|
| Austerity (primary surplus) | Fast | Very high | High (demand reduction) | Recession | r > g, no growth options |
| Growth enhancement | Slow | Low | Low | Uncertain | r < g, structural reforms possible |
| Inflation/financial repression | Medium | Hidden (tax on savers) | Medium (distortions) | Instability | Closed economy, captive buyers |
| Conversion/refinancing | Medium | Low | Low | Market disruption | r_market << r_coupon |
| Default/restructuring | Immediate | Very high | Very high | Exclusion from markets | Unsustainable foreign debt |

## Thresholds & Defaults

- **UK historical debt peaks**: ~240% GDP (1815, 1945)
- **UK post-war lows**: ~25% GDP (1914, 1990s)
- **Maastricht reference**: 60% GDP (political, not analytical)
- **OBR 2024 projection**: 310% GDP by mid-2070s under unchanged policy
- **Interest bill warning level**: >3% of GDP (UK reached 3.5% in 2023)
- **Foreign holdings concern**: >25% of total debt increases volatility risk
- **Index-linked proportion**: UK ~25% of gilts (unusually high; amplifies inflation risk)
- **Average gilt maturity**: Apparent 14–15 years; effective ~7–8 years post-QE

## Tells & Smells

- **If debt is rising in peacetime without crisis**: structural problem, investigate
- **If r > g and primary deficit**: explosive dynamics, act soon
- **If government creates off-balance-sheet vehicles**: likely hiding true costs
- **If fiscal rules are repeatedly redefined**: credibility erosion
- **If foreign holdings rising fast**: vulnerability to flight
- **If QE has been large and prolonged**: effective duration has shortened
- **If index-linked proportion is high**: inflation spike will disproportionately hurt
- **If political commitment to repayment institutions is weak**: future flexibility at risk

## Quick Reference: UK Debt History

| Period | Debt/GDP | Key Driver | Exit Strategy |
|--------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| 1694–1815 | 0% → 240% | Wars with France | Growth (century-long) |
| 1815–1914 | 240% → 25% | Peace + industrial growth | Growth + modest repayment |
| 1914–1919 | 25% → 130% | WWI | Inflation + growth |
| 1919–1939 | 130% → 110% | Interwar stagnation | Conversion (1932) |
| 1939–1945 | 110% → 240% | WWII | Financial repression + growth |
| 1945–1975 | 240% → 50% | Growth + inflation | Growth (primary driver) |
| 1975–2008 | 50% → 40% | Moderation | Growth |
| 2008–2017 | 40% → 89% | Financial crisis | Austerity (limited) |
| 2017–2024 | 89% → 99% | Covid, Brexit, shocks | Uncertain |
