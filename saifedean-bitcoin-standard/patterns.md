# Patterns

## Pattern: Monetary Competition and Selection
**When to use**: Evaluating any good's potential to become or remain money
**How**: Assess the good across the five monetary properties: scarcity, durability, divisibility, recognisability, portability. The good that best combines these properties will win market share as money.
**Trade-offs**: No good is perfect across all dimensions; trade-offs exist (e.g., gold is durable but not easily divisible in small amounts)
**Examples**: Primitive moneys (Ch 2); gold's dominance (Ch 3); Bitcoin's emergence (Ch 8)

## Pattern: Hard Money Preserves Civilisation
**When to use**: Assessing whether a monetary standard promotes long-term prosperity
**How**: Examine the monetary standard's hardness (stock-to-flow). Hard money lowers time preference, encourages saving and capital accumulation, and supports cultural flourishing. Easy money does the opposite.
**Trade-offs**: Hard money constrains government action and crisis response; but this constraint is the point
**Examples**: Byzantine bezant (Ch 3); La Belle Epoque (Ch 3); post-1971 fiat era (Ch 4)

## Pattern: Government Capture of Money
**When to use**: Analysing the political economy of any monetary system
**How**: Ask who controls money creation and what incentives they face. Governments always have an incentive to inflate because it is the hidden tax that funds spending without legislative approval.
**Trade-offs**: Government money provides flexibility in crisis; but the crisis-response power is systematically abused
**Examples**: Roman debasement (Ch 3); Continental currency (implied); post-1914 fiat systems (Ch 4)

## Pattern: The Cantillon Wealth Transfer
**When to use**: Analysing the distributional effects of monetary policy
**How**: Trace who receives new money first (banks, asset holders, government contractors) and who receives it last (wage earners, fixed-income recipients). The first group benefits at the expense of the last.
**Trade-offs**: Monetary expansion may stimulate activity in the short run; but the distributional cost is real and permanent
**Examples**: Post-2008 QE (implied); all fiat inflation (Ch 5)

## Pattern: Interest Rate Manipulation Causes Malinvestment
**When to use**: Diagnosing the causes of boom-bust cycles
**How**: When central banks suppress interest rates below their natural level, investment projects that are not economically viable appear profitable. When rates normalise, these projects fail, causing recession.
**Trade-offs**: Low rates may stimulate short-term activity; but they systematically misallocate capital
**Examples**: 2008 financial crisis (Ch 6); dot-com bubble (implied); all fiat business cycles (Ch 6)

## Pattern: Bitcoin's Antifragile Response to Attacks
**When to use**: Assessing Bitcoin's resilience to regulatory, technical, or market shocks
**How**: Bitcoin does not merely survive attacks; it grows stronger. Bans increase awareness; exchange failures validate self-custody; price crashes shake out weak hands and concentrate ownership among believers.
**Trade-offs**: Short-term volatility and disruption; long-term strengthening
**Examples**: China mining bans (post-book); Mt. Gox collapse (implied); multiple "deaths" of Bitcoin (Ch 10)

## Pattern: Store of Value Precedes Medium of Exchange
**When to use**: Evaluating Bitcoin's monetisation trajectory
**How**: Money historically evolves from collectible/store of value to medium of exchange to unit of account. Judging Bitcoin by its current transaction volume mistakes the early phase for the end state.
**Trade-offs**: A money that is not yet widely used for daily transactions may still be valuable as a store of value
**Examples**: Gold's historical evolution (Ch 3); Bitcoin's current adoption pattern (Ch 9)

## Pattern: Altcoins Sacrifice Decentralisation for Features
**When to use**: Evaluating cryptocurrencies other than Bitcoin
**How**: Assess whether the altcoin has made trade-offs that reduce decentralisation, security, or immutability. If so, it is not competing with Bitcoin as money but as a technology platform.
**Trade-offs**: Speed and features are valuable for some applications; but they are not the properties that make money trustworthy
**Examples**: Ethereum, Ripple, and other altcoins (Ch 10)

## Pattern: The Bezzle Cycle
**When to use**: Understanding the boom-bust pattern of fiat economies
**How**: Monetary inflation creates an illusion of wealth (the bezzle) as nominal asset prices rise. When inflation slows or reverses, the illusion is shattered, revealing malinvestment and insolvency.
**Trade-offs**: Inflationary booms feel prosperous; but they are followed by real busts
**Examples**: 2008 financial crisis (Ch 6, Ch 7); all fiat credit cycles

## Pattern: Separation of Money and State
**When to use**: Designing or evaluating monetary institutions
**How**: Ask whether the monetary system constrains government power or enables it. Sound money limits government; fiat money enables omnipotent government.
**Trade-offs**: Constrained government cannot respond as flexibly to crises; but unconstrained government creates the crises
**Examples**: Gold standard constraints (Ch 3); post-1971 government expansion (Ch 4, Ch 7)
