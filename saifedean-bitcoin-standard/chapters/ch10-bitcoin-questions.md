# Chapter 10: Bitcoin Questions

## Core Idea
Common objections to Bitcoin — that mining is wasteful, that it cannot scale, that it is for criminals, that altcoins are better — misunderstand its fundamental value proposition. Bitcoin's "inefficiencies" (energy use, slow transaction speed, inability to change) are precisely what make it secure, decentralised, and resistant to capture. Altcoins sacrifice these properties for speed and features, making them unsuitable as money.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Mining as Security, Not Waste**: Bitcoin's energy consumption is not waste but the cost of securing a global, permissionless monetary network. The energy is converted into immutable, verifiable transaction history. Compared to the cost of the existing banking and military apparatus that secures fiat money, Bitcoin's energy use is trivial.
- **Immutability as Feature**: Bitcoin's inability to change its monetary policy is not a bug but its most important feature. A money that can be changed by developers or miners is not sound money. Bitcoin's ossification is what makes it trustworthy.
- **Antifragility**: Bitcoin does not merely resist shocks; it grows stronger from them. Attacks, bans, and crises increase awareness, development, and network security. Each survived challenge validates Bitcoin's resilience.
- **Altcoins as Compromises**: Alternative cryptocurrencies trade decentralisation and security for speed, features, or governance flexibility. This makes them unsuitable as money because money's primary requirement is trustworthiness, not convenience.
- **Blockchain, Not Bitcoin, Is the Scam**: The popular slogan "blockchain, not Bitcoin" inverts the reality. Blockchain without Bitcoin's proof-of-work and decentralisation is merely an inefficient database. The value is in the monetary application, not the data structure.

## Key Concepts
- **Proof of work**: The energy-intensive process that secures Bitcoin and makes it costly to attack
- **51% attack**: The theoretical attack requiring majority control of mining power
- **Lightning Network**: A second-layer solution for fast, cheap Bitcoin transactions
- **Altcoin**: Any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin
- **ICO (Initial Coin Offering)**: A fundraising method for altcoins, often associated with scams
- **Hard fork**: A permanent divergence in the blockchain; Bitcoin's resistance to hard forks preserves its monetary policy

## Mental Models
- Think of Bitcoin's energy use as the cost of replacing armies, banks, and central banks with mathematics and physics
- Use the antifragility framework to understand why attacks on Bitcoin tend to strengthen it

## Anti-patterns
- **Judging Bitcoin by transaction throughput**: Visa-scale throughput is not Bitcoin's goal; final settlement of large-value transactions is
- **Assuming altcoins can replicate Bitcoin's monetary properties**: They sacrifice decentralisation, which is the essential property

## Key Takeaways
1. Bitcoin mining is not waste; it is the cost of securing a global monetary network
2. Bitcoin's inability to change its monetary policy is its most important feature
3. Bitcoin is antifragile — it grows stronger from attacks and crises
4. Altcoins sacrifice decentralisation for speed and features, making them unsuitable as money
5. "Blockchain, not Bitcoin" inverts the reality: the value is in the monetary application
