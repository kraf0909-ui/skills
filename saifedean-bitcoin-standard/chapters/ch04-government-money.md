# Chapter 4: Government Money

## Core Idea
Government money — fiat currency — represents a historical aberration: the replacement of market-selected hard money with politically controlled easy money. The twentieth century's shift from gold to government money enabled unprecedented monetary inflation, wealth expropriation, and economic instability. The track record of government money is one of systematic debasement.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Government Money Aberration**: For most of history, money was market-selected hard commodity. Government-issued fiat money is a recent and destructive departure from this norm. The twentieth century was the first in which government money dominated.
- **Monetary Nationalism**: The replacement of the international gold standard with national fiat currencies destroyed the global monetary unity of La Belle Epoque. Each government gained the power to manipulate its own currency, leading to competitive devaluations and trade restrictions.
- **The Track Record of Debasement**: Every government money in history has been debased. The only variable is the speed — from gradual (US dollar, ~95% loss since 1913) to catastrophic (Weimar, Zimbabwe, Venezuela).

## Key Concepts
- **Fiat money**: Currency not backed by a commodity; its value derives from government decree and legal tender laws
- **Monetary nationalism**: The system of national currencies with government control over exchange rates and money supply
- **Bretton Woods (1944)**: A pseudo-gold standard where only foreign central banks could redeem dollars for gold; collapsed in 1971
- **Nixon shock (1971)**: The suspension of dollar convertibility to gold, ending the last link between major currencies and gold
- **Hyperinflation**: The catastrophic endpoint of government money; complete loss of purchasing power

## Mental Models
- Think of government money as a recurring historical pattern: governments take control of money, then debase it to finance spending
- Use the debasement track record to assess the long-term viability of any government currency

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming modern central banking has solved the debasement problem**: The dollar has lost ~95% of its value since the Fed was created
- **Ignoring the political incentives**: Governments always have an incentive to inflate; the only constraint is credibility and technical capacity

## Key Takeaways
1. Government money is a historical aberration, not the norm
2. The twentieth century saw the replacement of market money with political money
3. Monetary nationalism destroyed the global monetary unity of the gold standard
4. Every government money in history has been debased; the only variable is speed
5. The US dollar has lost ~95% of its purchasing power since 1913
