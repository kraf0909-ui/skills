# Chapter 2: Primitive Moneys

## Core Idea
Before precious metals, humans used a wide variety of primitive moneys — shells, beads, stones, cattle, salt. These goods were selected as money because they possessed the key monetary properties, particularly hardness (difficulty of production). The Yapese Rai stones demonstrate that money need not be physically portable to function; what matters is the ledger of ownership.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hardness as Monetary Selection Criterion**: The goods that became primitive moneys were those that were hardest to produce — requiring significant labour, time, or skill to create. Easy-to-produce goods could not sustain monetary value because supply would expand to meet demand.
- **Stock-to-Flow Ratio**: The ratio of existing stock to annual production flow. Higher stock-to-flow means greater hardness because new production is small relative to existing stock. Rai stones had effectively infinite stock-to-flow because production had ceased.
- **Collectibles as Proto-Money**: Primitive moneys were often collectibles — objects valued for their beauty, rarity, and the skill required to produce them. These properties made them salable across time and space.

## Key Concepts
- **Rai stones**: Large stone discs on the island of Yap, used as money despite being immobile; ownership was transferred by oral ledger
- **Wampum**: Shell beads used as money by Native Americans; value derived from labour-intensive production
- **Cowrie shells**: Used as money across Africa and Asia; hardness came from transport costs
- **Hardness**: Resistance to increases in supply; the difficulty of producing new units
- **Stock-to-flow**: Existing stock divided by annual production; the measure of hardness

## Mental Models
- Use stock-to-flow as the primary metric for comparing monetary hardness across any asset
- Think of primitive moneys as natural experiments in monetary competition: the hardest goods won

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming money requires portability**: Rai stones show that a reliable ledger can substitute for physical transport
- **Ignoring production cost**: Goods that are easy to produce cannot sustain monetary value

## Key Takeaways
1. Primitive moneys were selected for hardness — difficulty of production
2. Stock-to-flow ratio is the quantitative measure of monetary hardness
3. Rai stones demonstrate that money can function without physical portability if ownership is reliably recorded
4. The hardest primitive moneys (those with highest stock-to-flow) persisted longest
5. The transition from primitive to metallic money was driven by the superior hardness and salability of metals
