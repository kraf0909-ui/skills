# Glossary

**51% attack** — A theoretical attack on Bitcoin requiring control of a majority of mining power; economically irrational at scale. (Ch 10)

**Altcoin** — Any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin. (Ch 10)

**Antifragility** — The property of growing stronger from shocks and stressors; Bitcoin's response to attacks and crises. (Ch 10)

**Austrian business cycle theory** — The theory that artificially low interest rates cause malinvestment and boom-bust cycles. (Ch 6)

**Bezzle** — The gap between apparent and real value in an inflationary system; an illusion of wealth. (Ch 7)

**Bitcoin** — A decentralised digital currency with a fixed supply of 21 million, secured by proof of work. (Ch 8)

**Blockchain** — A distributed, immutable ledger of transactions; Bitcoin's innovation is combining it with proof of work and digital scarcity. (Ch 8, Ch 10)

**Bretton Woods** — The 1944 monetary agreement establishing a pseudo-gold standard; collapsed in 1971. (Ch 4)

**Byzantine bezant** — The gold coin that served as international reserve currency for 600+ years. (Ch 3)

**Cantillon Effect** — The non-neutral distributional effects of monetary expansion; benefits first recipients at the expense of last. (Ch 5)

**Capital accumulation** — The process of saving and investing to increase future productive capacity. (Ch 5)

**Censorship resistance** — The inability of any authority to block or reverse Bitcoin transactions. (Ch 9)

**Difficulty adjustment** — Bitcoin's automatic recalibration of mining difficulty every 2,016 blocks to maintain 10-minute block time. (Ch 8)

**Fiat money** — Currency not backed by a commodity; its value derives from government decree. (Ch 4)

**Final settlement** — Irreversible transfer of ownership without counterparty risk. (Ch 9)

**Gresham's Law** — Bad money drives out good when both circulate at fixed legal ratios. (Ch 3)

**Halving** — The 50% reduction in Bitcoin block reward every 210,000 blocks (~4 years). (Ch 8)

**Hard money** — Money that is costly to produce and resistant to increases in supply. (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 3)

**Hardness** — Resistance to increases in supply; the difficulty of producing new monetary units. (Ch 2)

**Hyperinflation** — Catastrophic loss of purchasing power caused by excessive money creation. (Ch 4)

**Interest rate** — The price of time; the premium for present goods over future goods. (Ch 6)

**La Belle Epoque** — The period 1871–1914 under the classical gold standard; unprecedented global trade and peace. (Ch 3)

**Lightning Network** — A second-layer protocol for fast, cheap Bitcoin transactions. (Ch 10)

**Malinvestment** — Investment in projects that appear profitable only because of distorted price signals. (Ch 6)

**Medium of exchange** — A good acquired for trading rather than consumption. (Ch 1)

**Monetary nationalism** — The system of national currencies with government control over money. (Ch 4)

**Nixon shock (1971)** — Suspension of dollar convertibility to gold; end of Bretton Woods. (Ch 4)

**One-to-many innovation** — Incremental improvements and scaling of existing technologies. (Ch 5)

**Price signals** — The information conveyed by market prices about relative scarcity and value. (Ch 6)

**Primitive money** — Non-metallic moneys (shells, stones, beads) used before precious metals. (Ch 2)

**Private key** — The cryptographic secret that controls access to bitcoins. (Ch 8)

**Proof of work** — The energy-intensive process that secures Bitcoin and makes it costly to attack. (Ch 8, Ch 10)

**Rai stones** — Large stone discs used as money on Yap; ownership transferred by oral ledger. (Ch 2)

**Regression Theorem** (Mises) — Money must originate as a commodity with non-monetary use value. (Ch 1)

**Salability** — The ease with which a good can be sold at the prevailing market price. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Sound money** — Money that preserves purchasing power and resists debasement. (Ch 3, Ch 5, Ch 6, Ch 7)

**Stock-to-flow** — The ratio of existing stock to annual production; the measure of monetary hardness. (Ch 2, Ch 8)

**Store of value** — An asset that preserves purchasing power across time. (Ch 1, Ch 9)

**Time preference** — The degree of preference for present goods over future goods. (Ch 5)

**Unit of account** — The common measure in which prices are quoted. (Ch 1, Ch 9)

**Zero-to-one innovation** — Fundamental, paradigm-shifting innovations. (Ch 5)
