## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. Hard Money vs. Easy Money
The fundamental distinction is between money that is costly to produce (hard money: gold, Bitcoin) and money that is cheap to produce (easy money: seashells, fiat currency). Hard money resists inflation and preserves value across time; easy money is subject to arbitrary debasement. Use hard money when you need a reliable store of value across generations.

### 2. Stock-to-Flow as Hardness Metric
Stock-to-flow ratio (existing stock divided by annual production) is the quantitative measure of monetary hardness. Gold has a ratio of ~60; Bitcoin's rises from ~25 (at halving) toward infinity as issuance approaches zero. Higher stock-to-flow means lower supply elasticity and greater resistance to inflation. Use this metric to compare the hardness of any monetary asset.

### 3. Salability as the Essence of Money
Money is the most salable good — the one most readily accepted in exchange. Salability has three dimensions: across space (transportability), across scale (divisibility), and across time (durability/store of value). A good becomes money not by decree but by winning the market competition for salability. Bitcoin's digital nature gives it unprecedented spatial salability.

### 4. Time Preference and Civilization
Time preference (the degree to which individuals prefer present consumption over future consumption) is the fundamental determinant of civilizational progress. Sound money lowers time preference because it preserves purchasing power into the future, encouraging saving, investment, and long-term thinking. Unsound money raises time preference, encouraging consumption, debt, and short-termism.

### 5. The Cantillon Effect
New money is not neutral. Those who receive it first (banks, government contractors, asset holders) benefit at the expense of those who receive it last (wage earners, savers). This distributional effect of monetary inflation is invisible in standard economics but is a primary mechanism of wealth transfer under fiat systems.

### 6. Sound Money as Capitalism's Information System
Prices are the information system of the market economy. Sound money produces accurate price signals because it is not subject to manipulation. Fiat money distorts prices — particularly interest rates — leading to malinvestment, business cycles, and financial crises. The interest rate is the most important price in the economy; when central banks manipulate it, they destroy the information content of the price system.

### 7. The Bezzle
In an inflationary fiat system, the nominal value of assets and institutions systematically overstates their real value. This gap — the "bezzle" (Galbraith's term, adopted by Ammous) — creates an illusion of wealth that is periodically revealed in crises. Sound money prevents the bezzle by preventing the systematic overstatement of value.

### 8. Bitcoin's Monetary Properties
Bitcoin combines the monetary properties that made gold the historical standard — scarcity, durability, divisibility, recognisability, portability — with digital advantages: perfect portability, perfect divisibility, perfect verifiability, and programmatic scarcity. Its 21 million cap and halving schedule make it the hardest money ever invented.

### 9. Decentralisation as Security
Bitcoin's security does not come from any single entity but from the distributed consensus of miners, nodes, and users. No single point of failure means no single point of control. This makes Bitcoin resistant to censorship, seizure, and debasement — the three primary risks of government money.

### 10. The Separation of Money and State
Just as the separation of church and state was essential for religious freedom, the separation of money and state is essential for economic freedom. Government control of money has been the primary source of monetary instability, war finance, and wealth expropriation throughout history. Bitcoin enables this separation for the first time.
