# Glossary

**Adjustable Peg** — Exchange rate fixed to another currency or gold but adjustable in cases of "fundamental disequilibrium." The Bretton Woods system's central innovation, which proved difficult to operate in practice. (Ch 4)

**Adjustment Mechanism** — The changes in prices and quantities by which market forces eliminate balance-of-payments deficits and surpluses. Under the gold standard, primarily the Humean price-specie flow. (Ch 2)

**Balance of Payments** — The record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world. Comprises the current account (trade in goods and services) and capital account (financial flows). (Ch 1)

**Bimetallism** — A commodity-money standard under which the authorities grant legal-tender status to coins minted with two metals (typically gold and silver). Vulnerable to arbitrage when market and mint ratios diverge. (Ch 2)

**Brassage** — The fee paid for coining precious metal under a commodity money standard. (Ch 2)

**Bretton Woods System** — The international monetary system established in 1944, featuring adjustable pegs, capital controls, and the IMF. Operated from 1945 to 1971. (Ch 4)

**Capital Controls** — Regulations limiting the ability of firms or households to convert domestic currency into foreign exchange. Essential to the Bretton Woods system but increasingly leaky over time. (Ch 4, 5)

**Capital Flight** — The withdrawal of funds from assets denominated in a particular currency, typically motivated by expectations of devaluation. (Ch 3, 4)

**Convertibility** — The ability of a currency to be freely converted into foreign exchange or gold at a fixed price. (Ch 2, 4)

**Credibility** — The confidence of markets that a government will defend its exchange rate peg regardless of domestic costs. Essential for the viability of fixed-rate systems. (Ch 2, 3)

**Currency Board** — A monetary arrangement under which a country ties its monetary policy to that of another country by statute or constitution, issuing domestic currency only against foreign reserves. (Ch 5, 6)

**Currency War** — Competitive currency devaluation or monetary easing under which a country seeks to gain advantage at the expense of neighbors. (Ch 7)

**Current Account** — The component of the balance of payments reflecting transactions in goods and services. A deficit signifies that purchases from foreigners exceed sales to foreigners. (Ch 1, 6, 7)

**Dollarization** — Adopting the U.S. dollar as the domestic currency, eliminating the national currency entirely. (Ch 5, 6)

**Embedded Liberalism** — A commitment to free markets tempered by a broader commitment to social welfare and full employment. The political foundation of the Bretton Woods system. (Ch 1, 4)

**Euro** — The single European currency, launched in 1999, replacing national currencies for participating EU members. (Ch 5, 6, 7)

**European Monetary System (EMS)** — System of pegged but adjustable currencies established by members of the European Community in 1979. Precursor to the euro. (Ch 5)

**European Snake** — A collective arrangement of European countries in the 1970s to peg their exchange rates within narrow bands. Early attempt at European monetary coordination. (Ch 5)

**Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM)** — The component of the European Monetary System under which participating countries pegged their exchange rates. (Ch 5)

**Fiat Money** — Paper currency not backed by gold or other commodities; its value derives from government decree and public confidence. (Ch 3, 5)

**Floating Exchange Rate** — An exchange rate allowed to vary according to market forces. A "clean float" occurs without government intervention; a "dirty float" involves managed variation. (Ch 5)

**Gold Devices** — Techniques used by central banks under the gold standard to encourage gold inflows or discourage outflows without formally breaking convertibility. (Ch 2)

**Gold Exchange Standard** — A system under which countries' international reserves can take the form of convertible foreign currencies as well as gold. Characteristic of the interwar and Bretton Woods systems. (Ch 2, 4)

**Gold Points** — The exchange rate boundaries beyond which it becomes profitable to ship gold rather than buy foreign exchange. (Ch 2)

**Gold Pool** — An arrangement in the 1960s under which major central banks cooperated to support the official gold price of $35 per ounce. (Ch 4)

**Gold Standard** — A monetary system in which the standard economic unit of account is based on a fixed quantity of gold. The classical gold standard operated from the 1870s to 1914. (Ch 2)

**Gresham's Law** — The principle that "bad money drives out good" — when two currencies circulate at a fixed ratio, the overvalued one dominates transactions. (Ch 2)

**Humean Mechanism** — The price-specie flow mechanism described by David Hume: gold flows automatically correct balance-of-payments imbalances through changes in national price levels. (Ch 2)

**IMF Conditionality** — Policy requirements attached to IMF lending, typically including fiscal austerity, monetary tightening, and structural reforms. (Ch 4, 6)

**Impossible Trinity** — The principle that a country cannot simultaneously maintain fixed exchange rates, free capital mobility, and independent monetary policy. Must choose two of three. (Ch 5, 6, 8)

**Inflation Targeting** — A monetary policy framework in which the central bank announces a target inflation rate, publishes forecasts, and explains decisions in terms of target consistency. (Ch 6)

**Lender of Last Resort** — An institution (typically the central bank) that provides liquidity to the banking system in times of crisis. Under the gold standard, this created a tension with currency defense. (Ch 2, 3)

**Maturity Mismatch** — Borrowing short-term while lending or investing long-term. A key vulnerability in banking and a factor in the Asian crisis. (Ch 6)

**Network Externalities** — The phenomenon where the value of a monetary standard to each user increases with the number of other users. Explains path dependence in monetary systems. (Ch 1, 2)

**Original Sin** — The inability of emerging markets to borrow abroad in their own currency, forcing them to borrow in foreign currency and creating currency mismatch vulnerabilities. (Ch 6)

**Path Dependence** — The property of economic and political systems where current options are constrained by past decisions and historical legacies. (Ch 1, 2, 8)

**Plaza Accord (1985)** — Agreement among G5 countries to depreciate the U.S. dollar against the yen and Deutsche Mark. (Ch 5)

**Quantitative Easing (QE)** — Unconventional monetary policy in which central banks purchase assets to inject liquidity into the financial system when interest rates are near zero. (Ch 7)

**Scarce Currency Clause** — A provision in the Bretton Woods Articles of Agreement allowing IMF members to discriminate against countries whose policies caused systemic problems. Never invoked. (Ch 4)

**Securitization** — The process of converting loans into tradable securities. Central to the subprime crisis. (Ch 7)

**Special Drawing Rights (SDR)** — An international reserve asset created by the IMF in 1969 to supplement gold and dollar reserves. (Ch 4)

**Target2** — The Eurozone's real-time gross settlement system; imbalances in Target2 reflect capital flight within the currency union. (Ch 7)

**Triffin Dilemma** — The conflict between domestic and international objectives for the reserve currency country: it must run deficits to supply liquidity, but those deficits eventually undermine confidence. (Ch 4)

**Troika** — The three institutions (IMF, European Commission, European Central Bank) that managed euro area bailout programs during the crisis. (Ch 7)
