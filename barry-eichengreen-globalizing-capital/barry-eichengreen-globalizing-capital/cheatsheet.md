# Cheatsheet

## Decision Rules

### When analyzing a country's exchange rate regime:
1. Check the impossible trinity: which two does it have?
2. Assess credibility: can policymakers defend the peg regardless of domestic costs?
3. Check reserves: are they adequate to withstand capital flight?
4. Evaluate competitiveness: is the real exchange rate aligned with fundamentals?
5. Review history: has the country maintained pegs before?

### When a crisis hits a pegged rate:
- If fundamentals are sound and the attack is speculative → defend with rate hikes and intervention
- If fundamentals are inconsistent with the peg → adjust the peg or float
- If balance sheet mismatches exist → float may trigger deeper crisis; consider capital controls
- If reserves are nearly exhausted → orderly devaluation is better than chaotic collapse

### When evaluating capital account liberalization:
| Condition | Liberalize? | Proceed with caution if |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| Strong financial regulation | Yes | Weak supervision |
| Credible exchange rate | Yes | Peg under pressure |
| Sustainable fiscal position | Yes | High deficits |
| Trade already liberalized | Yes | Trade restrictions remain |
| Macroprudential tools ready | Yes | No tools to manage inflows |

## Regime Comparison Matrix

| Regime | Exchange Rate Stability | Policy Autonomy | Crisis Type | Best For |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| Gold Standard | High | Very low | Banking panics, deflation | Countries with flexible labor markets, limited democracy |
| Bretton Woods | Medium | Medium (with controls) | Balance-of-payments crises | Countries prioritizing trade growth over financial integration |
| Floating | Low | High | Currency volatility, asset bubbles | Large, financially developed economies |
| Currency Board | Very high | None | Sudden stops, banking crises | Small open economies with strong institutions |
| Monetary Union | Very high (internal) | None | Sovereign debt crises, internal imbalances | Countries with deep economic and political integration |

## Key Thresholds & Benchmarks

- **Reserves**: 3 months of imports = minimum; 6+ months = comfortable
- **Current account deficit**: >5% of GDP = danger zone for emerging markets
- **Short-term debt/reserves**: >1 = vulnerability to rollover crisis
- **Fiscal deficit**: >3% of GDP (Maastricht) or >6-8% = unsustainable with market financing
- **Inflation targeting**: 2% = standard target for advanced economies
- **ERM band**: 2.25% = narrow; 15% = wide (post-1993)
- **Gold coverage**: Pre-1914 central banks held 30-50% gold backing

## Tells & Smells

- If a country with a peg has rising inflation while its trading partners don't → real appreciation → competitiveness problem
- If forward market shows persistent premium over peg → markets expect devaluation
- If short-term debt exceeds reserves → rollover crisis risk
- If banks borrow short-term in foreign currency → balance sheet crisis risk
- If a government says "we will never devalue" → devaluation may be imminent
- If the reserve currency country runs persistent deficits → Triffin dilemma in action

## Euro Crisis Diagnostic

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Policy Response |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Rising spreads on peripheral debt | Loss of confidence, competitiveness gap | Austerity, structural reform, ECB support |
| Target2 imbalances | Capital flight from periphery to core | ECB liquidity provision |
| Banking stress | Sovereign-bank doom loop | Bank recapitalization, resolution |
| Recession + austerity | Internal devaluation without exchange rate adjustment | Fiscal transfer, debt restructuring, or exit |
| Political radicalization | Economic pain from adjustment | Growth-oriented policies, debt relief |

## Quick Reference: The Trilemma

```
        Fixed Exchange Rate
               /\
              /  \
             /    \
            /      \
           /        \
          /          \
         /            \
Capital Mobility ---- Monetary Independence

Choose two:
- Fixed + Capital mobility → No monetary independence (currency board, euro)
- Fixed + Monetary independence → Capital controls (Bretton Woods, China)
- Capital mobility + Monetary independence → Float (U.S., Japan, UK post-1992)
```
