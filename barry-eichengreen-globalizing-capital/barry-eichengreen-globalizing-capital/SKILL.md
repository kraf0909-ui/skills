## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. The Polanyi-Eichengreen Thesis: The Incompatible Trinity
Use when analyzing why monetary systems succeed or fail.
- Democracy + Capital Mobility + Fixed Exchange Rates = Unstable
- In the 19th century, limited democracy allowed fixed rates with capital mobility
- In the 20th century, democratization forced a choice: restrict capital or float
- Post-1973: Most advanced countries chose floating; Europe chose monetary union

### 2. Network Externalities in Monetary Systems
Use when explaining why monetary arrangements display path dependence.
- Countries adopt the standard of their trading partners and creditors
- Britain's "accidental" gold standard (1717) set the trajectory for 150 years
- Coordination problems make collective reform difficult
- Reform requires exceptional political solidarity (Bretton Woods, EU)

### 3. The U-Shaped Pattern of Capital Mobility
Use when analyzing the relationship between capital flows and exchange rate regimes.
- High capital mobility before 1914 → fixed rates worked
- Low capital mobility 1945-1971 → fixed rates (Bretton Woods) worked
- High capital mobility post-1973 → fixed rates became problematic
- Capital mobility alone does not explain regime choice; political insulation matters

### 4. The Humean Adjustment Mechanism
Use when analyzing how balance-of-payments imbalances correct under commodity standards.
- Price-specie flow: gold flows out of deficit countries, in to surplus countries
- Deficit countries experience deflation; surplus countries experience inflation
- This restores competitiveness and eliminates the imbalance
- Requires flexible wages and prices; fails when labor markets are rigid

### 5. The Credibility-Commitment Framework
Use when analyzing why some pegged rates survive while others collapse.
- Pegged rates require credible commitment to defend the parity
- 19th century: Insulation from politics made commitment credible
- 20th century: Democratization made commitment less credible
- Capital controls substituted for limited democracy as insulation
- When controls became ineffective, pegged rates became unsustainable

### 6. The Lender of Last Resort Dilemma
Use when analyzing banking crises under fixed exchange rates.
- Central banks must choose: defend the currency peg or bail out banks
- Under gold standard: domestic liquidity vs. gold reserves
- International cooperation can resolve the dilemma (e.g., 1890 Baring Crisis)
- Without cooperation, the system is vulnerable to banking panics

### 7. The Adjustable Peg Oxymoron
Use when analyzing why Bretton Woods-style systems fail.
- Parity changes should be rare but possible
- In practice, any hint of devaluation provokes capital flight
- This makes governments reluctant to adjust, leading to overvaluation
- Eventually, speculative attacks force abrupt, disorderly adjustments

### 8. The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma)
Use when analyzing macroeconomic policy constraints.
- Countries cannot simultaneously have: fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility, independent monetary policy
- Must choose two of three
- Post-WWII: Many chose fixed rates + capital controls (sacrificing capital mobility)
- Post-1973: Advanced economies chose floating + capital mobility (sacrificing fixed rates)
- Eurozone: Chose fixed rates (monetary union) + capital mobility (sacrificing independent monetary policy)
