# Chapter 7: A Decade of Crises

## Core Idea
The period 2007-2018 was defined by two major crises: the Global Financial Crisis (originating in U.S. subprime mortgages but transmitted globally through financial linkages) and the Eurozone crisis (revealing the fatal design flaws of monetary union without fiscal union). Both crises highlighted the unresolved tensions in the international monetary system.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Subprime Transmission Mechanism**: How a U.S. housing market problem became a global financial crisis
  - When to use: Analyzing how financial crises spread across borders
  - How: Track the securitization chain — mortgages → MBS → CDOs → global investors; plus interbank lending and European bank exposure
- **The Euro Crisis as Balance-of-Payments Crisis**: Capital flows from core to periphery, then sudden stop
  - When to use: Analyzing the Eurozone crisis and its parallels with emerging market crises
  - How: Pre-crisis capital inflows to periphery fueled booms; post-crisis sudden stop forced austerity; without exchange rate adjustment, only internal devaluation remains
- **Currency Wars**: Competitive monetary easing by advanced countries that spills over to emerging markets
  - When to use: Analyzing spillovers from unconventional monetary policy
  - How: QE in advanced countries drives capital to emerging markets, causing appreciation and asset bubbles; emerging markets respond with capital controls or competitive easing

## Key Concepts
- **Subprime mortgages**: Loans to borrowers with poor credit; repackaged into securities
- **Securitization**: Converting loans into tradable securities
- **Shadow banking**: Non-bank financial intermediation outside traditional regulation
- **Too big to fail**: Institutions whose collapse would threaten systemic stability
- **Quantitative easing (QE)**: Central bank purchases of assets to inject liquidity
- **Troika**: IMF, European Commission, ECB — the three institutions managing euro bailouts
- **Grexit**: Potential Greek exit from the euro
- **Target2 imbalances**: Payment system imbalances within the Eurozone reflecting capital flight
- **Currency swap lines**: Central bank agreements to provide foreign currency liquidity

## Mental Models
- Think of the GFC as a crisis of financial globalization — U.S. innovations spread globally through integrated markets
- Use the "euro crisis as balance-of-payments crisis" lens to see parallels with emerging market crises
- View currency wars as a symptom of the impossible trinity: when everyone eases simultaneously, emerging markets face impossible choices

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming the GFC solely on U.S. subprime**: European banks were equally exposed and leveraged
- **Attributing the euro crisis solely to Greek profligacy**: Private capital flows from core to periphery were the root cause
- **Ignoring China's role**: China's reserve accumulation and currency policy shaped global imbalances

## Worked Example
The euro crisis (Greece):
- Pre-crisis: Capital flowed from Germany/France to Greece, fueling a consumption boom; Greek wages and prices rose, losing competitiveness
- 2009: New government revealed hidden deficits; deficit was 15.4% of GDP, not 3.7%
- Spread: Contagion to Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Italy — all faced rising spreads
- Troika response: Bailout conditional on austerity (tax hikes, spending cuts, structural reforms)
- Greek outcome: GDP fell 25%; unemployment rose to 27%; debt-to-GDP rose despite austerity
- Design flaw: No lender of last resort for sovereigns; no fiscal transfer mechanism; no exit procedure
- Lesson: Monetary union without fiscal union, banking union, or political union is dangerously fragile

## Key Takeaways
1. The GFC originated in U.S. subprime but spread globally through securitization and interbank markets
2. European banks were as exposed as U.S. banks, making it a truly global crisis
3. The euro crisis revealed fatal design flaws: no lender of last resort for sovereigns, no fiscal union, no exit mechanism
4. Greece's crisis was a balance-of-payments crisis disguised as a sovereign debt crisis
5. Unconventional monetary policy (QE) spilled over to emerging markets, provoking "currency war" complaints
6. China's rise changed the dynamics of the international monetary system
7. Digital currencies and China's e-yuan raised questions about the future of the dollar-based system
