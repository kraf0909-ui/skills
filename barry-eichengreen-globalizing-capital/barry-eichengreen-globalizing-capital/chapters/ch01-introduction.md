# Chapter 1: Introduction

## Core Idea
The international monetary system is the glue that binds national economies together. Its evolution cannot be understood through economic theory alone — it is fundamentally a historical process shaped by political economy, network externalities, and the tension between economic liberalization and democratization.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Polanyi-Eichengreen Thesis**: Democratization conflicts with fixed exchange rates and free capital mobility
  - When to use: Analyzing why monetary systems collapse or transform
  - How: Examine the political insulation of policymakers; when democracy expands, fixed rates become harder to maintain
- **Network Externalities in Monetary Systems**: The value of a monetary standard depends on how many others use it
  - When to use: Explaining path dependence and coordination failures in monetary reform
  - How: Identify the dominant standard; understand why individual countries rationally follow it even if a superior alternative exists
- **U-Shaped Capital Mobility**: International capital mobility was high before 1914, low mid-century, high again post-1970s
  - When to use: Evaluating claims that capital mobility alone explains exchange rate regime choice
  - How: Compare the political and institutional context across eras, not just capital flow volumes

## Key Concepts
- **Embedded liberalism**: Free markets tempered by commitment to social welfare and full employment (John Ruggie)
- **Path dependence**: History constrains future options; reform is a collective endeavor with high negotiating costs
- **International capital mobility**: The ease with which financial capital crosses borders
- **Pegged vs. floating exchange rates**: The fundamental regime choice, with tradeoffs shaped by capital mobility and political constraints

## Mental Models
- Think of monetary systems as operating systems with network effects — Windows vs. Linux analogy
- Use the "insulation from politics" lens to compare the viability of the same regime across different eras
- View the impossible trinity (fixed rates, capital mobility, monetary independence) as a binding constraint, not a suggestion

## Anti-patterns
- **Capital mobility determinism**: Assuming high capital mobility automatically forces floating rates (ignores the pre-1914 experience)
- **Ignoring politics**: Treating monetary arrangements as purely technical rather than politically contingent
- **Reform optimism**: Underestimating the coordination problems that block collective monetary reform

## Key Takeaways
1. International capital mobility follows a U-shape over the last 150 years
2. Capital mobility alone does not determine exchange rate regime choice — politics matters
3. The 19th-century gold standard worked because governments were insulated from democratic pressure
4. 20th-century democratization made fixed rates harder to sustain
5. Capital controls substituted for limited democracy as insulation after WWII
6. Monetary systems exhibit strong network externalities and path dependence
7. Successful reform requires exceptional political solidarity (Bretton Woods, EU)
