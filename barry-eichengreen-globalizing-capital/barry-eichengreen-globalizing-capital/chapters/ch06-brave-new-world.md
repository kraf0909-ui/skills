# Chapter 6: A Brave New Monetary World

## Core Idea
The period from 1997-2007 was exceptionally turbulent: the Asian financial crisis, emerging market defaults, European monetary unification, and the rise of global imbalances. Countries responded with greater exchange rate flexibility, inflation targeting, and regional financial arrangements — but underlying vulnerabilities remained.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Asian Crisis Model**: Maturity mismatch and currency mismatch in balance sheets
  - When to use: Analyzing emerging market financial crises
  - How: Identify when banks/firms borrow short-term in foreign currency but lend/invest long-term in domestic currency
- **Inflation Targeting as Exchange Rate Substitute**: When the exchange rate can no longer anchor expectations, inflation targeting provides an alternative nominal anchor
  - When to use: Analyzing monetary policy frameworks in emerging markets post-1997
  - How: Central banks announce inflation target, publish forecasts, explain policy decisions in terms of target consistency
- **Global Imbalances**: Chronic current account deficits in some countries matched by surpluses in others
  - When to use: Analyzing the sustainability of international payments patterns
  - How: Track current account balances; identify structural vs. cyclical components

## Key Concepts
- **Original sin**: Emerging markets' inability to borrow abroad in their own currency
- **Maturity mismatch**: Borrowing short-term, lending long-term
- **Currency mismatch**: Borrowing in foreign currency, earning in domestic currency
- **Contagion**: Crisis in one country spreading to others with similar vulnerabilities
- **IMF conditionality**: Policy requirements attached to IMF lending
- **Chiang Mai Initiative (2000)**: Asian regional swap arrangement
- **Euro (1999)**: Single European currency replacing national currencies for 11 initial members
- **Stability and Growth Pact**: Fiscal rules for euro area members
- **Dollarization**: Adopting the U.S. dollar as domestic currency

## Mental Models
- Think of the Asian crisis as a balance sheet crisis, not just a currency crisis — the currency collapse made existing balance sheet mismatches catastrophic
- Use "original sin" to understand why emerging markets are inherently vulnerable to external shocks
- View inflation targeting as a second-best solution when fixed rates are no longer credible

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming the IMF alone for crisis severity**: Countries' own policy choices created the vulnerabilities
- **Attributing the Asian crisis to crony capitalism alone**: Balance sheet mismatches were the proximate cause
- **Ignoring the euro's design flaws**: The Stability and Growth Pact was too rigid; there was no lender of last resort for sovereigns

## Worked Example
The 1997 Asian crisis (Thailand):
- Context: Thai banks borrowed short-term in dollars, lent long-term in baht for property and industrial projects
- Trigger: Property bubble burst; non-performing loans rose
- Speculative attack: Investors doubted Thailand's ability to maintain the baht peg
- Defense: Central bank spent $30 billion in reserves; raised interest rates
- Outcome: July 2, 1997 — Thailand floated the baht; it fell 20% immediately
- Contagion: Crisis spread to Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, others
- Lesson: Currency pegs + balance sheet mismatches = explosive vulnerability

## Key Takeaways
1. The Asian crisis (1997) revealed the dangers of currency and maturity mismatches
2. "Original sin" — emerging markets' inability to borrow in their own currency — creates inherent vulnerability
3. Countries responded with greater exchange rate flexibility and inflation targeting
4. The euro's launch (1999) was unprecedented in monetary history
5. Global imbalances (U.S. deficits, Chinese surpluses) grew to unprecedented levels
6. The Chiang Mai Initiative showed regional responses to global system gaps
7. The apparent calm of 2002-2007 masked growing financial vulnerabilities
