# Chapter 2: The Gold Standard

## Core Idea
The classical gold standard (1870s-1914) was not a deliberately designed system but emerged spontaneously through network externalities. Its viability depended on specific historical conditions: limited democracy, flexible labor markets, and international solidarity in crises. The rise of fractional reserve banking exposed its Achilles' heel — the lender of last resort dilemma.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Humean Price-Specie Flow Mechanism**: Automatic balance-of-payments adjustment under commodity money
  - When to use: Analyzing how fixed-rate systems correct external imbalances
  - How: Gold flows out of deficit countries, causing deflation; into surplus countries, causing inflation; this restores competitiveness
- **The Lender of Last Resort Dilemma**: Central banks must choose between defending the currency peg and preventing banking panics
  - When to use: Analyzing banking crises under fixed exchange rates
  - How: Raising rates attracts gold but hurts banks; lowering rates saves banks but drains gold reserves
- **Gold Devices**: Techniques central banks used to manipulate gold flows without formally breaking the standard
  - When to use: Understanding how countries maintained the facade of convertibility while managing practical constraints
  - How: Interest-free loans to gold importers, redeeming notes only at central offices, varying bar quality

## Key Concepts
- **Bimetallism**: Monetary standard using both gold and silver; vulnerable to arbitrage when market and mint ratios diverge
- **Gresham's Law**: "Bad money drives out good" — when two currencies circulate, the overvalued one dominates
- **Convertibility**: The ability to exchange currency for gold at a fixed price
- **Gold points**: Exchange rate boundaries beyond which gold arbitrage becomes profitable
- **Limping gold standard**: Silver remains legal tender but is no longer freely coined (e.g., France)
- **Latin Monetary Union (1865)**: Coordination attempt among Belgium, France, Italy, Switzerland to harmonize coinage
- **Scandinavian Monetary Union (1873)**: Denmark, Norway, Sweden recognized each other's currencies as legal tender

## Mental Models
- Think of the gold standard as a self-organizing system, not a designed one — Britain's "accidental" adoption via Newton's 1717 mint ratio set the trajectory
- Use the "Humean mechanism" as a benchmark: how well does any fixed-rate system replicate this automatic adjustment?
- View the lender of last resort dilemma as the fundamental tension in any fixed-rate system with fractional reserve banking

## Anti-patterns
- **Idealizing the gold standard**: Ignoring that it depended on undemocratic politics and flexible labor markets
- **Attributing the shift to gold to silver discoveries alone**: Network externalities and British economic dominance were the primary drivers
- **Ignoring the role of luck**: The system's survival required international solidarity in crises, which was not guaranteed

## Worked Example
The 1890 Baring Crisis:
- Problem: British merchant bank Baring Brothers faced collapse due to Argentine loan defaults
- Threat: Potential banking panic and gold outflow from Britain
- Response: Bank of England organized an international rescue with support from Bank of Russia and Bank of France
- Result: Crisis contained without breaking the gold standard
- Lesson: International solidarity could resolve the lender of last resort dilemma

## Key Takeaways
1. The gold standard emerged spontaneously, not by design — Britain's "accidental" adoption in 1717 was pivotal
2. Network externalities drove other countries to follow Britain's lead
3. The system required: limited democracy, flexible labor markets, open trade, and international solidarity
4. Fractional reserve banking created the lender of last resort dilemma
5. Gold devices allowed central banks to manage the standard flexibly without formally abandoning it
6. The prewar system's stability was historically contingent, not inherent to the gold standard itself
