# Chapter 5: After Bretton Woods

## Core Idea
The collapse of Bretton Woods in 1973 transformed international monetary affairs. Countries faced a trilemma: they could not simultaneously maintain fixed exchange rates, free capital mobility, and independent monetary policy. Different regions made different choices — the U.S. and Japan floated; developing countries maintained pegs with controls; Europe pursued monetary integration.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma)**: Fixed exchange rate + free capital mobility + independent monetary policy = pick two
  - When to use: Analyzing any country's macroeconomic policy constraints
  - How: Identify which two the country has chosen; the third is sacrificed
- **The European Snake (1972)**: Early European attempt to limit exchange rate fluctuations
  - When to use: Understanding the origins of European monetary integration
  - How: A narrow band (2.25%) within which European currencies fluctuated against each other
- **The European Monetary System (EMS, 1979)**: More structured arrangement with the ecu and Exchange Rate Mechanism
  - When to use: Analyzing how Europe attempted to maintain stable intra-European rates while floating against the dollar
  - How: Grid of bilateral parities; financing facilities; periodic realignments

## Key Concepts
- **Floating exchange rates**: Allowed to vary according to market forces; "clean" vs. "dirty" (managed) float
- **Capital account liberalization**: Progressive removal of controls on international financial flows
- **Eurodollar markets**: Offshore dollar markets that circumvented national controls
- **Currency substitution**: Use of foreign currency in place of domestic currency
- **Plaza Accord (1985)**: G5 agreement to depreciate the dollar
- **Louvre Accord (1987)**: Attempt to stabilize exchange rates after the Plaza depreciation
- **Black Wednesday (1992)**: Sterling forced out of the ERM by speculative attack

## Mental Models
- Think of the post-Bretton Woods era as a natural experiment in regime choice under the trilemma
- Use the "impossible trinity" as a diagnostic tool: any country claiming to have all three is hiding something
- View European monetary integration as an attempt to solve the trilemma by eliminating intra-European exchange rates

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming floating eliminates external constraints**: Floating countries still face constraints through trade and capital flows
- **Ignoring the political economy of regime choice**: Technical arguments often mask distributional conflicts
- **Overstating the stability of the EMS**: The 1992-93 crises revealed its vulnerabilities

## Worked Example
The 1992 ERM crisis:
- Context: German reunification required high interest rates; other ERM members faced recession
- Tension: Countries couldn't lower rates without breaking their pegs to the Deutsche Mark
- Attack: Speculators targeted the pound, lira, and other currencies perceived as overvalued
- Defense: Britain raised rates to 15% and spent £27 billion in reserves
- Outcome: Britain and Italy suspended ERM membership; bands widened from 2.25% to 15%
- Lesson: Pegged rates are unsustainable when domestic and foreign economic conditions diverge significantly

## Key Takeaways
1. The collapse of Bretton Woods forced countries to confront the impossible trinity
2. Large countries (U.S., Japan) chose floating + capital mobility
3. Developing countries mostly chose fixed rates + capital controls
4. Europe chose to eliminate intra-European exchange rates through the Snake, then EMS
5. The EMS worked while economic conditions were similar across members; German reunification exposed its fragility
6. The 1992-93 crises forced a choice: wider bands or monetary union
7. Countries that chose monetary union (euro) sacrificed independent monetary policy
