# Chapter 3: Interwar Instability

## Core Idea
The interwar period demonstrates what happens when a monetary system designed for one political and economic environment is reconstructed in a fundamentally changed world. The revived gold standard of the 1920s lacked the credibility, flexibility, and international solidarity of its prewar predecessor, making it vulnerable to shocks that ultimately produced the Great Depression.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Adjustable Peg Oxymoron**: Parity changes should be rare but possible; in practice, any hint of adjustment provokes capital flight
  - When to use: Analyzing why Bretton Woods-style systems collapse
  - How: Recognize that the mere discussion of devaluation can trigger speculative attacks, making orderly adjustment impossible
- **Competitive Devaluation**: Countries devalue to gain trade advantage, provoking retaliation
  - When to use: Analyzing 1930s trade wars and currency conflicts
  - How: One country's devaluation compresses its import demand, hurting trading partners; they respond with their own devaluations or tariffs
- **Credibility Gap**: The interwar gold standard lacked credibility because governments were no longer insulated from political pressure
  - When to use: Comparing why the pre-1914 gold standard worked but the interwar version failed
  - How: Examine whether policymakers can credibly commit to defending the parity regardless of domestic costs

## Key Concepts
- **Sterling's overvaluation**: Britain returned to gold in 1925 at the prewar parity, rendering British exports uncompetitive
- **War debts and reparations**: Inter-allied debts distorted international capital flows and political relations
- **Beggar-thy-neighbor policies**: Tariffs and devaluations that export unemployment to trading partners
- **Banking crises**: The U.S. banking panics of 1930-1933 transmitted distress globally through the gold standard
- **Gold bloc**: Countries (France, Belgium, Netherlands, Switzerland, Poland) that clung to gold after Britain departed in 1931
- **Managed floating**: The regime that emerged after the gold standard's final collapse in 1936

## Mental Models
- Think of the interwar gold standard as a fragile reconstruction — same architecture, weaker foundation
- Use the "credibility gap" to explain why identical rules produced different outcomes pre- and post-WWI
- View the Great Depression as a cascade: banking crisis → gold standard constraints → deflation → protectionism

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming the gold standard alone**: The problem was not gold but the political and economic context
- **Ignoring Britain's overvaluation**: Churchill's 1925 decision to return at the prewar parity was a critical error
- **Attributing the Depression to a single cause**: It was a complex interaction of monetary, banking, and trade factors

## Worked Example
Britain's 1931 departure from gold:
- Context: Overvalued sterling since 1925, rising unemployment, budget deficits during the Depression
- Trigger: May Committee recommended austerity (cutting unemployment benefits); Labour government split and collapsed
- Capital flight: Investors feared devaluation or default
- Response: National Government formed; sought international loans
- Outcome: Despite a $650 million international credit, pressure continued; Britain suspended gold convertibility on September 21, 1931
- Lesson: Once credibility is lost, even massive intervention may not save a peg

## Key Takeaways
1. The interwar gold standard failed because it lacked the political and economic preconditions of the prewar system
2. Britain's return to gold at the prewar parity (1925) created persistent overvaluation
3. War debts and reparations distorted international capital flows
4. The adjustable peg proved to be an oxymoron — any hint of adjustment provoked capital flight
5. Banking crises (especially in the U.S.) transmitted distress globally through gold standard constraints
6. Competitive devaluations and protectionism followed the gold standard's collapse
7. The lesson learned: future systems needed exchange rate flexibility and capital controls
