# Chapter 4: The Bretton Woods System

## Core Idea
The Bretton Woods system (1945-1971) was a deliberate attempt to design an international monetary system that learned from interwar failures. It featured adjustable pegs, capital controls, and an IMF — but the adjustable peg proved rigid, controls leaked, and the system's survival depended on the dollar's unique role, which eventually became its fatal flaw.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Embedded Liberalism**: Free markets tempered by commitment to full employment and social welfare
  - When to use: Understanding the political foundations of post-WWII economic arrangements
  - How: Recognize that Bretton Woods was designed to accommodate domestic policy priorities, not just international stability
- **The Adjustable Peg as Oxymoron**: In practice, parity changes were extraordinarily rare
  - When to use: Analyzing why supposedly flexible fixed-rate systems become rigid
  - How: Track the frequency of parity adjustments; when they become rare, the system loses its shock-absorption capacity
- **The Dollar's Exorbitant Privilege**: The U.S. could run deficits that other countries could not
  - When to use: Analyzing global imbalances and reserve currency dynamics
  - How: The reserve currency country faces no immediate balance-of-payments constraint because its liabilities are held as reserves by others

## Key Concepts
- **Adjustable peg**: Exchange rates fixed to the dollar but adjustable in cases of "fundamental disequilibrium"
- **Capital controls**: Permitted under Bretton Woods to insulate domestic policy from speculative flows
- **IMF surveillance**: Monitoring of national policies to discourage destabilizing behavior
- **Scarce currency clause**: Never-invoked provision allowing discrimination against countries whose policies caused systemic problems
- **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)**: Created in 1969 as a supplement to gold and dollar reserves
- **Gold pool**: 1960s arrangement where central banks cooperated to maintain the $35/ounce gold price
- **Triffin dilemma**: The conflict between domestic and international objectives for the reserve currency country
- **Sterling crisis (1967)**: Britain's devaluation revealed the system's vulnerability
- **Nixon shock (1971)**: Suspension of dollar-gold convertibility, effectively ending Bretton Woods

## Mental Models
- Think of Bretton Woods as a designed system learning from interwar failures — but design could not overcome inherent contradictions
- Use the "adjustable peg oxymoron" to understand why supposedly flexible systems become rigid
- View the Triffin dilemma as the fundamental contradiction of any reserve currency system

## Anti-patterns
- **Idealizing Bretton Woods**: Ignoring that it depended on capital controls and limited convertibility
- **Attributing collapse solely to U.S. profligacy**: The system's design made the Triffin dilemma inevitable
- **Ignoring the role of private markets**: Eurodollar markets circumvented controls and undermined the system

## Worked Example
The 1967 sterling crisis and the 1971 Nixon shock:
- 1967: Britain devalued sterling by 14.3% despite IMF support; revealed that even major currencies were vulnerable
- Late 1960s: U.S. Vietnam War spending and Great Society programs created persistent deficits
- 1971: Dollar overhang (more dollars abroad than U.S. gold reserves) made convertibility unsustainable
- August 15, 1971: Nixon suspended dollar-gold convertibility; imposed 10% import surcharge
- Result: Bretton Woods effectively ended; Smithsonian Agreement (December 1971) attempted salvage but failed within months
- Lesson: The reserve currency country cannot indefinitely run deficits without undermining confidence

## Key Takeaways
1. Bretton Woods was a deliberate design incorporating interwar lessons: adjustable pegs, capital controls, IMF
2. "Embedded liberalism" meant the system accommodated domestic priorities (full employment) over pure exchange rate stability
3. The adjustable peg proved to be an oxymoron — parity changes were rare and disruptive
4. Capital controls were essential but increasingly leaky, especially via Eurodollar markets
5. The Triffin dilemma made the system's collapse inevitable: the reserve currency country must run deficits to supply liquidity, but those deficits eventually undermine confidence
6. The dollar's "exorbitant privilege" allowed the U.S. to delay adjustment longer than other countries could
7. The system's end was messy — the Smithsonian Agreement failed within months of its creation
