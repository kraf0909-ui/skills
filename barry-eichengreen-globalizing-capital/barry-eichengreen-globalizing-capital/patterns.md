# Patterns

## Crisis Diagnosis Patterns

### Fixed-Rate Crisis Sequence
**When to use**: Analyzing speculative attacks on pegged exchange rates.
**How**:
1. Identify the peg and its sustainability (current account, reserves, competitiveness)
2. Detect early warning signals (reserve loss, interest rate spreads, forward market pressure)
3. Recognize the trigger (policy divergence, external shock, political crisis)
4. Observe the speculative attack (capital flight, reserve depletion)
5. Identify the outcome (successful defense, devaluation, float, or capital controls)
**Trade-offs**: Defending the peg may require painful interest rate hikes; abandoning it may damage credibility for years.

### Balance-of-Payments Crisis (First Generation)
**When to use**: Analyzing crises driven by inconsistent fiscal and monetary policy under a peg.
**How**:
1. Identify the inconsistency (expansionary fiscal/monetary policy + fixed exchange rate)
2. Track reserve losses as money creation outpaces demand
3. Recognize that the crisis timing is predictable based on reserve exhaustion
4. Understand that the attack occurs before reserves are literally zero (rational speculation)
**Trade-offs**: The crisis is inevitable once inconsistent policies are adopted; the only question is timing.

### Self-Fulfilling Crisis (Second Generation)
**When to use**: Analyzing crises where multiple equilibria exist.
**How**:
1. Identify that the peg is sustainable if markets believe it is, unsustainable if they don't
2. Recognize that the government's cost of defending the peg depends on market expectations
3. Understand that a shift in expectations can trigger a crisis even without fundamental deterioration
4. Note that multiple equilibria are more likely when reserves are low and unemployment is high
**Trade-offs**: Policy can influence which equilibrium prevails, but cannot always prevent a shift.

### Balance Sheet Crisis (Third Generation)
**When to use**: Analyzing crises driven by currency and maturity mismatches.
**How**:
1. Identify currency mismatch (foreign currency debt, domestic currency revenues)
2. Identify maturity mismatch (short-term borrowing, long-term investment)
3. Recognize that depreciation dramatically worsens balance sheets
4. Understand that the crisis can be triggered by even small exchange rate movements
5. Note that the crisis is deeper and more prolonged than pure speculative attacks
**Trade-offs**: Floating may prevent the crisis but exposes balance sheets; fixing prevents depreciation but invites attack.

## Regime Analysis Patterns

### The Impossible Trinity Diagnostic
**When to use**: Evaluating any country's macroeconomic policy framework.
**How**:
1. Identify the country's exchange rate regime (fixed, floating, or intermediate)
2. Assess the degree of capital account openness
3. Evaluate the independence of monetary policy
4. Check whether the country actually has what it claims
5. Identify which of the three is being sacrificed
**Trade-offs**: There is no free lunch — any claim to have all three is hiding something.

### Credibility Assessment for Pegged Rates
**When to use**: Evaluating whether a fixed exchange rate is sustainable.
**How**:
1. Assess political insulation: Can policymakers defend the peg regardless of domestic costs?
2. Check reserve adequacy: Are reserves sufficient to withstand capital flight?
3. Evaluate competitiveness: Is the real exchange rate aligned with fundamentals?
4. Examine fiscal sustainability: Is the government budget compatible with the peg?
5. Review the track record: Has the country defended pegs in the past?
**Trade-offs**: A credible peg provides stability but sacrifices policy flexibility; an incredible peg invites speculative attack.

### Capital Account Liberalization Sequence
**When to use**: Evaluating whether a country should liberalize capital flows.
**How**:
1. Assess domestic financial system strength (regulation, supervision, bank health)
2. Evaluate exchange rate regime credibility
3. Check fiscal sustainability
4. Consider the sequencing: trade liberalization first, then long-term capital, then short-term flows
5. Prepare macroprudential tools to manage inflows
**Trade-offs**: Liberalization brings efficiency gains but increases vulnerability to crises; sequencing matters.

## Historical Comparison Patterns

### Pre-1914 vs. Interwar Gold Standard Comparison
**When to use**: Understanding why identical rules produced different outcomes.
**How**:
1. Compare labor market flexibility (flexible pre-1914, rigid interwar)
2. Compare political insulation (limited democracy pre-1914, mass democracy interwar)
3. Compare international solidarity (cooperative pre-1914, adversarial interwar)
4. Compare financial structure (less leveraged pre-1914, more leveraged interwar)
5. Compare the role of the hegemon (Britain strong pre-1914, weakened interwar)
**Trade-offs**: The pre-1914 system was more stable but less democratic; the interwar system attempted to preserve democracy but couldn't sustain the gold standard.

### Bretton Woods vs. Post-1973 Comparison
**When to use**: Evaluating the tradeoffs between fixed and floating rates.
**How**:
1. Compare exchange rate volatility (lower under Bretton Woods, higher post-1973)
2. Compare policy autonomy (more constrained under Bretton Woods, more flexible post-1973)
3. Compare crisis frequency (different types: balance-of-payments crises vs. currency crises)
4. Compare inflation performance (better post-1973 with inflation targeting)
5. Compare trade growth (strong under both, but for different reasons)
**Trade-offs**: Fixed rates provide stability but constrain policy; floating provides flexibility but adds volatility.

## Policy Response Patterns

### Lender of Last Resort Decision
**When to use**: Central banks facing banking crises under fixed exchange rates.
**How**:
1. Assess the solvency of the distressed institution (illiquid vs. insolvent)
2. Evaluate the systemic importance (contagion risk)
3. Consider the exchange rate implications (will lending weaken the currency?)
4. Check for international support (can foreign central banks help?)
5. Decide: provide liquidity, arrange resolution, or let fail
**Trade-offs**: Lending may save the bank but weaken the currency; not lending may trigger systemic collapse.

### IMF Program Design
**When to use**: Countries seeking or receiving IMF support.
**How**:
1. Identify the root cause (fiscal, external, banking, structural)
2. Design conditionality addressing the root cause
3. Calibrate the pace of adjustment (too fast = recession; too slow = loss of credibility)
4. Ensure financing is sufficient to restore confidence
5. Build in structural reforms to prevent recurrence
**Trade-offs**: Too much conditionality = ownership problems; too little = moral hazard; too fast = unnecessary pain; too slow = program failure.

### Euro Crisis Response
**When to use**: Analyzing policy responses to sovereign debt crises in monetary unions.
**How**:
1. Recognize that standard tools (devaluation, independent monetary policy) are unavailable
2. Identify the choice: austerity (internal devaluation), bailout, or exit
3. Evaluate the feasibility of each option given political constraints
4. Consider the systemic implications (contagion risk to other members)
5. Assess the institutional constraints (no lender of last resort for sovereigns)
**Trade-offs**: Austerity deepens recession; bailouts create moral hazard; exit risks financial collapse.
