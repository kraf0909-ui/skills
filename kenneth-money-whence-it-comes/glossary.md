# Glossary

**Bagehot's dictum** — "Lend freely, at a penalty rate, on good collateral" — the foundational principle of central banking crisis response. (Ch 4)

**Balance of payments** — The record of a country's economic transactions with the rest of the world; under gold, deficits led to gold outflows. (Ch 10)

**Bank run** — When depositors simultaneously demand their money, forcing illiquid banks to fail. (Ch 3)

**Bimetallism** — A monetary standard based on two metals (typically gold and silver) at a fixed legal ratio. (Ch 2)

**Bretton Woods** — The 1944 conference that established the post-WWII international monetary order: fixed but adjustable exchange rates, IMF, World Bank. (Ch 17, Ch 18, Ch 20)

**Chartalism** — The theory that money derives its value from state acceptance for tax payments; foundation of Modern Monetary Theory. (Ch 1, Ch 5)

**Continental currency** — Paper money issued by the Continental Congress during the American Revolution; hyperinflated to near worthlessness. (Ch 6)

**Credit creation** — The process by which bank lending expands the money supply; banks create money, not just intermediate it. (Ch 3)

**Cross of Gold** — William Jennings Bryan's 1896 speech attacking the gold standard; symbol of the monetary class conflict. (Ch 9)

**Debasement** — Reducing the precious metal content of coins while maintaining face value; a standard fiscal tool of sovereigns. (Ch 2)

**Deflation** — A sustained decline in the general price level; increases the real burden of debt and enriches creditors. (Ch 9, Ch 13)

**Deposit insurance** — Government guarantee of bank deposits, designed to prevent bank runs. (Ch 3)

**Dollar supremacy** — The dollar's role as the world's primary reserve currency after WWII, giving the US unique privileges. (Ch 18)

**Effective demand** — The level of demand actually exercised in the economy; the central Keynesian concept. (Ch 16)

**Fiat money** — Money by government decree, not backed by or convertible to a physical commodity. (Ch 1, Ch 5, Ch 8)

**Financialisation** — The growing size and importance of financial markets and institutions relative to the real economy. (Ch 20)

**Fractional reserve banking** — The practice of keeping only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lending the remainder. (Ch 3)

**Free silver** — The demand to monetise silver at a 16:1 ratio to gold, expanding the money supply and relieving deflation. (Ch 9)

**Gold standard** — A monetary system in which currency is convertible to gold at a fixed rate. (Ch 2, Ch 10, Ch 11, Ch 13, Ch 15)

**Greenbacks** — Fiat paper money issued by the Union during the Civil War; not convertible to specie. (Ch 8)

**Gresham's Law** — "Bad money drives out good": when two monies circulate at a fixed legal ratio, the overvalued form displaces the undervalued. (Ch 2, Ch 5)

**Hyperinflation** — Extremely rapid price increases, typically exceeding 50% per month; Weimar Germany is the archetype. (Ch 6, Ch 12)

**IMF (International Monetary Fund)** — Created at Bretton Woods to provide liquidity and supervise exchange rates. (Ch 17)

**Keynesian economics** — The theory that aggregate demand determines output and employment, and that government can manage demand through fiscal policy. (Ch 16, Ch 17, Ch 19)

**Lender of last resort** — The central bank's role in providing liquidity to the banking system during crises. (Ch 4)

**Liquidationism** — The belief that depressions purge the economy of excesses and should not be interfered with. (Ch 13)

**Maturity transformation** — Borrowing short-term and lending long-term; the source of banking's utility and fragility. (Ch 3)

**National Banking Act (1863–1864)** — Created federally chartered banks and a uniform national currency backed by Treasury bonds. (Ch 8)

**Nixon shock (1971)** — Suspension of dollar convertibility to gold; end of Bretton Woods. (Ch 20)

**Open market operations** — Central bank buying and selling of government securities to influence the money supply. (Ch 4)

**Populism** — The agrarian political movement demanding monetary expansion, financial reform, and debt relief. (Ch 9)

**Rentenmark (1923)** — The German currency reform that ended hyperinflation by creating a new currency backed by real assets. (Ch 12)

**Reparations** — War damages imposed on Germany by the Treaty of Versailles; a key cause of Weimar hyperinflation. (Ch 12)

**Seigniorage** — The profit from issuing money, whether by minting coins or printing currency. (Ch 2)

**Specie** — Coin made of precious metal, especially gold or silver. (Ch 2)

**Stagflation** — The combination of high inflation and high unemployment; challenged Keynesian orthodoxy in the 1970s. (Ch 20)

**Token money** — Money whose value as a medium of exchange exceeds its intrinsic commodity value. (Ch 1)

**Triffin dilemma** — The conflict between supplying global dollar liquidity (requiring US deficits) and maintaining confidence in the dollar (requiring US surpluses). (Ch 18, Ch 20)

**Wampum** — Shell beads used as money by Native Americans and colonists in early America. (Ch 5)

**World Bank** — Created at Bretton Woods to finance post-war reconstruction and development. (Ch 17)
