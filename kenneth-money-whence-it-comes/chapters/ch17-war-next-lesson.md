# Chapter 17: War and the Next Lesson

## Core Idea
World War II confirmed Keynesian principles on a massive scale. The war demonstrated that a government willing to spend and borrow could eliminate unemployment and mobilise the economy. Price controls prevented inflation despite massive deficits. The post-war monetary order — Bretton Woods — was designed to prevent the monetary chaos of the interwar period.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **War as Keynesian Stimulus**: WWII was the ultimate demonstration of Keynesian principles. Massive government spending eliminated unemployment, raised output, and demonstrated that demand, not supply, was the binding constraint.
- **Price Controls as Inflation Prevention**: Despite massive deficits and money creation, inflation was contained by direct price controls. This showed that inflation was not an automatic consequence of deficit spending.
- **Bretton Woods as Compromise**: The post-war monetary order was a compromise between American desires for dollar supremacy and the need for international stability. It created the IMF, the World Bank, and a system of fixed but adjustable exchange rates.

## Key Concepts
- **War finance**: Massive government borrowing and money creation to fund military expenditure
- **Price controls**: Government limits on prices and wages to prevent inflation
- **Bretton Woods (1944)**: Conference that established the post-war international monetary order
- **IMF (International Monetary Fund)**: Created to provide liquidity and supervise exchange rates
- **World Bank**: Created to finance post-war reconstruction and development

## Mental Models
- Think of WWII as a demonstration that demand constraints, not supply constraints, were the binding limit in the 1930s
- Use the price control experience to understand that inflation is a political choice, not an automatic consequence of deficits

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming war prosperity proves Keynesianism works in peacetime**: War spending is different from civilian spending in its political feasibility and social effects
- **Ignoring the costs of price controls**: They create shortages, black markets, and distortions

## Key Takeaways
1. WWII demonstrated that massive government spending could eliminate unemployment
2. Price controls prevented inflation despite huge deficits
3. The war proved that demand, not supply, had been the binding constraint in the 1930s
4. Bretton Woods created a new international monetary order designed for stability
5. The post-war period was shaped by the lessons of the interwar monetary chaos
