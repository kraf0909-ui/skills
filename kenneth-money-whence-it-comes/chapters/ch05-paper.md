# Chapter 5: Of Paper

## Core Idea
Paper money was the great monetary innovation — and the great monetary scandal, in the eyes of orthodoxy. It separated money entirely from physical commodities and made its value purely a matter of state decree and social convention. The history of paper money is the history of governments learning to create and manage fiat currency.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fiat Money as Ultimate Abstraction**: Paper money completed the separation of money from commodity. Its value derives entirely from state decree and social acceptance, not from intrinsic worth.
- **The Colonial American Experience**: American colonies were laboratories of monetary innovation. Wampum, tobacco, and paper money all circulated because they solved practical problems of trade and taxation.
- **The Tax-Driven Demand for Money**: Paper money worked in the colonies because governments accepted it for taxes. This "chartalist" principle — that tax obligations create demand for state money — remains central to understanding fiat currency.

## Key Concepts
- **Fiat money**: Money by government decree, not convertible to a commodity
- **Wampum**: Shell beads used as money by Native Americans and colonists
- **Tobacco money**: Tobacco served as currency in colonial Virginia and Maryland
- **Colonial paper money**: Issued by colonial assemblies to finance government and stimulate trade
- **Chartalism**: The theory that money derives its value from state acceptance for tax payments

## Mental Models
- Think of paper money as a tax credit: it has value because the government accepts it for taxes
- Use the colonial experience to understand how money emerges from practical needs, not from top-down design

## Anti-patterns
- **Dismissing paper money as inherently unstable**: It has been stable when well-managed and unstable when poorly managed — like any monetary system
- **Assuming commodity money is "natural"**: All money is conventional; commodity money just hides the convention behind a shiny metal

## Key Takeaways
1. Paper money completed the separation of money from physical commodities
2. American colonies were laboratories of monetary innovation (wampum, tobacco, paper)
3. Tax acceptance was the foundation of paper money's value
4. Colonial paper money was often successful but was repeatedly restricted by British authorities
5. The history of paper money is deeply political — about who controls money creation
