# Chapter 6: An Instrument of Revolution

## Core Idea
The American Revolution was financed primarily by paper money — the Continental currency. This was not a mistake but a necessity: a revolutionary government without taxing power or foreign credit had no alternative. The hyperinflation of the Continentals was the price of independence, and it was "effective and indispensable."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Inflation as the Tax of Revolution**: When a government cannot tax or borrow conventionally, the printing press is the only available instrument. The Continental Congress issued paper money because it had no other way to pay for the war.
- **The Political Economy of Hyperinflation**: Hyperinflation is not merely an economic phenomenon but a political one. It occurs when a government's survival depends on continued money creation and when there is no credible commitment to future stability.
- **The Conventional Condemnation**: Orthodox historians condemn the Continental currency as a failure, but Galbraith argues it was a success: it financed a war that could not have been financed otherwise.

## Key Concepts
- **Continental currency**: Paper money issued by the Continental Congress during the American Revolution
- **Hyperinflation**: Extremely rapid price increases caused by excessive money creation
- "**Not worth a Continental**": The phrase describing the currency's near-total loss of value
- **Currency depreciation**: The decline in purchasing power of paper money relative to specie
- **Price controls**: Attempts to limit price increases by legal decree

## Mental Models
- Think of revolutionary inflation as a war tax levied on money holders: it is unjust but may be the only available means
- Use the "effective and indispensable" frame when evaluating inflationary finance in existential crises

## Anti-patterns
- **Condemning Continental currency without proposing alternatives**: There was no alternative source of finance
- **Assuming hyperinflation is always avoidable**: It may be the least bad option in extreme circumstances

## Key Takeaways
1. The American Revolution was financed primarily by paper money issuance
2. The Continental currency hyperinflated — "not worth a Continental" — but it financed independence
3. Inflation was the tax of last resort for a government without conventional revenue
4. Orthodox condemnation ignores the absence of alternatives
5. The episode established a pattern: American crises would repeatedly be met with paper money
