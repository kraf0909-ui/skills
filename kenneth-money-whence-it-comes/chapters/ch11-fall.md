# Chapter 11: The Fall

## Core Idea
World War I destroyed the classical gold standard. All belligerents suspended convertibility, printed money, and borrowed massively. The post-war attempt to restore gold at the pre-war parity was economically damaging and ultimately futile. The interwar period was a monetary chaos of floating rates, competitive devaluations, and failed restoration attempts.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **War as Monetary Revolution**: WWI forced every belligerent off the gold standard, into paper money, and into massive debt. The monetary world that emerged was fundamentally different from the one that had existed in 1914.
- **The Folly of Restoration**: Attempting to restore the gold standard at the pre-war parity imposed crippling deflation on countries (especially Britain) whose price levels had risen during the war. Churchill's 1925 decision to return Britain to gold at the old rate was, in Galbraith's judgment, "the most decisively damaging action involving money in modern times."
- **Interwar Monetary Chaos**: The restored gold standard was unstable, inequitable, and short-lived. Competitive devaluations, exchange controls, and protectionism were the inevitable consequences.

## Key Concepts
- **Gold standard suspension (1914)**: All major powers halted gold convertibility at the outbreak of war
- **War finance**: Massive money creation and borrowing to fund military expenditure
- **Restoration (1925)**: Britain's return to gold at the pre-war parity, followed by other countries
- **Competitive devaluation**: Countries devaluing their currencies to gain export advantage
- **Exchange controls**: Government restrictions on currency conversion and capital movements

## Mental Models
- Think of WWI as a monetary Big Bang: the old universe was destroyed and a new one emerged
- Use the restoration folly to understand why returning to old parities after inflation is economically destructive

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming the gold standard could have been restored successfully**: The war had changed relative prices and economic structures too profoundly
- **Ignoring the distributional effects of restoration**: It imposed deflationary pain on workers and debtors

## Key Takeaways
1. WWI destroyed the classical gold standard; all belligerents suspended convertibility
2. Post-war attempts to restore gold at pre-war parities were economically damaging
3. Britain's 1925 return to gold at the old rate imposed severe deflation
4. The restored gold standard was unstable and inequitable
5. The interwar period was a monetary chaos that ended with the final abandonment of gold
