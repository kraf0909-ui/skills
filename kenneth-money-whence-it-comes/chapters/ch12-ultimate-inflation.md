# Chapter 12: The Ultimate Inflation

## Core Idea
Weimar Germany's hyperinflation (1921–1923) was the most spectacular monetary collapse in history. It was caused by the combination of war reparations, political instability, and the Reichsbank's willingness to monetise government deficits. The result was the destruction of savings, the middle class, and social stability — paving the way for fascism.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hyperinflation as Political Collapse**: Weimar hyperinflation was not merely an economic phenomenon but a political one. The government could not tax adequately, could not borrow, and chose to print rather than default on reparations.
- **The Reparations Trap**: The Versailles reparations burden made fiscal balance impossible. The German government faced a choice between defaulting on reparations, defaulting on domestic obligations, or printing money. It chose the latter.
- **The Destruction of the Middle Class**: Hyperinflation wiped out savings, destroyed the social foundation of democracy, and created the conditions for extremism. Monetary collapse became political collapse.

## Key Concepts
- **Hyperinflation**: Price increases exceeding 50% per month; in Weimar, prices doubled every few days at the peak
- **Reparations**: War damages imposed on Germany by the Treaty of Versailles
- **Reichsbank**: The German central bank, which monetised government deficits
- **Rentenmark (1923)**: The currency reform that ended hyperinflation by creating a new currency backed by real assets
- **Dawes Plan (1924)**: Restructured reparations and provided foreign loans to stabilise Germany

## Mental Models
- Think of hyperinflation as a symptom of state collapse, not a cause: the state has lost the capacity to tax and borrow
- Use the reparations trap to understand how external obligations can make domestic monetary stability impossible

## Anti-patterns
- **Attributing hyperinflation solely to printing money**: The printing was a response to an impossible fiscal situation
- **Ignoring the political consequences**: The destruction of savings created the social basis for Nazism

## Key Takeaways
1. Weimar hyperinflation was the most spectacular monetary collapse in history
2. It was caused by the combination of reparations, political instability, and deficit monetisation
3. The Reichsbank printed money because the alternative was defaulting on reparations or domestic debt
4. Hyperinflation destroyed savings, the middle class, and democratic stability
5. The Rentenmark reform and Dawes Plan eventually stabilised the currency
