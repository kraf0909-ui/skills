# Chapter 9: The Price

## Core Idea
The post-Civil War period saw a sustained deflation as the United States returned to the gold standard. This enriched creditors at the expense of debtors, particularly farmers, and provoked the Populist and free silver movements. The "Cross of Gold" speech and the 1896 election were the climactic political battles over monetary policy.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Deflation as Class War**: Sustained deflation transfers wealth from debtors to creditors. In an agricultural economy, farmers were debtors; bankers and bondholders were creditors. The gold standard was a class weapon.
- **The Populist Response**: The free silver movement sought to expand the money supply by monetising silver at a ratio favourable to silver. This was not economic illiteracy but a rational response to deflationary oppression.
- **The Cross of Gold**: William Jennings Bryan's 1896 speech crystallised the monetary conflict. His defeat marked the temporary victory of the gold standard and creditor interests.

## Key Concepts
- **Resumption of specie payments (1879)**: Return to gold convertibility after the Civil War suspension
- **Deflation (1865–1896)**: Sustained decline in the price level, increasing the real burden of debt
- **Free silver**: The demand to monetise silver at a 16:1 ratio to gold, expanding the money supply
- **Populist Party**: Agrarian political movement demanding monetary expansion and financial reform
- **Cross of Gold speech (1896)**: Bryan's famous oration against the gold standard

## Mental Models
- Think of the gold standard as a distributional choice, not a technical one: it favoured creditors over debtors
- Use the deflation framework to understand why debtors demand monetary expansion

## Anti-patterns
- **Dismissing free silver as economic ignorance**: It was a rational response to sustained deflation
- **Assuming the gold standard was neutral**: It had profound distributional consequences

## Key Takeaways
1. Post-Civil War deflation enriched creditors and impoverished debtors, especially farmers
2. The Populist and free silver movements were rational responses to deflationary oppression
3. Bryan's "Cross of Gold" speech (1896) crystallised the monetary class conflict
4. McKinley's victory and subsequent gold discoveries ended the free silver movement
5. The episode reveals monetary policy as inherently distributional and political
