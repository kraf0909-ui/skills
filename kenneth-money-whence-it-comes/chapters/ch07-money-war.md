# Chapter 7: The Money War

## Core Idea
The early American republic was shaped by a political war over money and banking. Alexander Hamilton's vision of a strong central bank, national debt, and creditor-class dominance prevailed over Thomas Jefferson's agrarian, anti-bank populism — but the conflict persisted for decades, setting the pattern for American monetary politics.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hamilton vs. Jefferson as Monetary Conflict**: This was not merely an economic debate but a struggle over the distribution of power. Hamilton's system favoured creditors, merchants, and manufacturers; Jefferson's favoured farmers and debtors.
- **The First and Second Banks of the United States**: These were proto-central banks that managed government finance, issued currency, and regulated state banks. Their creation and destruction reflected the shifting political balance.
- **The Political Economy of Banking**: Who controls the banks controls the allocation of credit and thus the distribution of economic power. Banking is never neutral.

## Key Concepts
- **First Bank of the United States (1791–1811)**: Hamilton's creation; modelled on the Bank of England; charter not renewed
- **Second Bank of the United States (1816–1836)**: Recreated after War of 1812; destroyed by Andrew Jackson
- **Bank War (1832–1836)**: Jackson's veto of the Bank recharter and removal of government deposits
- **State banks (pet banks)**: Jackson deposited government funds in state banks, contributing to the speculative boom and subsequent panic

## Mental Models
- Think of banking politics as a struggle between creditors and debtors: tight money favours creditors; easy money favours debtors
- Use the Hamilton-Jefferson framework to understand why American monetary politics has always been polarised

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming the Bank War was about abstract principles**: It was about power — who would control credit and the economy
- **Ignoring the distributional consequences**: Monetary policy always benefits some groups at the expense of others

## Key Takeaways
1. Hamilton created a national bank, national debt, and creditor-class dominance
2. Jefferson and Jackson opposed central banking as anti-republican and pro-elite
3. The First Bank was not renewed; the Second Bank was destroyed by Jackson
4. Jackson's "pet bank" policy contributed to the speculative boom of the 1830s
5. The pattern was set: American banking politics would be a recurring battle between financial elites and populist opponents
