# Chapter 10: The Impeccable System

## Core Idea
The classical gold standard (1870–1914) was the apotheosis of monetary orthodoxy — "impeccable" in its logical consistency, devastating in its real-world consequences. It enforced price stability (and often deflation), constrained government action, and served creditor interests. Its apparent success was purchased at the cost of suppressed wages, limited social provision, and recurrent crises.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Gold Standard as Creditor Orthodoxy**: The classical gold standard was not a neutral technical arrangement but a political choice that served the interests of creditors, rentiers, and the financial class. Its "rules of the game" required deflationary adjustment by debtor countries.
- **The Adjustment Mechanism**: Under the gold standard, balance-of-payments deficits led to gold outflows, monetary contraction, and deflation. This corrected the deficit but at enormous human cost — unemployment, wage cuts, and social unrest.
- **The Myth of Automatic Stability**: The gold standard did not self-correct smoothly. It required central bank cooperation, capital controls, and frequent suspension of rules. Its stability was partly illusory.

## Key Concepts
- **Classical gold standard**: International monetary system linking currencies to gold at fixed rates (c. 1870–1914)
- **Rules of the game**: The supposed obligation to contract the money supply when losing gold, expand when gaining it
- **Balance of payments**: The flow of gold between countries reflecting trade and capital movements
- **Rentier**: A person living on interest or investment income; the class that benefited most from price stability
- **Hume's price-specie flow mechanism**: The theoretical automatic adjustment process under gold

## Mental Models
- Think of the gold standard as a straitjacket: it prevents inflation but also prevents response to depression
- Use the creditor-debtor framework to understand who benefits from and who suffers under gold

## Anti-patterns
- **Romanticising the classical gold standard**: It was associated with deflation, inequality, and limited democracy
- **Assuming it was truly automatic**: It required management, cooperation, and frequent suspension

## Key Takeaways
1. The classical gold standard was the apotheosis of monetary orthodoxy
2. It served creditor interests by enforcing price stability and constraining governments
3. The adjustment mechanism required deflationary pain in deficit countries
4. Its apparent stability was partly illusory and purchased at high human cost
5. The system collapsed in WWI and could not be successfully restored
