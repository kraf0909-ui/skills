# Chapter 3: Banks

## Core Idea
Banking is ancient, but its essence — borrowing short and lending long, creating money through credit — makes it inherently unstable. The history of banking is a history of crises, panics, and the gradual development of mechanisms (deposit insurance, lender of last resort, regulation) to contain that instability.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Maturity Transformation as Risk**: Banks borrow short (deposits, which can be withdrawn at any time) and lend long (loans, which cannot be called in). This mismatch is the source of banking's utility and its fragility.
- **Credit Creation**: Banks do not merely intermediate between savers and borrowers; they create money by making loans. A bank loan creates a deposit, expanding the money supply.
- **The Inevitability of Crises**: Because confidence is fragile and herd behaviour amplifies both optimism and pessimism, banking crises are not accidents but inherent features of the system.

## Key Concepts
- **Maturity transformation**: Borrowing short-term and lending long-term
- **Fractional reserve banking**: Keeping only a fraction of deposits as reserves, lending the rest
- **Bank run**: When depositors simultaneously demand their money, forcing illiquid banks to fail
- **Credit creation**: The process by which bank lending expands the money supply
- **Herd behaviour**: The tendency of depositors and investors to move together, amplifying booms and busts

## Mental Models
- Think of banks as creating money "out of thin air" through lending — this is not fraud but the essence of modern banking
- Use the maturity mismatch framework to understand why even solvent banks can fail: they may have good assets but cannot liquidate them quickly enough

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming banks merely intermediate**: They create money; they do not just move it around
- **Believing regulation eliminates crises**: It mitigates but cannot eliminate the inherent instability

## Key Takeaways
1. Banking is ancient but its modern form — fractional reserve, maturity transformation — is inherently fragile
2. Banks create money through lending, not just intermediate existing funds
3. Banking crises are inherent, not accidental; they recur throughout history
4. Deposit insurance and lender-of-last-resort facilities were developed to contain this instability
5. Regulation mitigates but cannot eliminate banking fragility
