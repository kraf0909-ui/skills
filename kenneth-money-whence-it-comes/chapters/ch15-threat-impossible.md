# Chapter 15: The Threat of the Impossible

## Core Idea
The New Deal was a revolution in monetary policy. Roosevelt abandoned the gold standard, devalued the dollar, confiscated private gold holdings, and embraced deficit spending. These were the "impossible" policies that orthodoxy said would destroy the currency and the economy. They did the opposite.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Abandonment of Gold**: Roosevelt took the US off gold in 1933, freeing monetary policy from its deflationary straitjacket. This was the single most important monetary decision of the depression.
- **Devaluation as Recovery Tool**: The dollar was devalued against gold (from $20.67 to $35 per ounce), raising the domestic price level and stimulating exports.
- **Gold Confiscation**: Private gold holdings were confiscated and made illegal, ensuring that monetary policy could not be undermined by private speculation.
- **The Rejection of Orthodoxy**: The New Deal represented a fundamental break with the balanced-budget, gold-standard orthodoxy that had caused the disaster.

## Key Concepts
- **Gold standard abandonment (1933)**: Executive order halting gold convertibility and prohibiting private gold holdings
- **Devaluation (1934)**: The dollar was devalued from $20.67 to $35 per ounce of gold
- **Gold Reserve Act (1934)**: Transferred title of all monetary gold to the federal government
- **Silver Purchase Act (1934)**: Required the Treasury to purchase silver, expanding the monetary base
- **Deficit spending**: The embrace of deliberate budget deficits as a recovery tool

## Mental Models
- Think of leaving gold as removing a straitjacket: it allows policy to respond to conditions rather than to a fixed external anchor
- Use the "threat of the impossible" frame when orthodoxy says a policy cannot work: it may be orthodoxy that is wrong

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming gold abandonment caused inflation**: It caused moderate price increases that reversed deflation — the desired outcome
- **Ignoring the political courage required**: Roosevelt faced fierce opposition from financial elites

## Key Takeaways
1. Roosevelt abandoned the gold standard in 1933, freeing monetary policy
2. The dollar was devalued from $20.67 to $35 per ounce, raising prices
3. Private gold holdings were confiscated to prevent monetary sabotage
4. The New Deal rejected balanced-budget orthodoxy and embraced deficit spending
5. These "impossible" policies were the foundation of recovery
