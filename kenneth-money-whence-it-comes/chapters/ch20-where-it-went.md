# Chapter 20: Where It Went

## Core Idea
The Bretton Woods system collapsed in the early 1970s as the US suspended gold convertibility and allowed the dollar to float. This ended the era of fixed exchange rates and inaugurated an age of floating currencies, financial deregulation, and recurrent crises. The trajectory of money was toward ever-greater abstraction, complexity, and instability.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The End of Bretton Woods**: Nixon's suspension of gold convertibility in 1971 (the "Nixon shock") was the formal end of the post-war monetary order. It was inevitable given the Triffin dilemma and US inflation.
- **Floating Rates as New Order**: The move to floating exchange rates was supposed to allow countries to pursue independent monetary policies. In practice, it created volatility, speculation, and new forms of instability.
- **Financialisation**: The decades after 1973 saw the growth of financial markets, financial innovation, and the increasing dominance of finance over the real economy. This was the direction in which money had been heading.

## Key Concepts
- **Nixon shock (1971)**: Suspension of dollar convertibility to gold; imposition of wage and price controls
- **Smithsonian Agreement (1971)**: Failed attempt to restore fixed rates at new parities
- **Floating exchange rates**: Currencies whose values are determined by market forces rather than fixed to gold or the dollar
- **Financialisation**: The growing size and importance of financial markets relative to the real economy
- **Stagflation**: The combination of high inflation and high unemployment that challenged Keynesian orthodoxy

## Mental Models
- Think of the end of Bretton Woods as the end of a compromise: the US could no longer sustain both domestic full employment and international dollar stability
- Use the financialisation framework to understand why crises became more frequent after 1973

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming floating rates solved the problems of fixed rates**: They created new problems — volatility, speculation, currency crises
- **Blaming the collapse on a single decision**: It was the result of structural contradictions, not just policy error

## Key Takeaways
1. Nixon suspended gold convertibility in 1971, ending Bretton Woods
2. The move to floating exchange rates created new forms of volatility
3. Stagflation challenged the Keynesian consensus
4. Financial markets grew rapidly, leading to increasing financialisation
5. The trajectory was toward greater monetary abstraction and instability
