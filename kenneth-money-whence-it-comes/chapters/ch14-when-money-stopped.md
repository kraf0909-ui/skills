# Chapter 14: When the Money Stopped

## Core Idea
The monetary mechanism of the Great Depression was a catastrophic contraction of the money supply. Bank failures destroyed deposits; the Federal Reserve refused to create new money; and the gold standard prevented monetary expansion. When the money stopped, the economy stopped.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Monetary Theory of the Depression**: Friedman and Schwartz's argument that the depression was caused by Federal Reserve failure to prevent monetary contraction. Galbraith endorses this view while placing it in a broader political context.
- **Bank Failures as Monetary Destruction**: When banks fail, deposits are destroyed. This is not a transfer but a destruction of money. The wave of bank failures in 1930–1933 destroyed a third of the money supply.
- **The Fed's Failure**: The Federal Reserve had the power to prevent the monetary contraction but chose not to use it. This was the greatest policy failure in American monetary history.

## Key Concepts
- **Monetary contraction**: The sharp decline in the money supply during 1930–1933
- **Bank failures**: Over 9,000 banks failed between 1930 and 1933
- **Deposit destruction**: When banks fail, deposits disappear; this reduces the money supply
- **Federal Reserve inaction**: The Fed failed to lend to distressed banks or expand the monetary base
- **Gold standard constraint**: Prevented the US from expanding the money supply even if the Fed had wanted to

## Mental Models
- Think of bank failures as monetary destruction, not just financial distress: they shrink the money supply and deepen the depression
- Use the monetary contraction framework to understand why the depression was so severe and so prolonged

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming the depression on speculation alone**: Speculation caused the crash; monetary contraction caused the depression
- **Assuming the Fed was powerless**: It had the tools but not the will

## Key Takeaways
1. The money supply contracted by roughly one-third between 1929 and 1933
2. Over 9,000 banks failed, destroying deposits and shrinking the money supply
3. The Federal Reserve failed to act as lender of last resort or to expand the monetary base
4. The gold standard prevented monetary expansion
5. The monetary contraction was the primary mechanism by which the crash became a depression
