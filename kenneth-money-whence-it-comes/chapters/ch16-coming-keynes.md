# Chapter 16: The Coming of J. M. Keynes

## Core Idea
Keynes's great contribution was not technical but intellectual: he freed economics from the superstition that money and markets self-correct to full employment. The General Theory (1936) provided the theoretical foundation for the New Deal's practical innovations and established fiscal policy as a legitimate tool of macroeconomic management.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Keynes as Liberator**: Keynes liberated economics from the "impeccable system" of classical orthodoxy. The classical model was logically consistent but empirically false: markets do not automatically clear at full employment.
- **Effective Demand**: The central Keynesian insight: output and employment are determined by aggregate demand, not by supply-side factors alone. If demand is insufficient, the economy can settle at a persistent unemployment equilibrium.
- **The Role of Fiscal Policy**: When private demand is inadequate, government spending can fill the gap. Deficits are not inherently evil; they are a tool for managing aggregate demand.

## Key Concepts
- **The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936)**: Keynes's magnum opus
- **Effective demand**: The level of demand actually exercised in the economy, as opposed to notional demand
- **Liquidity preference**: The desire to hold money rather than other assets, which can drive interest rates above the level consistent with full employment
- **The multiplier**: The idea that an initial increase in spending generates further rounds of income and spending
- **Deficit spending**: Deliberate government borrowing to finance expenditure and stimulate demand

## Mental Models
- Think of Keynesian economics as a theory of market failure: markets can fail to clear, and government can help
- Use the effective demand framework when analysing recessions: is the problem supply or demand?

## Anti-patterns
- **Reducing Keynes to "spend more"**: His theory was about managing aggregate demand, not indiscriminate spending
- **Assuming Keynes rejected markets**: He sought to save capitalism from its own instability, not to replace it

## Key Takeaways
1. Keynes freed economics from the belief that markets automatically self-correct
2. Effective demand, not supply, determines output and employment
3. Fiscal policy — deficit spending — is a legitimate tool for managing demand
4. The General Theory provided the theoretical foundation for New Deal practice
5. Keynesian economics became the dominant orthodoxy after WWII
