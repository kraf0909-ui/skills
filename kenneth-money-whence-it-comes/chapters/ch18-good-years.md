# Chapter 18: Good Years: The Preparation

## Core Idea
The post-war decades (1945–1970) were the "good years" of American capitalism — sustained growth, low unemployment, and monetary stability under the Bretton Woods system. Dollar supremacy gave the US unique privileges, but also created the Triffin dilemma: the US had to run deficits to supply the world with dollars, yet those deficits undermined confidence in dollar convertibility to gold.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Dollar Supremacy**: The dollar became the world's reserve currency, giving the US the ability to borrow in its own currency and to run persistent deficits without immediate crisis.
- **The Triffin Dilemma**: To supply the world with dollars (needed for trade and reserves), the US had to run balance-of-payments deficits. But those deficits eroded confidence in the dollar's gold backing. This was an inherent contradiction of the Bretton Woods system.
- **The Keynesian Consensus**: Fiscal policy, demand management, and social provision were accepted across the political spectrum. Monetary policy was subordinated to full employment.

## Key Concepts
- **Bretton Woods system**: Fixed but adjustable exchange rates with the dollar pegged to gold ($35/ounce) and other currencies pegged to the dollar
- **Reserve currency**: The dollar became the primary currency held by central banks as reserves
- **Triffin dilemma**: The conflict between supplying global liquidity (requiring US deficits) and maintaining confidence in the dollar (requiring US surpluses)
- **Keynesian consensus**: Broad political agreement on the legitimacy of fiscal policy and demand management
- **Embedded liberalism**: The post-war compromise between free markets and social protection

## Mental Models
- Think of dollar supremacy as an "exorbitant privilege" (de Gaulle's phrase): the US could borrow cheaply and run deficits, but at the cost of eventual instability
- Use the Triffin dilemma to understand why fixed exchange rate systems with a single reserve currency are inherently unstable

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming the good years were normal**: They were historically exceptional, dependent on specific institutional arrangements
- **Ignoring the Triffin dilemma**: The seeds of Bretton Woods' collapse were present from its creation

## Key Takeaways
1. The post-war decades saw sustained growth, low unemployment, and monetary stability
2. Dollar supremacy gave the US unique privileges in international finance
3. The Triffin dilemma — the conflict between supplying dollars and maintaining gold backing — was inherent
4. The Keynesian consensus supported fiscal policy and demand management
5. The "good years" were preparation for the eventual collapse of Bretton Woods
