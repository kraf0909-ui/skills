# Chapter 4: The Bank

## Core Idea
The central bank emerged as the institutional response to banking instability. The Bank of England (1694) was the prototype: a bank that could lend to other banks in crisis, manage the government's debt, and eventually control the money supply. The central bank is not a neutral technocratic institution but an expression of state power and creditor interests.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Lender of Last Resort**: Walter Bagehot's principle that the central bank should lend freely to solvent but illiquid banks in a crisis — at a penalty rate and against good collateral. This is the foundational doctrine of central banking.
- **Central Bank as Creditor Institution**: Central banks were created and shaped by the interests of creditors. Their primary mission has often been to protect the value of money for those who hold it, not to promote employment or growth.
- **The Evolution from Private to Public**: The Bank of England began as a private institution with public functions; over centuries, central banks became public institutions with private-sector relationships.

## Key Concepts
- **Bank of England (1694)**: Founded to lend to the government; became the model for central banks
- **Lender of last resort**: The central bank's role in providing liquidity to the banking system in crisis
- **Bagehot's dictum**: "Lend freely, at a penalty rate, on good collateral"
- **Bank rate**: The interest rate at which the central bank lends to commercial banks
- **Open market operations**: Buying and selling government securities to influence the money supply

## Mental Models
- Use Bagehot's dictum when designing crisis response: lend freely to prevent collapse, but at penalty rates to discourage moral hazard
- Think of central banks as balancing two missions: financial stability (protecting the system) and price stability (protecting creditors)

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming central bank independence is timeless**: It is a recent and contested development
- **Ignoring the creditor bias**: Central banks have historically prioritised price stability over employment

## Key Takeaways
1. The Bank of England (1694) was the prototype central bank, created to finance government war borrowing
2. The lender-of-last-resort function emerged gradually as a response to banking crises
3. Bagehot's dictum remains the foundational principle of crisis management
4. Central banks have historically reflected creditor interests more than debtor interests
5. The evolution from private to public institution took centuries and remains incomplete
