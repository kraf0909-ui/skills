# Chapter 2: Of Coins and Treasure

## Core Idea
Metallic money — gold and silver coins — was the dominant form for millennia, but its value was always partly conventional. Kings debased coinage when they needed revenue; Gresham's Law ensured that bad money drove out good; and the "soundness" of metallic money was as much political theatre as economic necessity.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Debasement as Fiscal Instrument**: Rulers reduced the precious metal content of coins to finance wars and spending. This was not fraud but a standard tool of public finance — the medieval equivalent of printing money.
- **Gresham's Law**: "Bad money drives out good." When two forms of money circulate at a fixed legal ratio, the overvalued form (bad money) drives the undervalued form (good money) out of circulation into hoarding or export.
- **The Myth of Intrinsic Value**: Even gold and silver coins derived much of their value from state certification and legal tender status, not just from their metal content.

## Key Concepts
- **Debasement**: Reducing the precious metal content of coins while maintaining face value
- **Seigniorage**: The profit from issuing money, whether by minting coins or printing currency
- **Gresham's Law**: Bad money drives out good when both circulate at fixed legal ratios
- **Bimetallism**: A monetary standard based on two metals (typically gold and silver) at a fixed ratio
- **Specie**: Coin made of precious metal

## Mental Models
- Use Gresham's Law whenever two forms of money with different intrinsic values circulate at the same nominal value
- Think of debasement as a tax on money holders: it transfers wealth from those holding old coins to the sovereign issuing new ones

## Anti-patterns
- **Romanticising metallic money**: Gold standards had their own crises, manipulations, and distributional consequences
- **Ignoring the role of state certification**: A gold coin without a credible issuer is just a lump of metal

## Key Takeaways
1. Metallic money dominated for millennia but was never purely about intrinsic value
2. Debasement was a standard fiscal tool, not exceptional fraud
3. Gresham's Law shaped monetary dynamics whenever multiple monies circulated
4. Bimetallism was inherently unstable because market and legal ratios diverged
5. The "soundness" of metallic money was partly political theatre
