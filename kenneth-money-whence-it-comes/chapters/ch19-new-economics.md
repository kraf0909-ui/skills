# Chapter 19: The New Economics at High Noon

## Core Idea
The 1960s were the high noon of Keynesian economics. The Kennedy-Johnson tax cut, the Great Society programmes, and the Vietnam War spending all reflected Keynesian principles. But the combination of full-employment fiscal policy with Vietnam War spending created inflationary pressures that would eventually undermine the Keynesian consensus and the Bretton Woods system.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Keynesianism at High Noon**: The 1960s represented the fullest application of Keynesian demand management. Fiscal policy was actively used to maintain full employment; monetary policy was accommodative.
- **The Guns-and-Butter Dilemma**: The Vietnam War and the Great Society were pursued simultaneously without corresponding tax increases. This was a political choice with monetary consequences.
- **The Erosion of Confidence**: As inflation rose and the US balance of payments deteriorated, confidence in the dollar's gold backing eroded. Foreign central banks began converting dollars to gold.

## Key Concepts
- **Kennedy-Johnson tax cut (1964)**: A deliberate Keynesian stimulus to reduce unemployment
- **Great Society**: Johnson's ambitious social programmes expanding government spending
- **Vietnam War spending**: Massive military expenditure without corresponding tax increases
- **Guns and butter**: The attempt to pursue both military and domestic spending simultaneously
- **Balance of payments deficit**: The US was losing gold as foreign central banks converted dollars

## Mental Models
- Think of the 1960s as a demonstration of Keynesianism's limits: it could maintain full employment but could not prevent inflation when demand was pushed beyond capacity
- Use the guns-and-butter framework to understand the political economy of inflation

## Anti-patterns
- **Blaming Keynesianism for the inflation**: The inflation was caused by Vietnam War spending without tax increases, not by Keynesian principles per se
- **Ignoring the political choices**: The inflation was a result of political decisions, not economic inevitability

## Key Takeaways
1. The 1960s were the high noon of Keynesian economics in practice
2. The Kennedy-Johnson tax cut was a deliberate demand-management stimulus
3. Vietnam War spending and the Great Society created inflationary pressures
4. The US balance of payments deteriorated as dollars flowed abroad
5. The inflation and gold outflows would eventually destroy Bretton Woods
