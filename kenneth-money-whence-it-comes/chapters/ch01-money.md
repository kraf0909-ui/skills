# Chapter 1: Money

## Core Idea
Money is not a thing of intrinsic value but a social convention — a token that functions because people believe it will be accepted in exchange. This belief is sustained by habit, by the state, and by the banking system. Understanding money requires understanding power and politics, not metallurgy or physics.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Money as Convention**: Money works because of collective belief, not because of its physical composition. Gold, paper, or digital entries all function as money when the social consensus accepts them.
- **The State Theory of Money**: The state creates money by declaring what it will accept for taxes and debts. This "chartalist" view (later developed by Modern Monetary Theory) traces back to the earliest paper monies.
- **The Two Sources of Money**: Governments create money by issuing currency; banks create money by extending credit. These twin engines have different dynamics and political economies.

## Key Concepts
- **Token money**: Money whose value as a medium of exchange exceeds its intrinsic value as a commodity
- **Fiat money**: Money by government decree, not backed by a physical commodity
- **Commodity money**: Money whose value derives from the material it is made of (gold, silver, tobacco)
- **Credit money**: Money created through bank lending; the dominant form in modern economies

## Mental Models
- Think of money as language: it works because we agree on its meaning, not because words have physical properties
- Use the convention framework when assessing whether a new form of money (cryptocurrency, CBDC) can succeed: does it create a credible social consensus?

## Anti-patterns
- **The commodity fallacy**: Believing money must be backed by something "real" like gold
- **The neutrality assumption**: Treating money as a neutral veil over real economic activity

## Key Takeaways
1. Money is a social institution, not a natural phenomenon
2. The history of money is the history of power — who gets to create it, who benefits from it
3. Governments and banks are the two sources of money creation
4. Belief and confidence are the ultimate foundations of monetary systems
5. When belief collapses, money collapses — regardless of its physical form
