# Patterns

## Pattern: Inflation as Tax of Last Resort
**When to use**: When a government faces existential crisis without conventional revenue
**How**: Issue paper money or expand the monetary base to finance expenditure; accept the inflation as the cost of survival
**Trade-offs**: Inflation erodes savings and transfers wealth from creditors to debtors; but it may be the only available finance
**Examples**: Continental currency (Ch 6); greenbacks (Ch 8); Weimar Germany (Ch 12)

## Pattern: The Self-Inflicted Wound of Orthodoxy
**When to use**: When policymakers cling to orthodox doctrine in the face of obvious crisis
**How**: Identify the orthodox prescription (balanced budgets, gold standard, liquidationism) and recognise that its rigorous application is worsening the crisis
**Trade-offs**: Abandoning orthodoxy risks credibility and political opposition; clinging to it deepens the disaster
**Examples**: Hoover's liquidationism (Ch 13); Britain's 1925 return to gold (Ch 11); Revenue Act of 1932 (Ch 13)

## Pattern: Maturity Transformation as Banking Essence
**When to use**: Analysing any banking system or financial institution
**How**: Identify the maturity mismatch between liabilities (short-term, withdrawable) and assets (long-term, illiquid); assess the fragility this creates
**Trade-offs**: Maturity transformation provides liquidity to depositors and long-term finance to borrowers; but it creates inherent fragility
**Examples**: Pre-Federal Reserve banking (Ch 3); 1929–1933 bank failures (Ch 14); shadow banking (implied in Ch 20)

## Pattern: The Political Economy of Monetary Standard
**When to use**: Evaluating any monetary standard (gold, fiat, currency board, etc.)
**How**: Ask who benefits and who loses from the standard; identify the class and distributional consequences
**Trade-offs**: Tight money standards favour creditors; loose money standards favour debtors; there is no neutral standard
**Examples**: Gold standard favoured rentiers (Ch 10); deflation impoverished farmers (Ch 9); greenbacks favoured debtors (Ch 8)

## Pattern: Central Bank as Creditor Institution
**When to use**: Assessing central bank policy and independence
**How**: Recognise that central banks historically emerged to protect creditor interests; evaluate whether current policy reflects this bias
**Trade-offs**: Protecting creditors maintains confidence and price stability; but it may sacrifice employment and growth
**Examples**: Bank of England's origins (Ch 4); Federal Reserve's deflationary bias (Ch 13); interwar central bank cooperation (Ch 11)

## Pattern: The War-Monetary Revolution
**When to use**: Understanding how wars transform monetary systems
**How**: War breaks the existing monetary order (gold convertibility, balanced budgets); emergency finance creates new instruments and institutions; the post-war order is permanently changed
**Trade-offs**: War finance enables survival but creates inflation, debt, and institutional legacies that shape the peace
**Examples**: American Revolution (Ch 6); Civil War (Ch 8); WWI (Ch 11); WWII (Ch 17)

## Pattern: Problem-Driven Innovation
**When to use**: Understanding how monetary institutions evolve
**How**: Identify the specific problem (war finance, cash management, crisis response) that drove the innovation; assess whether the solution persists because it is useful or because of path dependence
**Trade-offs**: Problem-driven solutions are pragmatic but may create institutional rigidities
**Examples**: Bank of England (Ch 4); National Banking Act (Ch 8); Federal Reserve (Ch 13); Bretton Woods (Ch 17)

## Pattern: The Folly of Restoration
**When to use**: When policymakers seek to return to a pre-crisis monetary arrangement
**How**: Assess whether the conditions that made the old arrangement viable still exist; recognise that crises often change economic structures permanently
**Trade-offs**: Restoration provides continuity and confidence; but it may impose inappropriate constraints on a changed economy
**Examples**: Britain's 1925 return to gold (Ch 11); interwar gold standard restoration (Ch 11); failed Smithsonian Agreement (Ch 20)

## Pattern: Floating Rates as Incomplete Solution
**When to use**: Evaluating exchange rate regimes
**How**: Recognise that floating rates solve the problems of fixed rates (constraint on monetary policy, speculative attacks) but create new problems (volatility, speculation, currency misalignment)
**Trade-offs**: Floating rates allow monetary independence but sacrifice exchange rate stability; fixed rates provide stability but sacrifice policy autonomy
**Examples**: End of Bretton Woods (Ch 20); interwar floating (Ch 11)

## Pattern: Financial Folly as Recurrent
**When to use**: When assessing whether current financial arrangements are stable
**How**: Recognise that euphoria, speculative excess, panic, and crisis are recurrent features of financial systems, not eliminable bugs
**Trade-offs**: Regulation can mitigate but not eliminate crises; each generation believes "this time is different"
**Examples**: South Sea Bubble (Ch 3); 1929 (Ch 13); 2008 (Ch 21)

## Pattern: The Keynesian Demand Management Cycle
**When to use**: Understanding macroeconomic policy over the business cycle
**How**: In recession, expand demand through fiscal and monetary policy; in boom, contract demand to prevent inflation; the challenge is timing and political will
**Trade-offs**: Demand management can reduce unemployment but may cause inflation; the political economy favours expansion over contraction
**Examples**: New Deal (Ch 15); WWII (Ch 17); 1960s (Ch 19); post-2008 (implied in Ch 21)
