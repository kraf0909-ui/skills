# Cheatsheet

## Decision Rules

### When to abandon the gold standard?
- **Abandon when**: Deflation is severe, unemployment is high, and the domestic economy needs monetary expansion more than external credibility
- **Maintain when**: Inflation is the primary threat, external confidence is fragile, and the political system supports austerity
- **Historical rule**: Countries that abandoned gold earliest in the 1930s recovered first

### When to use inflationary finance?
- **Use when**: The alternative is defeat in war, state collapse, or sovereign default; conventional taxation and borrowing are exhausted
- **Avoid when**: Conventional finance is available; the political system can sustain tax increases
- **Warning**: Inflationary finance is a tax on money holders; it destroys savings and may destroy social stability

### When to apply Keynesian stimulus?
- **Apply when**: Unemployment is high, private demand is inadequate, and monetary policy is constrained (liquidity trap, zero lower bound)
- **Avoid when**: The economy is at full employment; the stimulus will crowd out private investment or cause inflation
- **Keynesian rule**: "The boom, not the slump, is the right time for austerity"

### When to act as lender of last resort?
- **Lend when**: Solvent but illiquid institutions are facing runs; the alternative is contagious failure
- **Conditions**: Lend freely, at a penalty rate, on good collateral (Bagehot's dictum)
- **Do not lend when**: The institution is insolvent; the problem is structural, not liquidity

## Decision Tree: Choosing a Monetary Standard

```
Is external credibility the top priority?
  YES → Fixed rate (gold peg, currency board, dollarisation)
        → Accept loss of monetary independence
  NO → Is monetary policy flexibility needed?
          YES → Floating rate or managed float
                    → Accept exchange rate volatility
          NO → Intermediate regime (crawling peg, basket peg)
                    → Accept partial constraints on both dimensions
```

## Trade-off Matrix: Monetary Standards

| Standard | Price Stability | Crisis Response | Credibility | Distributional Bias | Best For |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|----------|
| Gold standard | High (deflationary) | Very poor | High | Pro-creditor | Creditor nations, small open economies |
| Fiat with independent central bank | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate | Neutral-banker | Advanced economies with stable institutions |
| Fiat with political control | Uncertain | Good (in theory) | Low | Pro-debtor | Crisis situations, developmental states |
| Currency board | High | Very poor | Very high | Pro-creditor | Hyperinflation exit, small economies |
| Dollarisation | High | None | Very high | Pro-creditor | Countries with no monetary credibility |

## Thresholds & Defaults

- **Hyperinflation threshold**: >50% price increase per month (Cagan's definition)
- **Weimar peak**: Prices doubled every 3–4 days in late 1923
- **US monetary contraction 1929–1933**: ~1/3 of money supply destroyed
- **Bank failures 1930–1933**: Over 9,000 US banks failed
- **Gold price devaluation 1934**: $20.67 → $35 per ounce
- **Bretton Woods gold price**: $35 per ounce (1944–1971)
- **Kennedy-Johnson tax cut**: ~$11 billion (1964)
- **Nixon shock**: August 1971, suspension of convertibility

## Tells & Smells

- **If a government is printing money to finance war**: inflation is coming; assess whether the war is winnable and the inflation tolerable
- **If policymakers advocate "liquidation" in a depression**: they are applying orthodox doctrine that will deepen the crisis
- **If a country is trying to restore a pre-crisis parity**: ask whether economic conditions support it; if not, the restoration will be painful and probably temporary
- **If a central bank is refusing to lend in a panic**: it is failing its primary function, regardless of the theoretical justification
- **If "this time is different" is being said**: it probably isn't; check historical parallels
- **If a fixed exchange rate is being defended with high interest rates**: the defence may be more costly than the devaluation it seeks to prevent
- **If monetary policy is subordinated to fiscal needs**: inflation risk is elevated; the question is whether the subordination is temporary or structural

## Quick Reference: Monetary Crises

| Crisis | Cause | Monetary Response | Outcome |
|--------|-------|-------------------|---------|
| Continental currency (1776–1781) | Revolutionary war finance | Paper money issuance | Hyperinflation; independence achieved |
| Greenbacks (1861–1879) | Civil war finance | Fiat paper, suspension of specie | Inflation; eventual resumption |
| 1873–1896 deflation | Gold standard, growth | Tight money, no response | Populist revolt; gold discoveries |
| Weimar hyperinflation (1921–1923) | Reparations, war finance | Unlimited money creation | Currency collapse; Rentenmark reform |
| Great Depression (1929–1933) | Gold standard, Fed failure | Contraction, then abandonment | Recovery after leaving gold |
| Stagflation (1970s) | Vietnam, oil shocks, excess demand | Initially contractionary | End of Bretton Woods, Volcker shock |
