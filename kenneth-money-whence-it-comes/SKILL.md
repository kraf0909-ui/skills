## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. Money as a Social Convention, Not a Commodity
Money is not gold, silver, or any physical thing with intrinsic value. It is a social convention — a token that works because people believe it will be accepted in exchange. This belief is sustained by the state, the banking system, and social habit. Understanding money requires understanding power and politics, not just metallurgy.

### 2. The Two Sources of Money Creation
Money is created in two ways: (1) governments issue paper currency (for better or worse), and (2) banks create credit through lending. These are the twin engines of the money supply, each with its own dynamics, risks, and political economy.

### 3. The Gold Standard as Political Theatre
The gold standard was never a purely economic institution. It was a political device to reassure creditors, constrain governments, and signal respectability. Its economic costs — deflationary bias, constraint on crisis response — were real but secondary to its political and symbolic functions.

### 4. Inflation as the Tax of Last Resort
Governments facing existential crises (war, revolution) turn to the printing press because it is the only tax that can be levied instantly and without legislative consent. Inflation is not a moral failing but a predictable response to fiscal desperation. It is "deeply disapproved of in conventional histories, despite the fact that it was effective and indispensable."

### 5. Banking Crises as Inevitable, Not Accidental
Banking crises are not anomalies but inherent features of a system where banks borrow short and lend long, where confidence is fragile, and where herd behaviour amplifies both booms and busts. Galbraith's view aligns with what would later be called Minskyan instability.

### 6. The Political Economy of Banking
Banking is not a technical activity separable from politics. The history of money is a history of political battles — between debtors and creditors, between agrarians and financiers, between populists and bankers — and the bankers "invariably win." Central banks are not neutral technocratic institutions but expressions of political power.

### 7. The Self-Inflicted Wound of Orthodoxy
The worst economic disasters are not caused by external shocks but by the rigid application of orthodox doctrine — the gold standard, balanced budgets, liquidationism — in the face of obvious need for flexibility. The 1929–1933 depression was a "self-inflicted wound" caused by policymakers clinging to impeccable theories that were catastrophically wrong for the circumstances.

### 8. Keynes as Liberator from Monetary Superstition
Keynes's great contribution was not technical but intellectual: he freed economics from the belief that money and markets self-correct to full employment. The "impeccable system" of classical economics was impeccable only in its logical consistency, not in its correspondence to reality.

### 9. The Bretton Woods Compromise
The post-WWII monetary order was a compromise between the American desire for dollar supremacy and the need for international stability. It worked while it lasted but contained the seeds of its own destruction, as the Triffin dilemma predicted.

### 10. Where Money Went: Financialisation and Instability
The trajectory of money in the twentieth century was toward ever-greater financial complexity, ever-larger institutions, and ever-more-frequent crises. Stability did not increase with knowledge; if anything, the opposite occurred. The disasters of 2008 would not have surprised Galbraith.
