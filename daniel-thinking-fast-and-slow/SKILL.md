## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### System 1 and System 2
The foundational dual-process framework:
- **System 1 (Fast Thinking)**: Operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. Generates impressions and feelings.
- **System 2 (Slow Thinking)**: Allocates attention to the effortful mental activities that demand it, including complex computations. Associated with the subjective experience of agency, choice, and concentration.

**Key insight**: System 1 is the hero of the book — it generates suggestions for System 2, which usually adopts them. System 2 is lazy and only intervenes when necessary. Most of our thinking is System 1, but we identify with System 2.

### WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is)
System 1's signature limitation: it constructs the best possible story from the information available, without considering what it doesn't know. This leads to overconfidence, framing effects, and base-rate neglect.

**Application**: Always ask "What information am I missing?" before making important judgments.

### Prospect Theory
Kahneman and Tversky's landmark theory of decision under risk, which replaced expected utility theory:
- **Reference dependence**: Outcomes are evaluated relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms
- **Loss aversion**: Losses loom larger than gains (approximately 2-2.5x)
- **Diminishing sensitivity**: Marginal value decreases as distance from reference point increases
- **Probability weighting**: People overweight small probabilities and underweight moderate to high probabilities

**The Fourfold Pattern**: High-probability gains → risk averse; High-probability losses → risk seeking; Low-probability gains → risk seeking (lottery tickets); Low-probability losses → risk averse (insurance).

### Heuristics and Biases
Mental shortcuts that are usually efficient but systematically biased:
- **Representativeness**: Judging probability by similarity to stereotypes (leads to base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy)
- **Availability**: Judging frequency by ease of recall (leads to media bias, personal experience distortion)
- **Anchoring**: Starting from an initial value and adjusting insufficiently
- **Affect**: Letting feelings toward something determine beliefs about it

### The Two Selves
- **Experiencing Self**: Lives in the present, experiences moment-to-moment feelings. Duration matters.
- **Remembering Self**: Constructs stories of the past, makes decisions for the future. Governed by Peak-End Rule (peak intensity + ending quality), duration neglected.

**Implication**: We choose between memories of experiences, not between experiences themselves.

### Expert Intuition: When to Trust It
Expert intuition is credible only when:
1. The environment is sufficiently regular and predictable
2. The expert has had sufficient practice to learn these regularities (typically >10,000 hours)
3. Feedback is rapid and unambiguous

**Don't trust expert intuition for**: Political forecasting, stock picking, long-term economic predictions, complex social phenomena.
